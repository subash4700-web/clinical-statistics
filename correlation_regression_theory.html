1. What is correlation?

Correlation measures the strength and direction of the linear relationship between two continuous variables. It answers: when one variable goes up, does the other tend to go up too (positive), go down (negative), or show no consistent pattern (near zero)?

Correlation does not tell you which variable causes which, and it does not tell you how much one variable changes when the other changes. It only measures how tightly the two move together.

Classic mistake: Correlation is not causation. Two variables can be strongly correlated because they are both driven by a third factor you have not measured. Always think about the biology before interpreting a correlation.

Examples in the laboratory:

  • HbA1c and fasting glucose — positively correlated (both reflect long-term glycaemia)
  • eGFR and creatinine — negatively correlated (as kidneys fail, creatinine rises and eGFR falls)
  • Patient age and sodium — weak or no correlation in a healthy population

2. Pearson r — calculation and interpretation

The most common correlation coefficient is the Pearson product-moment correlation coefficient (r). It is calculated as the covariance of the two variables divided by the product of their standard deviations:

Pearson r r = Σ[(xᵢ − x̄)(yᵢ − ȳ)] / √[Σ(xᵢ − x̄)² × Σ(yᵢ − ȳ)²]

The result is always between −1 and +1:

−1.0Perfect negative
−0.7Strong negative
0No relationship
+0.7Strong positive
+1.0Perfect positive

The quadrant intuition — how correlation works

The numerator of Pearson r is Σ(xᵢ − x̄)(yᵢ − ȳ). To understand what this means, draw the mean lines x̄ (vertical) and ȳ (horizontal) on a scatter plot. This creates four quadrants:

QuadrantPosition(xᵢ − x̄)(yᵢ − ȳ)ProductContributes to
1 (top-left)x below mean, y above meannegativepositivenegativeNegative correlation
2 (top-right)x above mean, y above meanpositivepositivepositivePositive correlation
3 (bottom-left)x below mean, y below meannegativenegativepositivePositive correlation

The key insight: If points cluster in quadrants 2 and 3 (both high or both low) → the sum of products is positive → positive correlation. If points cluster in quadrants 1 and 4 (one high, one low) → the sum is negative → negative correlation. If points are spread evenly across all four quadrants → the positives and negatives cancel → r ≈ 0.

Negative correlation example:

If we measured exercise hours (x) vs resting heart rate (y), more exercise → lower heart rate. Points would cluster in quadrants 1 and 4: high x with low y, and low x with high y. The products would be mostly negative → r < 0.
Important: A statistically significant p-value does not mean the correlation is clinically meaningful. With n = 500, even r = 0.09 will be statistically significant, but it explains less than 1% of the variance. Always report r alongside the p-value and sample size.

Significance test: Pearson r is tested with a t-statistic (df = n − 2):

t-statistic for r t = r × √(n − 2) / √(1 − r²)

3. r vs R² — what is the difference?

r is the correlation coefficient. R² (r squared, also called the coefficient of determination) is r multiplied by itself. They answer different questions:

StatisticWhat it tells youClinical example
r = 0.80Strong positive linear relationshipHbA1c and fasting glucose in diabetic patients
R² = 0.6464% of the variance in Y is explained by XFasting glucose alone explains 64% of the variation in HbA1c — other factors (diet, red cell lifespan) account for the rest
r = 0.50Moderate relationshipBMI and total cholesterol — related, but many other factors involved
R² = 0.25Only 25% of variance explainedBMI alone is a weak predictor of cholesterol — do not use it as the sole predictor

How R² is calculated — SST, SSE, and the key formula

R² compares two models: (1) just using the mean to predict everything, vs (2) using the regression line. How much better does the line do?

SSE = 146.5
Variation around the regression line

R² = (346.9 − 146.5) / 346.9 = 0.578 — the regression line reduces the variation by 57.8%

The key insight: R² compares the variation around the mean (SST — a flat horizontal line) to the variation around the regression line (SSE — the best-fit line). If the regression line reduces the spread a lot → R² is high. If it barely improves over the mean → R² is low.

When SSE increases (points spread further from the line), R² falls. When SSE decreases (points cluster tightly around the line), R² rises.
Rule of thumb: Always report R² when you are talking about prediction (regression). Report r when you are talking about the association between two variables. They are mathematically related but conceptually different.

4. Spearman ρ — when to use a rank correlation

Spearman's rank correlation (ρ, rho) is a non-parametric alternative to Pearson r. Instead of working with the raw values, it ranks them from smallest to largest and then calculates Pearson r on the ranks.

Use Spearman ρ when:

  • One or both variables are clearly non-normally distributed (e.g. highly skewed analytes)
  • You have ordinal data (e.g. severity scores: mild / moderate / severe)
  • There are prominent outliers that you cannot remove but that should not dominate the result
  • The relationship may be monotonic but not strictly linear
In practice: For comparing two laboratory methods on the same patients, Pearson r is usually appropriate if values are roughly normally distributed. Spearman ρ is safer when the analyte spans a very wide range or has a skewed distribution (e.g. triglycerides, ferritin, troponin).

5. Linear regression — prediction, not just association

While correlation tells you how strongly two variables are related, regression goes further: it models the exact mathematical relationship so you can predict Y from X.

The simple linear regression model is:

Linear regression equation ŷ = a + b × x
where b (slope) = r × (sY / sX)
a (intercept) = ȳ − b × x̄

Interpreting the output:

  • Slope (b): For every 1-unit increase in X, Y is predicted to change by b units. A slope of 1.0 means the methods agree perfectly on scale.
  • Intercept (a): The predicted value of Y when X = 0. A non-zero intercept means there is a constant offset between the methods.
  • R²: The proportion of variance in Y explained by X.
  • Residual standard error (Sy.x): Average size of the prediction errors.
In method comparison: If method B perfectly agrees with method A, the regression line through the data should have a slope of 1.0 and an intercept of 0. Deviations from these values reveal proportional bias (slope ≠ 1) or constant bias (intercept ≠ 0).

Common mistake: Correlation is symmetric — r(X,Y) = r(Y,X). Regression is not — regressing X on Y gives a different line than regressing Y on X. In method comparison, which method you call X and which you call Y matters.

7. Residuals and model fit

A residual is the difference between the observed value and the value predicted by the regression line:

Residual eᵢ = yᵢ − ŷᵢ

Residuals are the "left-over" variation that the model could not explain. By examining them, you can check whether the model is appropriate:

  • Random scatter around zero — model is appropriate
  • Curved pattern — the relationship is not linear; consider a transformation
  • Fan shape (increasing spread) — heteroscedasticity; variance is not constant
  • One extreme residual — potential outlier; investigate before removing

The residual is the vertical distance from each point to the regression line. Regression finds the line that minimises the sum of squared residuals: Σ(yᵢ − ŷᵢ)² → minimum. This is why it's called least squares regression.
Always plot residuals. A residual plot reveals problems that R² alone hides. Two datasets with the same R² can have very different residual patterns — as Anscombe's Quartet demonstrates below.

9. Clinical context — method comparison regression

In clinical laboratory science, regression is most commonly used to compare two measurement methods run on the same patient samples. The goal is not prediction in the statistical sense — it is to quantify any systematic differences between the methods.

Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) — standard linear regression
Minimises residuals in Y only. Assumes X (reference method) is measured without error. Appropriate when the reference standard has negligible imprecision compared with the test method. Results are biased if both methods have significant imprecision.
Deming Regression
Accounts for measurement error in both X and Y. More appropriate than OLS when both methods have imprecision that contributes to the scatter. Requires an estimate of the ratio of the error variances (λ = σ²X / σ²Y).
Passing–Bablok Regression
Non-parametric. No normality assumption. Robust to outliers. The preferred method in most clinical laboratory method comparison studies (CLSI EP09). It calculates the median of all possible slopes from pairs of points.

Interpreting method comparison regression

  • Slope = 1.0, Intercept = 0: No systematic difference — methods agree perfectly on average
  • Slope ≠ 1.0: Proportional bias — the difference between methods grows as concentration increases
  • Intercept ≠ 0: Constant bias — a fixed offset exists across the entire range
Bland-Altman plot: Regression alone is not enough. The Bland-Altman difference plot (mean of methods vs difference between methods) directly visualises agreement and identifies whether bias is constant or proportional. Use both tools together.
