Hypothesis Visualisation In report ▼
Red curve — sampling distribution of Δ̄ (width = SE). The blue shading and CI bar show the 95% CI of Δ̄: where the true mean difference likely lies.
Gray curve — distribution of individual pairwise differences (width = sₚ, pooled SD). The gray shading and bar show the 95% reference range: natural spread of individual differences. The gray curve and reference range are included to illustrate the difference between individual variability and uncertainty about the mean — they are not part of the test.
What are we testing?
Difference of means (Δ̄ = μ₁ − μ₂)

Accuracy & limitations: The red curve is the theoretical sampling distribution of the mean difference Δ̄, requiring approximately normal group data or adequate n (CLT). The gray reference curve uses pooled SD, which assumes equal group variances — if Levene's test flags unequal variances, Welch's correction is applied to the test statistic but the visualization still uses pooled SD. With small, non-normal samples, prefer the Mann-Whitney U test.
How it works click to expand
95% Confidence Interval of Δ̄
CI = Δ̄ ± tdf, α/2 × SE   where   SE = sₚ √(1/n₁ + 1/n₂)
This interval answers: "Where does the true difference of means likely lie?" It narrows as n₁ + n₂ increases. This is the quantity tested by the independent t-test.
95% Reference Range (for illustration only — not part of the test)
Reference range = Δ̄ ± tdf, α/2 × sₚ
This interval answers: "Where would individual pairwise differences typically fall?" It does not narrow with larger n — individual variability is fixed. It is shown here solely to contrast with the CI and make the distinction visual.
Key insight: The CI uses SE = sₚ √(1/n₁+1/n₂), while the reference range uses sₚ directly. With n₁ = n₂ = 30, SE is ~√(n/2) ≈ 3.9× smaller than sₚ — which is why the red curve is much narrower.
