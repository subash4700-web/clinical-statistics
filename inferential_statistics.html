1. What is inferential statistics?

When we measure something in a lab or a clinical study, we never have access to the entire population — we work with a sample. Inferential statistics gives us a principled way to draw conclusions about a population based on that sample, and to quantify how much uncertainty comes with those conclusions.

The core question is always: Could the pattern we see in our sample have occurred by chance alone, or does it reflect something real in the population?

2. The Central Limit Theorem

The Central Limit Theorem (CLT) states that when you take repeated random samples from any population — regardless of the shape of that population's distribution — the distribution of sample means will approach a normal (Gaussian) distribution as sample size increases. This holds even if individual measurements are skewed, bimodal, or otherwise non-normal.

A common rule of thumb: the sampling distribution of the mean is approximately normal when n ≥ 30, though this threshold is lower for distributions that are already roughly symmetric, and higher for heavily skewed ones.

Why CLT matters in the lab: Many biological measurements are not normally distributed in individuals — serum ferritin is strongly right-skewed, troponin values cluster near zero with a long tail, and platelet counts are roughly log-normal. Yet when we compare group means (e.g. mean HbA1c in two patient groups), CLT justifies treating the distribution of those means as normal, making parametric tests valid even with non-normal raw data — as long as n is adequate.

This is the mathematical foundation that makes parametric tests (t-tests, ANOVA) robust in practice. Non-parametric tests, by contrast, work with ranks rather than means and do not rely on CLT.

TestRelies on CLT?Why
One-sample t-test Yes Compares a sample mean to a known value; assumes the sampling distribution of the mean is normal
Independent t-test Yes Compares means of two groups; CLT justifies normality of the difference between means
Paired t-test Yes Compares the mean of within-pair differences to zero; CLT applies to the distribution of those differences
One-way ANOVA Yes Extends t-test to ≥3 groups; assumes normally distributed group means and equal variances
Repeated-measures ANOVA Yes Within-subject means assumed to be normally distributed; relies on CLT for validity
Mann-Whitney U No Non-parametric; compares rank distributions, not means — no normality assumption required
Wilcoxon signed-rank No Non-parametric alternative to the paired t-test; uses ranks of differences, not their mean
Chi-Square / Fisher's Exact No Work with counts and proportions in contingency tables — no assumption about continuous distributions
McNemar / Kappa No Based on frequencies of agreement/disagreement in paired categorical data — CLT not involved
When CLT breaks down: With very small samples (n < 10–15) from heavily skewed distributions, the sampling distribution of the mean may not be adequately normal. In these cases, prefer non-parametric alternatives or consider transforming the data (e.g. log-transform for skewed lab values).
Practical takeaway: For most clinical laboratory comparisons with n ≥ 30, CLT makes parametric tests valid regardless of how individual patient values are distributed. Below that threshold, assess the raw data distribution first — or default to a non-parametric test.

3. Hypothesis testing

Every hypothesis test starts with two competing statements:

Null hypothesis (H₀): There is no effect, no difference, or no association. Any observed difference is due to random sampling variation.

Alternative hypothesis (H₁): There is a real effect or difference in the population.

H₀ and H₁ together must cover all possibilities — they are mutually exclusive and exhaustive. H₀ is always the "nothing is happening" position; H₁ is what you suspect might be true.

We never "prove" H₀ or H₁. Instead, we ask: assuming H₀ is true, how likely is it to observe data at least as extreme as what we got? That probability is the p-value.

One-tailed vs. two-tailed tests

The alternative hypothesis can be two-tailed (effect in either direction) or one-tailed (effect in one specific direction). This choice must be made before looking at the data.

Test typeH₀H₁Rejection region
Two-tailedμ = μ₀μ ≠ μ₀Both tails (high or low)
One-tailed (upper)μ ≤ μ₀μ > μ₀Upper tail only
One-tailed (lower)μ ≥ μ₀μ < μ₀Lower tail only

Understanding the tails

The "tail" refers to the extreme end(s) of the distribution where we look for evidence against H₀. The rejection region — where we reject H₀ if our test statistic falls there — can be on one side or both.

TailRejection regionWhat you're looking forp-value

Left-tailed (lower) Far left only Value is less than expected p = P(t ≤ tobs) — area in left tail only

The relationship between one-tailed and two-tailed p-values:
For symmetric distributions (t, z): pone-tailed = ptwo-tailed / 2

If a two-tailed test gives p = 0.06 (not significant at α = 0.05), a one-tailed test in the correct direction would give p = 0.03 (significant). This is why one-tailed tests are more powerful — but they must be justified before seeing the data, not chosen because the two-tailed test "almost" worked.
Example — QC bias check:
Two-tailed: "Does the analyser differ from target?" → H₁: μ ≠ 5.0 (bias in either direction is a problem)
One-tailed: "Is the new treatment better?" → H₁: μnew > μold (only interested in improvement)

Key differences:

A one-sided test is more powerful in the hypothesised direction (the p-value is halved), but it completely ignores differences in the other direction. If the effect goes the opposite way, you cannot reject H₀ regardless of how large it is.

In clinical laboratory work: almost always use two-tailed tests. Both directions matter — an analyser that reads too high is just as problematic as one that reads too low. One-tailed tests are only justified when you have a strong scientific reason to expect a specific direction, and a difference in the other direction is meaningless.

Multiple hypothesis tests

Some analyses involve more than one hypothesis test:

Sequential testing: ANOVA first tests whether any group differs (omnibus test). If significant, post-hoc tests (Tukey, Bonferroni) then test which specific pairs differ. Each step has its own H₀ and H₁.

Multi-aspect analysis: Method comparison typically runs several tests on the same dataset — Passing-Bablok (slope = 1? intercept = 0?), paired t-test (mean bias = 0?), and Bland-Altman (limits of agreement). Each answers a different question about agreement.

Multiple testing problem: Running many tests on the same data inflates the chance of a false positive. If you run 20 tests at α = 0.05, you expect 1 false positive by chance alone. Corrections like Bonferroni (divide α by the number of tests) or Tukey HSD (built into ANOVA post-hoc) control this.

4. The p-value

The p-value is the probability of obtaining a result at least as extreme as the observed one, assuming the null hypothesis is true. It is not the probability that H₀ is true, and it does not measure the size or importance of an effect.

Correct interpretation: A p-value of 0.03 means: if H₀ were true, there would be a 3% chance of seeing a difference this large (or larger) purely by chance.

A useful way to think about it: if the null hypothesis is true and you repeated the same experiment 100 times, you would expect to see a result as extreme as yours — or more extreme — in about p × 100 of those repetitions, purely by chance.

So p = 0.03 means roughly 3 out of 100 repetitions would produce a result this extreme by chance alone. That is rare enough to make you doubt H₀ — but it does not prove H₀ is false, and it says nothing about how large or clinically important the effect is.

Common misconceptions:
✗ "p = 0.03 means there is a 3% chance H₀ is true." — Wrong. The p-value assumes H₀ is true — it cannot tell you the probability that it is.
✗ "p = 0.03 means there is a 97% chance the result is real." — Wrong.
✗ "A smaller p-value means a bigger effect." — Wrong. A tiny p-value can come from a trivially small effect in a very large sample.
✗ "p ≥ 0.05 means there is no effect." — Wrong. It means the data are insufficient to reject H₀ — absence of evidence is not evidence of absence.

In practice, the p-value is treated as dichotomous: either p < α (reject H₀, "significant") or p ≥ α (fail to reject H₀, "not significant"). A p = 0.049 and p = 0.051 represent essentially the same evidence, yet are treated completely differently by this threshold. This is why effect size and confidence intervals matter more than the p-value alone.

5. Significance level (α)

The significance level α is the threshold we set before the experiment. It defines how willing we are to make a Type I error — falsely rejecting a true null hypothesis (a false positive).

The most commonly used threshold is α = 0.05, meaning we accept a 5% risk of a false positive. This is a convention, not a law — in some contexts (e.g. genomics, safety-critical decisions) much lower α values are used.

Key point: α is set before data collection. Changing it after seeing the results is a form of p-hacking and invalidates the test.

6. Type I and Type II errors

Every decision in hypothesis testing carries two possible error types:

H₀ is true (no real effect)H₀ is false (real effect exists)
Reject H₀ (p < α)Type I error — false positive (α)Correct decision — Power (1 − β)
Fail to reject H₀ (p ≥ α)Correct decision (1 − α)Type II error — false negative (β)
Type I error (α): You conclude there is an effect when there isn't one. Setting α = 0.05 means you accept a 5% risk of this.

Type II error (β): You fail to detect a real effect. Conventionally β = 0.20, meaning a 20% chance of missing a true effect.

There is an inherent trade-off: lowering α reduces false positives but increases false negatives, and vice versa. The only way to reduce both simultaneously is to increase sample size.

7. Statistical power

Power is the probability of correctly detecting a true effect — rejecting H₀ when H₁ is actually true. Power = 1 − β. A study with 80% power has a 20% chance of missing a real effect.

Power is determined by three factors:

FactorEffect on power
Sample size (n)Larger n → higher power. More data makes it easier to detect a true difference.
Effect size (Δ)Larger true difference → higher power. A large signal is easier to detect.
Significance level (α)Higher α → higher power, but at the cost of more false positives.
Try it: Open the Dance of the p-values simulator. Increase n while keeping the effect fixed — watch power rise and significant results become more frequent.
Underpowered studies: A non-significant result in an underpowered study does not mean there is no effect. It may simply mean the study was too small to detect it.

8. Confidence intervals

A 95% confidence interval (CI) is a range calculated from sample data that, in repeated sampling, would contain the true population parameter 95% of the time. It does not mean there is a 95% probability that the true value lies within any single interval.

Correct interpretation: If you repeated the same experiment 100 times and computed a 95% CI each time, approximately 95 of those intervals would contain the true population value. Any single CI either contains the true value or it doesn't — we just don't know which.
Common misconception: "There is a 95% probability that the true value lies within this specific interval." — The true value is fixed; it is the interval that varies between experiments.

The width of the CI reflects precision: a narrow CI means more precise estimation. Increasing sample size narrows the CI.

CI formula — general structure

All confidence intervals follow the same pattern:

95% CI = estimate ± critical value × standard error

The specific formula depends on what you are estimating:

CI forFormulaNull valueSignificant if CI excludesUsed in

Mean difference (paired)d̄ ± t × (sd/√n)0Zero (= no bias)Paired t-test
Difference of means(x̄₁−x̄₂) ± t × SE0Zero (= no difference)Independent t-test
Proportionp̂ ± z × √(p̂(1−p̂)/n)p₀The hypothesised proportionProportion test

Pattern: The "null value" is always the value that means "no effect." For differences it's 0. For ratios (OR, RR) it's 1. For slopes it depends on context — 0 (no relationship) or 1 (identity, no proportional bias). If the CI excludes the null value → statistically significant.
Worked example — "Does the CI include zero?"

Paired t-test comparing two analysers: mean difference d̄ = +1.2 mmol/L.

95% CI = [0.42, 1.98] → does NOT include 0 → significant bias. The true difference is somewhere between +0.42 and +1.98 — it's definitely not zero.

95% CI = [−0.35, 2.75] → DOES include 0 → not significant. The true difference could be zero — we can't rule out "no bias" with this data.
Worked example — "Does the CI include the hypothesised value?"

One-sample t-test: QC target μ₀ = 5.0 mmol/L. Measured mean x̄ = 5.18.

95% CI = [5.07, 5.29] → does NOT include 5.0 → reject H₀. The instrument is biased — the true mean is above the target.

95% CI = [4.92, 5.44] → DOES include 5.0 → fail to reject H₀. The target value is within the plausible range.
CI vs p-value — the CI tells you more:
The p-value only says "significant or not." The CI tells you the range of plausible values for the true effect. A CI of [0.1, 15.2] is significant but imprecise (wide). A CI of [1.8, 2.4] is significant AND precise (narrow). Both have p < 0.05, but the second is far more useful clinically.

9. Statistical vs. clinical significance

A statistically significant result (p < α) tells you only that the observed difference is unlikely to be due to chance. It says nothing about whether that difference is clinically meaningful.

With a large enough sample size, almost any difference — no matter how trivially small — will eventually become statistically significant. This is why effect size matters.

MeasureWhat it tells you
p-valueWhether the result is likely due to chance
Effect size (e.g. Cohen's δ)How large the difference is in standardised units, independent of sample size
Clinical significanceWhether the difference matters in practice — a clinical judgement, not a statistical one
Cohen's δ guidelines (approximate):
0.2 = small  ·  0.5 = medium  ·  0.8 = large

A δ of 0.05 with p < 0.001 may be statistically robust but clinically irrelevant. A δ of 0.8 with p = 0.08 may be clinically important but the study was underpowered to detect it.
Always report effect size alongside the p-value. Statistical significance without effect size is incomplete reporting.

10. Two ways of thinking about probability

The p-value interpretation above is a frequentist one — and for p-values, the frequentist interpretation is the correct one. Understanding the two schools of thought explains why so many people misread p-values.

Frequentist ✓Bayesian
Probability meansLong-run frequency over many repeated experimentsDegree of belief, updated as evidence accumulates
What p-value tells youP(data this extreme or more | H₀ is true) — correctDoes not use p-values
Can say "probability H₀ is true"?No — H₀ is either true or not; probability doesn't applyYes — expressed as a posterior probability
Requires prior knowledge?NoYes — requires a prior distribution
The frequentist interpretation is correct for p-values because p-values are a frequentist tool — they were designed within that framework. The question most researchers want to ask is actually Bayesian: "given my data, what is the probability that H₀ is true?" But a p-value cannot answer that. It answers the reverse: "given H₀ is true, how probable is this data?" Trying to give a p-value a Bayesian reading is not a matter of preference — it is simply wrong.

The two most influential statisticians in this area — Ronald Fisher and Jerzy Neyman/Egon Pearson — both worked within the frequentist framework. They disagreed on the details of how to use p-values: Fisher saw the p-value as a continuous measure of evidence against H₀, while Neyman and Pearson wanted a strict yes/no decision rule based on a pre-set threshold. But neither was asking "what is the probability that H₀ is true?" — that question belongs to the Bayesian world.

Bayesian statistics is a completely separate approach that answers different questions using different tools. Instead of p-values, it produces something called a posterior probability — a direct statement about how likely something is given the data. It is not better or worse than frequentist statistics; it is built for a different purpose and requires prior knowledge to be specified in advance.

11. Parametric vs. non-parametric tests

Statistical tests for quantitative data fall into two families: parametric and non-parametric. The choice depends on your data's characteristics.

Assumes normalityYes — data (or differences) should be approximately normal, or n ≥ 30No — works with any distribution shape
Data typeContinuous (interval / ratio)Ordinal or continuous
Sensitive toOutliers can distort the meanRobust against outliers (works on ranks)

ExamplePaired t-test: mean difference = 0?Wilcoxon: median difference = 0?

When to use which:

ScenarioParametricNon-parametric

Rule of thumb: If your data is continuous, reasonably symmetric, and n ≥ 20–30 — use parametric (more power). If your data is ordinal, heavily skewed, has outliers, or n is small — use non-parametric (more robust). When in doubt, run both — if they agree, report the parametric result.
Common misconception: "My data isn't normally distributed, so I must use a non-parametric test." This is often too cautious. The CLT means that for moderate sample sizes (n ≥ 30), the sampling distribution of the mean is approximately normal even if the raw data isn't. The normality assumption applies to the test statistic, not the raw data. For the paired t-test, it's the differences that need to be approximately normal — not the original measurements.

12. One-sample t-test

What is tested: Whether the mean of a single sample differs from a known or hypothesised value. For example: does the mean glucose concentration of our quality control samples differ from the target value of 5.0 mmol/L?

Assumptions:

AssumptionDetails
Data typeContinuous (interval or ratio scale)
IndependenceObservations are independent of each other
DistributionData should be approximately normally distributed — or n ≥ 30 (CLT applies)
Non-parametric alternativeIf data is skewed, has outliers, is ordinal, or n is small with non-normal distribution → use the Wilcoxon signed-rank test instead

Hypotheses:

H₀: μ = μ₀ (the population mean equals the hypothesised value)
H₁: μ ≠ μ₀ (two-sided) or μ > μ₀ / μ < μ₀ (one-sided)

Example: A QC sample with target 5.0 mmol/L was measured 20 times. Mean = 5.18, SD = 0.24.
H₀: μ = 5.0 — H₁: μ ≠ 5.0 — Is the instrument biased?

Test statistic:

t = (x̄ − μ₀) / (s / √n)     df = n − 1

SymbolMeaning
x̄Sample mean — the average of your measurements
μ₀The hypothesised population mean (the value you're testing against)
sSample standard deviation
√nSquare root of sample size
s / √nStandard error of the mean — how precisely x̄ estimates μ
dfDegrees of freedom = n − 1

The t-value measures how many standard errors the sample mean is from the hypothesised value. Compare |t| to the critical value from the t-distribution, or use the p-value directly.

Confidence interval interpretation:

The 95% CI for the mean is calculated as x̄ ± tcrit × (s / √n).
If the CI does not include the hypothesised value μ₀ → reject H₀ (significant at α = 0.05).
If the CI includes μ₀ → fail to reject H₀.
Worked example — QC glucose target:

A QC sample with target value μ₀ = 5.0 mmol/L is measured 20 times.
Results: x̄ = 5.18, s = 0.24, n = 20.

Step 1: Calculate t
t = (x̄ − μ₀) / (s / √n) = (5.18 − 5.0) / (0.24 / √20) = 0.18 / 0.054 = 3.35

Step 2: Find critical value
df = 20 − 1 = 19. tcrit = 2.093 at α = 0.05 (two-tailed).

Step 3: Decision
|3.35| > 2.093 → Reject H₀ (p = 0.003).

Step 4: Confidence interval
95% CI = 5.18 ± 2.093 × 0.054 = [5.07, 5.29]
The CI does not include 5.0 → confirms the instrument reads significantly higher than target. Mean bias = +0.18 mmol/L.

13. Independent t-test

What is tested: Whether the means of two independent groups differ. For example: is there a difference in serum creatinine between male and female patients?

Assumptions:

AssumptionDetails
Data typeContinuous (interval or ratio)
IndependenceThe two groups are independent (different subjects)
DistributionBoth groups approximately normal — or n ≥ 30 per group
Equal varianceStandard (Student's) t-test assumes equal variance. Use Welch's t-test when variances differ (recommended as default)
Non-parametric alternativeIf data is skewed, has outliers, is ordinal, or sample sizes are small with non-normal distribution → use Mann-Whitney U test instead

Hypotheses:

H₀: μ₁ = μ₂ (no difference between group means)
H₁: μ₁ ≠ μ₂

Example: Mean creatinine in males (n=45) = 88 μmol/L vs. females (n=52) = 72 μmol/L.
H₀: No difference — H₁: There is a difference.

Test statistic:

t = (x̄₁ − x̄₂) / √(s₁²/n₁ + s₂²/n₂)     (Welch's)

SymbolMeaning
x̄₁, x̄₂Sample means of group 1 and group 2
s₁², s₂²Sample variances of group 1 and group 2 — the square of each group's standard deviation (s² = SD²). Measures the spread within each group
n₁, n₂Sample sizes of group 1 and group 2

The pooled version (Student's) uses a common variance estimate: s²p = [(n₁−1)s₁² + (n₂−1)s₂²] / (n₁+n₂−2). Welch's version is preferred because it does not assume equal variances.

Confidence interval interpretation:

The 95% CI is for the difference between means (μ₁ − μ₂).
If the CI does not include 0 → the means are significantly different.
If the CI includes 0 → no significant difference at α = 0.05.
Worked example — Creatinine: males vs females:

Males (n₁ = 45): x̄₁ = 88 μmol/L, s₁ = 15.
Females (n₂ = 52): x̄₂ = 72 μmol/L, s₂ = 12.

Step 1: Calculate t (Welch's)
t = (88 − 72) / √(15²/45 + 12²/52) = 16 / √(5.0 + 2.77) = 16 / 2.79 = 5.73

Step 2: Degrees of freedom (Welch-Satterthwaite)
df ≈ 80 (calculated from the variance ratio formula)

Step 3: Decision
tcrit ≈ 1.99 at α = 0.05 (two-tailed). |5.73| > 1.99 → Reject H₀ (p < 0.001).

Step 4: Confidence interval & effect size
95% CI for the difference = 16 ± 1.99 × 2.79 = [10.4, 21.6] μmol/L — does not include 0.
Cohen's d = 16 / √((15² + 12²)/2) = 16 / 13.6 = 1.18 (large effect).

Males have significantly higher creatinine than females — a well-known physiological difference due to higher muscle mass.

14. Paired t-test

What is tested: Whether the mean difference between paired observations is zero. Pairs can be: before/after treatment on the same patient, two methods measuring the same sample, or matched subjects.

Assumptions:

AssumptionDetails
Data typeContinuous (interval or ratio)
Paired dataEach observation in group 1 has a natural pair in group 2
DistributionThe differences (d = x₁ − x₂) should be approximately normal — or n ≥ 30
Non-parametric alternativeIf differences are skewed, have outliers, or data is ordinal → use Wilcoxon signed-rank test instead (non-parametric for paired data)

Hypotheses:

H₀: μd = 0 (the mean difference between pairs is zero — no systematic bias)
H₁: μd ≠ 0 (there is a systematic difference)

Example: 30 patient samples measured on two analysers. Mean difference d̄ = +1.2 mmol/L, SDd = 2.1.
H₀: No systematic difference — H₁: One analyser reads systematically higher.

Test statistic:

t = d̄ / (sd / √n)     df = n − 1

SymbolMeaning
d̄Mean of the paired differences (di = x1i − x2i)
sdStandard deviation of the paired differences — how much the individual differences vary around d̄
√nSquare root of the number of pairs
sd / √nStandard error of the mean difference
dfDegrees of freedom = n − 1 (number of pairs minus 1)

Confidence interval interpretation:

The 95% CI is for the mean difference d̄.
If the CI does not include 0 → there is a systematic bias between the two conditions.
If the CI includes 0 → no evidence of systematic difference.
Worked example — Two analysers measuring sodium:

30 patient samples measured on both Analyser A (new) and Analyser B (reference). Same samples → paired.

Results: d̄ = +1.2 mmol/L, sd = 2.1, n = 30.

Step 1: Calculate t
t = d̄ / (sd / √n) = 1.2 / (2.1 / √30) = 1.2 / 0.383 = 3.13

Step 2: Decision
df = 30 − 1 = 29. tcrit = 2.045 at α = 0.05 (two-tailed).
|3.13| > 2.045 → Reject H₀ (p = 0.004).

Step 3: Confidence interval
95% CI = 1.2 ± 2.045 × 0.383 = [0.42, 1.98] mmol/L — does not include 0.

Conclusion: Analyser A reads systematically +1.2 mmol/L higher than Analyser B. The CI tells us the true bias is somewhere between +0.42 and +1.98 mmol/L. Whether this matters clinically depends on the sodium reference interval (136–145 mmol/L) — a 1.2 mmol/L bias may or may not be acceptable.
Common pitfall: The paired t-test detects constant (additive) bias — it tests whether the average difference is zero. It does not detect proportional bias (where the difference grows with concentration). For that, use Bland-Altman analysis or Passing-Bablok regression.

15. ANOVA (Analysis of Variance)

What is tested: Whether the means of three or more independent groups differ. ANOVA is an extension of the two-sample t-test to more than two groups. Two-way ANOVA additionally tests for a second factor and the interaction between factors.

Assumptions:

AssumptionDetails
Data typeContinuous (interval or ratio)
IndependenceObservations between and within groups are independent
DistributionData within each group approximately normal (robust when n ≥ 20 per group)
Homogeneity of varianceVariances should be similar across groups (Levene's test). ANOVA is moderately robust to this when group sizes are equal
Non-parametric alternativeIf data is skewed, has outliers, or groups are small with non-normal distributions → use Kruskal-Wallis test instead (non-parametric for 3+ groups)

Hypotheses (one-way):

H₀: μ₁ = μ₂ = μ₃ = … = μk (all group means are equal)
H₁: At least one group mean differs

Example: Comparing potassium levels from 3 different analysers. ANOVA tests if any analyser gives a systematically different result.

Test statistic:

F = MSbetween / MSwithin     df₁ = k − 1,   df₂ = N − k

SymbolMeaning

MSbetweenMean Square Between — the average variation between group means. Large when groups have different means
MSwithinMean Square Within — the average variation within each group (random noise). Used as the baseline for comparison

NTotal number of observations across all groups

df₂ = N − kDegrees of freedom for the denominator (within groups)

A large F means groups differ more than expected by chance. If F exceeds the critical value → reject H₀.

The intuition behind F: Imagine three groups, each as a bell curve. The grand mean (dashed line) sits in the middle. F asks: are the group means (x̄₁, x̄₂, x̄₃) far apart relative to how spread out the values are within each group?

Large F → group means are far apart, within-group spread is small → groups are genuinely different.
Small F → group means are close together, within-group spread is large → differences could be noise.

The pink arrows (between-group variation) compared to the black arrows (within-group variation) — that ratio is F.

16. Mann-Whitney U test

What is tested: Whether two independent groups differ in their distribution. It is the non-parametric alternative to the independent t-test. Instead of comparing means, it compares rank sums — making it suitable for ordinal data or continuous data that is not normally distributed.

Assumptions:

AssumptionDetails
Data typeOrdinal or continuous
IndependenceThe two groups are independent
Distribution shapeBoth groups should have a similar shape (though not necessarily normal). If shapes differ, the test detects differences in distribution, not just location
When to useWhen data is skewed, has outliers, is ordinal, or n is small (< 20)

Hypotheses:

H₀: The two groups come from the same distribution (no systematic difference)
H₁: The distributions differ (one group tends to have higher/lower values)

Example: Pain scores (1–10 scale) after two different anaesthetics. Ordinal data → Mann-Whitney is appropriate.

How it works:

All values from both groups are combined and ranked from lowest to highest. The rank sum for each group is calculated. If one group has systematically higher values, its rank sum will be unusually high (or low). The U statistic counts how often a value from group 1 precedes a value from group 2 in the ranking.

U₁ = n₁n₂ + n₁(n₁+1)/2 − R₁

SymbolMeaning
n₁, n₂Sample sizes of group 1 and group 2
R₁Sum of ranks assigned to group 1 in the combined ranking
U₁How often a group 1 value precedes a group 2 value
U = min(U₁, U₂)The test statistic — compared to critical value or normal approximation

Rank-based tests detect systematic differences — if one treatment consistently produces higher values, the rank sum will be shifted. However, they have less statistical power than parametric tests when assumptions are met.
Mann-Whitney vs Wilcoxon: Mann-Whitney is for independent groups (different subjects). Wilcoxon signed-rank is for paired data (same subjects, two conditions). Both use ranks, but the logic is different — Mann-Whitney compares rank sums between groups, Wilcoxon compares signed ranks of differences within pairs.

17. Wilcoxon signed-rank test

What is tested: Whether paired observations differ systematically. It is the non-parametric alternative to the paired t-test. Instead of assuming normally distributed differences, it works with the ranks of absolute differences.

Assumptions:

AssumptionDetails
Data typeOrdinal or continuous (paired)
Paired dataEach observation has a natural pair
SymmetryThe distribution of differences should be roughly symmetric (not necessarily normal)
When to useWhen differences are skewed, have outliers, or data is ordinal

Hypotheses:

H₀: The median difference between pairs is zero (no systematic bias)
H₁: The median difference is not zero

Example: 15 blood samples measured by two methods. Differences are skewed with an outlier. Paired t-test would be unreliable → Wilcoxon signed-rank is appropriate.

How it works:

  1. Compute differences di = x1i − x2i for each pair.
  2. Discard pairs where di = 0 (no difference — carries no information).
  3. Rank the absolute values |di| from smallest to largest (ignore the sign).
  4. Assign each rank back to its original sign:
    • W⁺ = sum of ranks where di was positive (condition 1 was higher than condition 2)
    • W⁻ = sum of ranks where di was negative (condition 2 was higher than condition 1)
  5. If there is no systematic difference, W⁺ and W⁻ should be roughly equal. If one is much larger → systematic shift in that direction.

W = min(W⁺, W⁻)

Compare W to a critical value table. For large n, a normal approximation is used.

Worked example — Before vs After treatment:

10 patients measured before and after treatment. Is there a systematic change?

For n = 10 at α = 0.05 (two-sided), Wcrit = 8.
23.5 > 8 → Fail to reject H₀ — no significant systematic difference between before and after.
Detecting systematic bias with ranks: If method B consistently reads higher than method A, most differences will be positive, and W⁻ will be small → significant result. The Wilcoxon test is robust against outliers because extreme values get the same rank increment as moderate ones.

18. Chi-square — Goodness of Fit

What is tested: Whether the observed frequency distribution of a categorical variable matches an expected (theoretical) distribution. For example: does the distribution of blood types in our hospital population match the known national distribution?

Assumptions:

AssumptionDetails
Data typeCategorical (nominal or ordinal) — counts/frequencies
IndependenceEach observation falls into exactly one category
Expected frequencyAll expected cell counts ≥ 5. If not, combine categories or use an exact test
Sample sizeTotal n should be reasonably large (≥ 20–30 as rough guide)

Hypotheses:

H₀: Observed frequencies match the expected distribution
H₁: Observed frequencies deviate from the expected distribution

Example: Expected blood type distribution: A=44%, O=42%, B=10%, AB=4%. Observed in 200 patients: A=96, O=78, B=18, AB=8. Does our sample match?

Test statistic:

χ² = Σ (Oi − Ei)² / Ei     df = k − 1

SymbolMeaning
OiObserved count — the actual number of observations in category i
EiExpected count — the number you would expect in category i if H₀ were true
(Oi − Ei)²Squared deviation — how far observed differs from expected (squared to make all values positive)
/ EiDivided by expected — normalises so categories with large counts don't dominate
ΣSum across all categories
kNumber of categories
df = k − 1Degrees of freedom

Large χ² → observed differs from expected → reject H₀.

Worked example — Blood type distribution:

A hospital wants to check if its patient population matches the known national blood type distribution. 200 patients are typed.

GOF vs Independence: Goodness of Fit tests one variable against a known distribution. Independence tests whether two variables are associated. Same formula, different question — GOF has df = k − 1, Independence has df = (r−1)(c−1).
When expected counts are too small: If any Ei < 5, the χ² approximation is unreliable. Solutions: combine small categories (e.g., merge B and AB into "other"), increase sample size, or use an exact multinomial test.

19. Chi-square — Test of Independence

What is tested: Whether two categorical variables are associated (dependent) or independent. For example: is there an association between blood type and susceptibility to a specific infection?

Assumptions:

AssumptionDetails
Data typeTwo categorical variables arranged in a contingency table (r × c)
IndependenceEach subject appears only once in the table
Expected frequencyAll expected cell counts ≥ 5. For 2×2 tables with small expected counts, use Fisher's exact test

Hypotheses:

H₀: The two variables are independent (no association)
H₁: The two variables are associated

Example: 2×2 table — ICU admission (yes/no) vs. diabetes status (yes/no). Is diabetes associated with ICU admission?

Test statistic:

χ² = Σ (Oij − Eij)² / Eij     df = (r−1)(c−1)

SymbolMeaning
OijObserved count in row i, column j
EijExpected count if the two variables were independent: Eij = (row total × column total) / grand total
rNumber of rows in the contingency table
cNumber of columns in the contingency table
df = (r−1)(c−1)Degrees of freedom

The test compares what we observe against what we would expect if the two variables were completely independent.

Worked example — Vaccination & Influenza:

20. Fisher's exact test

What is tested: The same as chi-square independence — whether two categorical variables are associated — but computed exactly rather than using a large-sample approximation. It is the preferred test for 2×2 tables with small expected cell counts (any expected count < 5).

Assumptions:

AssumptionDetails
Data typeTwo categorical variables in a 2×2 contingency table
IndependenceObservations are independent
Fixed marginsRow and column totals are considered fixed
When to useWhen any expected cell count < 5 (where chi-square is unreliable). Also valid for any 2×2 table regardless of size

Hypotheses:

H₀: No association between the two variables (odds ratio = 1)
H₁: There is an association (odds ratio ≠ 1)

Example: A rare adverse reaction occurs in 2 of 8 patients in the treatment group vs. 0 of 10 in the control group. Expected counts are too small for chi-square → Fisher's exact test.

How it works:

Fisher's test calculates the exact probability of observing the current table (or a more extreme one) under H₀, using the hypergeometric distribution. No approximation is needed — hence "exact". The p-value is the sum of probabilities for all tables as extreme or more extreme than the observed one.

The 2×2 table:

Outcome +Outcome −Total
Group 1aba + b
Group 2cdc + d
Totala + cb + dN

Expected cell counts:

Eij = (row total × column total) / N

When to use Fisher's vs Chi-square: Calculate the expected count for each cell. If any expected count is < 5, use Fisher's exact test. The rule is about expected counts, not observed — you can observe 0 in a cell and still use chi-square if the expected count is ≥ 5.

Effect size — Odds Ratio:

OR = (a × d) / (b × c)

OR valueInterpretation
OR = 1No association — the outcome is equally likely in both groups
OR > 1Group 1 is more likely to have the outcome than Group 2
OR < 1Group 1 is less likely to have the outcome than Group 2

The 95% CI for the odds ratio is calculated using the Woolf logit method: ln(OR) ± 1.96 × √(1/a + 1/b + 1/c + 1/d). If the CI excludes 1.0, the association is statistically significant.

Example: Testing whether a new rapid test detects infection as well as the standard test.

InfectedNot infected
Rapid test positivea = 8b = 2
Rapid test negativec = 1d = 14

Expected count for cell c: (1+14) × (8+1) / 25 = 5.4 ≥ 5 — chi-square would work, but Fisher's is always valid.
OR = (8 × 14) / (2 × 1) = 56.0 — strong association. p = 0.0002 → significant.
Patients testing positive on the rapid test are 56 times more likely to be truly infected.
Key insight: Fisher's exact test gives the same answer as chi-square for large samples, but is always valid regardless of sample size. In practice, many statisticians recommend using Fisher's exact test for all 2×2 tables — it is never wrong, only sometimes unnecessarily conservative.

21. McNemar's test

What is tested: Whether there is a systematic change in a binary outcome between two paired conditions. It focuses on the discordant pairs — cases where the outcome changed between conditions.

Assumptions:

AssumptionDetails
Data typeBinary/dichotomous outcome, paired observations
Paired dataSame subjects measured under two conditions (before/after, method A/method B)
Sample sizeThe number of discordant pairs (b + c) should be ≥ 10. For fewer, use the exact binomial version

The 2×2 table for paired data:

Condition 2: PositiveCondition 2: Negative
Condition 1: Positivea (concordant +/+)b (discordant +/−)
Condition 1: Negativec (discordant −/+)d (concordant −/−)

Hypotheses:

H₀: The probability of changing in each direction is equal: P(+→−) = P(−→+), i.e. b = c
H₁: There is a systematic shift in one direction (b ≠ c)

Example: 100 patients tested for an infection before and after treatment. Concordant (both positive or both negative) pairs are irrelevant. Only the discordant pairs matter: 15 patients converted from positive to negative (b), 3 from negative to positive (c). Is the treatment effective?

Test statistic:

χ² = (|b − c| − 1)² / (b + c)     df = 1

The "−1" is the continuity correction (Yates'). Only the discordant cells (b and c) are used. If b and c are roughly equal, the shift is not systematic. If one dominates, there is a directional change.

SymbolMeaning
bDiscordant pairs: Condition 1 positive → Condition 2 negative
cDiscordant pairs: Condition 1 negative → Condition 2 positive
|b − c|Absolute difference between discordant counts
− 1Yates' continuity correction (improves approximation for small counts)
b + cTotal discordant pairs — the only data that drives the test

Effect size — Odds Ratio (b/c):

The p-value tells you whether the shift is significant. The odds ratio tells you how large the asymmetry is:

OR = b / c

OR valueInterpretation
OR = 1Equal change in both directions — no systematic shift
OR > 1More changes from positive → negative (b dominates)
OR < 1More changes from negative → positive (c dominates)

95% CI for the odds ratio: if the CI excludes 1.0, the asymmetry is statistically significant.

Example: 100 patients tested before and after treatment. 15 converted positive → negative (b = 15), 3 converted negative → positive (c = 3).

OR = 15 / 3 = 5.0 — patients are 5 times more likely to convert from positive to negative than the reverse.
χ² = (|15 − 3| − 1)² / (15 + 3) = 121/18 = 6.72, p = 0.010 → significant.

The p-value says "the shift is real." The OR says "it's a 5-fold shift." Both are needed for a complete interpretation.
Worked example — Medication A vs B:

53 patients are treated with both Medication A and Medication B (paired design). For each patient, the outcome is B (better) or U (unchanged).

Key insight: McNemar's test is specifically designed to detect systematic bias in paired categorical data. Concordant pairs (a and d) carry no information about change — the entire test is driven by the discordant pairs. The odds ratio (b/c) quantifies the magnitude of that shift.

22. Cohen's Kappa & Weighted Kappa

What is tested: The level of agreement between two raters (or two methods) classifying the same items into categories, beyond what would be expected by chance alone. Kappa = 1 means perfect agreement, Kappa = 0 means agreement is no better than chance.

Assumptions:

AssumptionDetails
Data typeCategorical — two raters classify the same n items into the same set of categories
IndependenceThe items rated are independent of each other
Same categoriesBoth raters use the same category set
Cohen's vs. WeightedCohen's Kappa: nominal categories (disagree equally regardless of which categories). Weighted Kappa: ordinal categories (partial credit for near-misses)

Formula:

κ = (Po − Pe) / (1 − Pe)

Po — observed agreement:

The proportion of cases where both raters assigned the same category. For a 2×2 table:

Rater 2: PositiveRater 2: NegativeTotal
Rater 1: Positiveaba + b
Rater 1: Negativecdc + d
Totala + cb + dN

Po = (a + d) / N

where a = both say positive, d = both say negative. These are the concordant cells.

Pe — expected agreement by chance:

If the two raters were classifying independently at random, how often would they agree just by coincidence? We calculate this from each rater's marginal rates:

ComponentMeaningFormula
Rater 1 positive rateHow often Rater 1 says "Positive"(a + b) / N
Rater 2 positive rateHow often Rater 2 says "Positive"(a + c) / N
Rater 1 negative rateHow often Rater 1 says "Negative"(c + d) / N
Rater 2 negative rateHow often Rater 2 says "Negative"(b + d) / N

Then the expected chance agreement is:

Pe = ( (a+b)⁄N × (a+c)⁄N ) + ( (c+d)⁄N × (b+d)⁄N )

In numbers: 200 samples. Rater 1 says "Positive" on 120 → rate = 120/200 = 0.60. Rater 2 says "Positive" on 110 → rate = 110/200 = 0.55.

Pe = (0.60 × 0.55) + (0.40 × 0.45) = 0.33 + 0.18 = 0.51

So by pure chance, they would agree 51% of the time. If they actually agree 85% of the time (Po = 0.85):
κ = (0.85 − 0.51) / (1 − 0.51) = 0.34 / 0.49 = 0.69 — substantial agreement.
What Kappa does: It subtracts the chance agreement (Pe) from the observed agreement (Po), then scales by the maximum possible improvement over chance (1 − Pe). This is why κ = 0 means "no better than chance" — not "no agreement".

Interpretation (Landis & Koch):

KappaAgreement level
< 0.00Poor (worse than chance)
0.00 – 0.20Slight
0.21 – 0.40Fair
0.41 – 0.60Moderate
0.61 – 0.80Substantial
0.81 – 1.00Almost perfect
Example: Two pathologists independently classify 200 tissue samples as "malignant" or "benign". They agree on 170 (Po = 0.85). But by chance alone (given their individual rates), they would agree on 140 (Pe = 0.70). κ = (0.85 − 0.70) / (1 − 0.70) = 0.50 — moderate agreement.

Weighted Kappa (ordinal data):

When categories are ordered (e.g., "normal", "borderline", "abnormal"), disagreeing by one step is less severe than disagreeing by two steps. Weighted Kappa assigns partial credit using weights. Two common weighting schemes:

WeightingFormulaWhen to use
Linearw = 1 − |i−j| / (k−1)When each step of disagreement is equally important
Quadraticw = 1 − (i−j)² / (k−1)²When larger disagreements are disproportionately worse (most common)
Kappa paradoxes: Kappa can be misleadingly low when prevalence is very high or very low (most items fall in one category), even if raw agreement is high. This is called the prevalence problem. Always report both raw agreement (Po) and Kappa together.

  1. Motulsky, H.J. (2014). Intuitive Biostatistics: A Nonmathematical Guide to Statistical Thinking (3rd ed.). Oxford University Press.
    Hypothesis testing & p-values: pp. 127–169 · Statistical power: pp. 170–196 · Confidence intervals: pp. 43–58 · Statistical vs. clinical significance: pp. 197–210 · Frequentist interpretation: pp. 127–140 · Bayesian contrast: pp. 321–356
  2. Altman, D.G. (1991). Practical Statistics for Medical Research. Chapman & Hall/CRC.
    Central Limit Theorem: pp. 53–88 · Hypothesis testing framework: pp. 153–178 · Type I & II errors: pp. 153–165 · t-tests & quantitative tests: pp. 179–228 · Categorical tests (Chi-square, Fisher's, McNemar): pp. 229–280 · Kappa: pp. 403–409
  3. Gelman, A., Carlin, J.B., Stern, H.S., & Rubin, D.B. (2004). Bayesian Data Analysis (2nd ed.). Chapman & Hall/CRC.
    Bayesian probability and inference (contrast with frequentist framework): pp. 1–30
