1. Why accurate results matter

Every clinical decision — diagnosis, treatment, monitoring — depends on a laboratory result. When a clinician reads "potassium 6.2 mmol/L," they act on it: they may cancel surgery, administer emergency treatment, or order repeat testing. If that result is wrong, the patient may receive unnecessary treatment or, worse, miss treatment they need.

What happens when results are wrong?

Error typeClinical consequenceExample
False highUnnecessary treatment, delayed procedures, patient anxietyPotassium falsely elevated due to haemolysis → patient receives insulin/glucose they don't need → risk of iatrogenic hypoglycaemia (and a missed real cause of the symptoms)
False lowMissed diagnosis, delayed treatmentTroponin falsely low → AMI missed → patient sent home
ImpreciseUnreliable monitoring, inconsistent follow-up decisionsHbA1c varies ±8% between runs → clinician can't tell if diabetes treatment is working
Consistently biasedSystematic misclassification of patientsGlucose reads 0.5 mmol/L too high → healthy patients flagged as pre-diabetic

The laboratory's responsibility: You do not just produce numbers — you produce clinical decisions. Every imprecision and every bias translates directly into a risk to patient care. Analytical performance evaluation exists to quantify that risk and ensure it stays within clinically acceptable limits.

Think of it as signal vs noise: the signal is the true clinical change the clinician needs to see (a real rise in potassium, a real drop in HbA1c). The noise is everything that obscures it — biological fluctuation plus analytical imprecision and bias. If the noise is too large, the signal disappears into it and the clinical decision is made on the wrong information.

This module is, at its core, about one question: is the total noise in the result small enough that the clinical signal still gets through? Total noise has three sources — biological, pre-analytical, and analytical — and only the analytical noise is what this module unpacks in depth. The other two are introduced in — "Types of variation — biological, pre-analytical, and analytical" (where each source is broken down) and — "Pre-analytical, analytical, and post-analytical errors" (where the error phases are framed together). The analytical noise is broken into the performance characteristics ISO 15189 requires the lab to evaluate: precision (), bias (), linearity, interferences, detection capability (LoB / LoD / LoQ), and reference intervals (the last four covered in the Compliance group). Two parallel statistical frameworks then combine them into a single per-result error metric — the CLSI/Westgard framework (TE, TAE, Sigma) and the GUM/ISO framework (Measurement Uncertainty, MU); both explored in .

Next → Signal and noise both come from variation — so before we can evaluate analytical error, we need to understand the two fundamental sources of variation in any laboratory result: the patient (biological variation — the unavoidable baseline noise we cannot remove) and the measurement system (analytical variation — the noise we add on top, and the only one we can control). Together they define how much noise sits between the clinician and the true clinical signal.

2. Types of variation — biological, pre-analytical, and analytical

Every laboratory result is a combination of three independent sources of variation. All three add noise to the signal — none can be ignored:

SourceWhat it isCan you control it?
Biological variation (from the patient)The patient's own analyte levels fluctuate naturally — from hour to hour, day to day, and between individuals. This is not error; it is physiology.No — it is inherent to the patient. But you must understand it to set meaningful performance limits.
Pre-analytical variation (from sample handling)Everything that happens to the sample before it reaches the instrument: patient preparation (fasting, posture, time of day), tube type, tourniquet time, transport, centrifugation, storage. Often the largest source of noise — and the cause of ~60–70% of all lab errors.Partly — through SOPs, training, sample acceptance criteria, and standardised collection. Cannot be removed by statistics. Validation of changes to any of these factors is covered in Sample Validation → Concepts & Theory.
Analytical variation (from the instrument)The measurement system introduces its own variation: pipetting, reagent stability, detector noise, calibration drift. This is the error your laboratory can and must control.Yes — through quality control, calibration, and method validation.

(Post-analytical errors — transcription, wrong reference interval applied, miscommunication with the clinician — also affect the final result, but they are mistakes rather than statistical variation. See .)

Biological variation is the baseline noise — it is always there and cannot be reduced. Pre-analytical and analytical noise stack on top of that baseline. The total noise in a result is therefore biological (baseline) + pre-analytical + analytical. A clinician cannot tell them apart — they only see the final number. Your job is to keep the two components on top of the baseline small enough that the clinical signal still gets through.

Concrete example: if a patient's glucose rises from 5.0 to 5.4 mmol/L, is that a real clinical change or just biological fluctuation plus measurement imprecision? Whether you can tell depends on how the signal (0.4 mmol/L) compares with the combined biological + analytical noise.

Smaller biological variation → stricter analytical requirements. An analyte with a narrow CVi (e.g. sodium, CVi ≈ 0.6%) leaves almost no room for analytical error — even a 1% CV from the instrument would dominate the total variation. In contrast, an analyte with wide CVi (e.g. CRP, CVi ≈ 42%) is far more tolerant of analytical imprecision because the patient's own variation is already large. This is why the EFLM biological variation database gives different allowable CV% for every analyte — the limits are derived directly from CVi.

3. Biological variation — CVi and CVg explained

Biological variation describes the natural fluctuation in analyte concentrations in the human body. It has two components:

CVi — Intra-individual biological variation

CVi (also written CVI) is the variation within a single person over time, when they are in a stable physiological state. Even a completely healthy person's blood analytes fluctuate from day to day and hour to hour due to normal biological rhythms, hydration, posture, and circadian effects.

CVi example — Potassium: CVi ≈ 4.6% → potassium fluctuates by ≈ 4.6% within the same person week to week

CVi is measured in studies where the same individual is sampled repeatedly over time, under controlled conditions.

CVg — Inter-individual biological variation

CVg (also written CVG) is the variation between different people. Even in a healthy population, people have different set-points for the same analyte. CVg is typically larger than CVi.

CVg example — Potassium: CVg ≈ 5.6% → different healthy people have potassium levels that differ by ≈ 5.6% between individuals

The EFLM Biological Variation Database publishes both CVi and CVg estimates for hundreds of analytes — the authoritative source for the values used in the formulas below.

Why CVi and CVg matter for analytical quality

CVi and CVg are used to derive allowable limits for your analytical method — this is about setting quality goals for your analyser, not about interpreting individual patient results.

The reasoning behind the formulas:

  • Imprecision vs CVi: If your analytical CV% is large relative to CVi, analytical noise will obscure real biological signals — you cannot tell whether a change in a patient's result reflects true biology or measurement variation. The goal CVa ≤ 0.5 × CVi ensures your analytical noise is less than half the natural biological noise within a person.
  • Bias vs CVi + CVg: If your method has systematic bias, it shifts results away from the true value. If that shift is large relative to the combined biological variation (CVi and CVg), it can displace results incorrectly relative to the population reference range. The goal Bias ≤ 0.25 × √(CVi² + CVg²) keeps bias small enough not to distort clinical interpretation.
  • Total Allowable Error (TAE): A single patient result is affected by imprecision and bias at the same time, so a single combined limit is needed. Bias is added directly because it always shifts results in one direction. Imprecision is multiplied by 1.65 — the z-score for the one-sided 95% bound of a normal distribution — so that 95% of single results stay within the limit. The combined goal TAE_a ≤ 1.65 × CVa + Bias_a therefore caps the worst plausible error in any one result. Unpacked properly in .

The EFLM/Westgard hierarchy of quality goals uses CVi and CVg to derive these specific, clinically meaningful allowable limits:

Allowable imprecision (desirable goal): CVa ≤ 0.5 × CVi
Allowable bias (desirable goal): Bias_a ≤ 0.25 × √(CVi² + CVg²)
Allowable Total Error (desirable goal): TAE_a ≤ 1.65 × CVa + Bias_a

The factor 0.5 for imprecision means: your analytical noise should be less than half the natural biological noise within a person. If it is, analytical imprecision does not meaningfully obscure biological signals.

The factor 0.25 for bias uses both CVi and CVg because bias shifts results relative to both the patient's own set-point and the population-based reference interval.

Three levels of biological variation goals

LevelImprecision (CVa)BiasWhen to use
Desirable0.5 × CVi0.25 × √(CVi² + CVg²)Target for most clinical analytes
Minimum0.75 × CVi0.375 × √(CVi² + CVg²)Acceptable if desirable is not achievable
Optimum0.25 × CVi0.125 × √(CVi² + CVg²)Highest clinical demand (e.g. therapeutic drug monitoring)

Index of Individuality (II)

The ratio of within-person to between-person biological variation tells you whether the population reference interval is even useful for a given analyte. It is calculated as:

Index of Individuality: II = CVi / CVg

If a person fluctuates a lot relative to how much people differ from each other (high II), the population range covers their variation well. If a person barely fluctuates compared with how different people are (low II), each person sits in their own narrow band inside the population range — and a value can change a lot for that individual while still looking "normal" against the population.

The conventional thresholds:

IIInterpretationWhat to use clinically
≥ 1.4 (high)Patient fluctuates a lot relative to between-person differences. The population reference interval (RI) — the range derived from healthy people, covered fully in — covers the patient's natural range well.Population RI is meaningful.
0.6 – 1.4 (intermediate)Population RI is usable but with caution — both individual and population variation matter.Population RI + clinical context.
< 0.6 (low)Each patient sits in their own narrow band inside the population RI. A value can change substantially for that patient while still appearing "normal" against the population.Patient's own baseline + Reference Change Value (RCV). Examples: CRP, ferritin, troponin.

This is why a troponin "in the normal range" can still represent a clinically important rise from that patient's own baseline — and why the meaningful reference for low-II analytes is the patient, not the population.

In signal/noise terms: II tells you which biological noise baseline the signal must exceed — between-people noise (CVg) for high-II, within-person noise (CVi) for low-II. The RI is the right noise-aware tool for high-II analytes; RCV is the right tool for low-II analytes (defined in alongside the parallel MU+RI tool).

4. Sources of analytical error

Every measurement a laboratory makes contains some error. Understanding the type of error is the first step in knowing what to do about it.

Error typeWhat it meansExample
Random errorUnpredictable variation around the true value. Changes direction each measurement.Pipetting variation, detector noise
Systematic error (bias)Consistent shift in one direction. Results are always too high or always too low.Mis-calibration, reagent lot change
Total errorThe combined effect of random and systematic error on any single patient result.TE = |Bias| + z × SD

Key principle: Precision addresses random error. Bias addresses systematic error. Their combined effect — quantified by either TE (CLSI/Westgard) or MU (GUM/ISO) — addresses both together. You need all three to evaluate whether your method is fit for clinical use.

5. Pre-analytical, analytical, and post-analytical errors

A laboratory result passes through three phases before it reaches the clinician. Errors can occur at any stage:

PhaseWhat happensError examplesFrequency
Pre-analyticalEverything before the sample reaches the analyser: patient preparation, sample collection, transport, centrifugation, storageHaemolysis, wrong tube, insufficient fasting, delayed transport, incorrect labelling~60–70% of all lab errors
AnalyticalThe measurement itself: calibration, reagent performance, instrument precision, interferenceCalibration drift, reagent degradation, matrix effects, pipetting imprecision~10–15% of all lab errors
Post-analyticalEverything after the measurement: result validation, reporting, interpretation, communicationTranscription errors (correctly measured result mis-copied — e.g. typing "1.4" instead of "4.1" into the LIS, mis-hearing a phoned value), wrong reference interval applied, delayed reporting, misinterpretation~20–25% of all lab errors

Pre-analytical errors are by far the most common, but day-to-day they are controlled through SOPs, training, and specimen rejection criteria — not through statistical monitoring of the running method. When the pre-analytical pathway itself changes (a new tube, additive, transport, storage), statistics does come in: that validation work is covered in Sample Validation → Concepts & Theory. Post-analytical errors are mostly discrete mistakes, controlled by IT systems, double-checks, and reporting workflows.

The rest of this module focuses on the analytical phase — the part where the method itself is continuously quantified and controlled using statistics (precision, bias, total error, QC, EQA). When we say "error" in the following sections, we mean analytical error unless stated otherwise.

6. Why we do controls

A laboratory cannot measure every patient sample twice. Instead, it runs control materials — samples with a known or expected value — alongside patient samples. If the control result is within acceptable limits, the patient results measured in the same run are considered reliable.

QuestionControl typeWhat it tells youPerformance term measured
Am I consistent with myself over time?IQC — internal quality control: same stable material run every batch, compared to your own historical mean ± SDRandom variation and drift in your method, run to run and day to dayImprecision (CVa) — the random-error term of TAE. Cannot detect bias on its own.
Do I agree with my peers?EQA / EQAS — external quality assessment: blinded sample sent to many labs; your result compared to peer-group meanWhether your result is shifted relative to the consensus of other labs running the same analyteBias vs. peer consensus — the systematic-error term of TAE, relative to peers.
Am I correct in absolute terms?CRM — certified reference material: value assigned by a reference method, traceable to SI unitsWhether your result matches the true value, independently of what other labs doBias vs. true value — the systematic-error term of TAE, against an absolute reference.

Analogy: Imagine you have a 10 kg calibration weight. IQC is weighing that same 10 kg weight on your own scale every morning — if the reading drifts away from 10 kg over time, you know your scale is changing. EQA is sending that same 10 kg weight (or an identical one) to several other labs to weigh on their scales — if your scale reads 10.0 kg but the others all read 10.3 kg, your scale is systematically off compared to everyone else's, even though it looks stable in your own daily check.

Controls are not patient samples. A control material may behave differently from a real patient sample due to matrix effects (stabilisers, preservatives, animal-sourced proteins). A method can pass all QC checks and still produce biased results on patient samples.

7. IQC, CRM, and EQA — monitoring and detecting bias

Three control types work together. IQC monitors day-to-day stability and precision; CRM and EQA are the two ways to actually detect bias, since bias can only be measured when you know (or can compare to) the true value.

Internal Quality Control (IQC)

A stable control material run alongside patient samples in every run. The mean and SD are established from your own initial runs (typically 20 days), and every subsequent result is compared to those limits using Westgard or similar rules.

  • Advantage: Cheap, continuous, and immediate — flags drift, shifts, or sudden imprecision in real time.
  • Limitation: Cannot detect bias on its own — the target value is your own historical mean, so a method that has been wrong from day one will pass IQC indefinitely. IQC tells you whether you are consistent with yourself, not whether you are correct.

Certified Reference Material (CRM)

A material with a known, certified assigned value — traceable to a reference method or SI units. You measure it on your analyser and compare your result to the certified value.

  • Advantage: Direct, objective comparison to a known true value.
  • Limitation: CRMs are expensive. Commutability must be verified — the CRM matrix should behave the same as real patient samples on your method.

Pitfalls — not every CRM works for every method:

  • Method-specific CRMs: some are assigned using one specific assay (often immunoassays). Measuring on a different platform can show "bias" that is really just a difference in what each method detects.
  • Commutability (does the material behave like a real patient sample on your analyser?): a CRM matrix can behave differently from patient samples on your analyser. An apparent bias may be a matrix effect, not real method error. Always check the certificate for a commutability statement.
  • Wrong concentration level: a CRM only assesses bias at its own level. A method can be unbiased at the medical decision point and biased at the upper end of the measuring range — or vice versa.

External Quality Assessment (EQA)

Also called proficiency testing (e.g. DEKS in Denmark, UKNEQAS in the UK). The same sample is sent to all participating laboratories. Your result is compared against the peer group mean or an assigned target value.

  • Advantage: Independent external check. Reveals systematic bias that internal QC would never detect — because internal QC only tells you if you are consistent, not if you are correct.
  • Limitation: EQA materials are often lyophilized or stabilized. Their matrix may differ from real patient samples, so apparent bias may be a matrix effect rather than a true analytical problem.

How they complement each other: IQC catches drift and imprecision continuously but cannot find bias. CRM gives you an absolute bias against a known true value. EQA gives you a relative bias compared to your peers. A bias confirmed in both CRM and EQA is almost certainly real — and IQC tells you whether the rest of the time the method is at least stable at that bias level.

8. Matrix effects

The matrix is everything in a sample other than the analyte being measured — water, proteins, lipids, salts, stabilizers. Different matrices can affect how an instrument responds to the same concentration of analyte.

This is why a bias seen in EQA or with QC material does not always mean your method is wrong. The material's matrix may simply behave differently from real patient samples on your analyser.

Never conclude there is a bias based on a single material alone. Confirm bias using a commutable material — one whose matrix behaves like real patient samples on your method. Patient-based bias estimation (e.g. method comparison against a reference method) is the most reliable approach.

10. Precision — what it means and how to measure it

Precision describes how reproducible your measurements are — how close repeated measurements of the same sample are to each other. It does not tell you whether the results are correct, only whether they are consistent.

Precision is expressed as:

  • SD (Standard Deviation) — in the same units as the measurement. Useful for fixed-concentration QC material.
  • CV% (Coefficient of Variation) — SD expressed as a percentage of the mean. Allows comparison across different concentration levels and analytes.
CV% formula: CV% = (SD / Mean) × 100

Precision ≠ accuracy. A method can be very precise (low CV%) but systematically biased. Think of a scale that always reads 0.5 kg too high — consistent but wrong. Both precision and bias must be evaluated independently.

11. Repeatability, intermediate precision, and reproducibility

These three terms describe precision under different conditions. The conditions you vary during your study determine which type you are measuring.

🟢 Repeatability (within-run)
Same sample · same day · same run · same operator · same reagents · same calibration
This is the tightest precision you will ever achieve. It represents only random pipetting and detector variation.
🟡 Intermediate precision (within-laboratory)
Same laboratory · different days · possibly different operators, reagent lots, or calibrations
This is what real-world performance looks like. Day-to-day drift is captured here. This is what patients experience.
🔴 Reproducibility (between-laboratory)
Different laboratories · different instruments · different everything
Used in method standardisation and reference interval studies. Broadest variation.

There is no single "intermediate precision" — what you vary defines what you measure. If you vary days only, you get day-to-day intermediate precision; if you also vary operators and reagent lots, you get a broader measure. Always state what conditions were varied when reporting it. Intermediate precision is always ≥ repeatability because it includes all the within-run variation plus additional between-day sources.

12. Practical study design — the duplicate method

The simplest precision study is to measure the same sample many times in one run and calculate the SD. This gives you repeatability — but nothing more.

To capture real-world laboratory performance, you need to spread measurements across multiple days and runs. CLSI publishes two formal protocols — EP05-A3 for full validation and EP15-A3 for verification — each with a specific recommended layout (see the last column of the table below).

DesignWhat you measureUse forRecommended layout (CLSI)
All measurements on one dayRepeatability only (Sw)Best-case precision estimateWithin-run only — typically ≥ 20 replicates of each level on the same day. Use only as a quick check; not sufficient for validation. There is no standalone CLSI protocol for repeatability — Sw simply falls out of EP05/EP15. The term itself is defined in ISO 5725-2 and VIM (JCGM 200) §2.20.
Measurements spread across multiple daysIntermediate precision (St = √(Sw² + Sb²))Real-world performance estimateEP05-A3 (full validation): 20 days × 2 runs/day × 2 replicates/run = 80 measurements per level (commonly written 20 × 2 × 2). This structured design lets nested ANOVA partition within-run (Sw) from between-run/between-day (Sb).
EP15-A3 (verification of manufacturer's claim): 5 days × 5 replicates/day = 25 measurements per level (5 × 5).
Run at least 2 concentration levels (low + high covering the medical decision range).

The replicates within each run give you the within-run SD (Sw). The variation of run means around the grand mean gives you the between-run component (Sb). Combined, they give you the intermediate precision SD (St).

Recommendation: Always spread your precision study across multiple days. A one-day study will make your method look more precise than it actually is under real laboratory conditions.

13. Why you cannot add standard deviations

When combining two sources of variation, it is tempting to simply add their SDs. This is incorrect and will always overestimate the true combined precision.

The correct approach is to add the variances (SD²), then take the square root:

Wrong — do not do this: St = Sw + Sb
Correct — combine variances: St = √(Sw² + Sb²)

Example: Sw = 2.0 and Sb = 2.0

  • Wrong: 2.0 + 2.0 = 4.0
  • Correct: √(2.0² + 2.0²) = √8 = 2.83

In this example the incorrect addition gives a result 41% higher than the correct value. The overestimate depends on the relative size of the two components — it is largest when they are equal, and smaller when one dominates — but it is always an overestimate. This is not a small rounding difference — it is a fundamental statistical error.

Rule: Variances add. Standard deviations do not. This applies everywhere in statistics — not just to precision calculations.

14. Bias — systematic error

Bias is a consistent, directional difference between your method's results and the true value. Unlike random error, bias does not cancel out over many measurements — it shifts all results in the same direction.

Absolute bias: Bias = Your mean − True (assigned) value
Relative bias (%): Bias% = ((Your mean − True value) / True value) × 100

Constant vs proportional bias

Bias does not always behave the same way across the measuring range. Two distinct shapes matter clinically:

  • Constant bias — a fixed offset that does not change with concentration. Every result is shifted by the same amount. Example: a method reads 0.5 mmol/L too high at every level — at 2 mmol/L it reports 2.5; at 20 mmol/L it reports 20.5. Typical causes: calibrator offset, baseline drift, reagent blank error. In regression against a reference method, constant bias shows up as an intercept ≠ 0.
  • Proportional bias — a scaling error that grows with concentration. The fractional deviation is constant, but the absolute gap widens. Example: a method reads 5% too high — at 10 mmol/L it reports 10.5; at 100 mmol/L it reports 105. Typical causes: calibrator value error, matrix effects that scale with analyte, method specificity differences. In regression, proportional bias shows up as a slope ≠ 1.

A method can have both at once (slope ≠ 1 and intercept ≠ 0). The four combinations are summarised in the method-comparison section (slope/intercept interpretation table). Clinically they matter differently: constant bias affects low-concentration results most (the fixed offset is a larger fraction of the true value there); proportional bias affects high-concentration results most (the absolute error grows with concentration).

Common causes of bias

  • Incorrect calibration or calibrator lot change
  • Reagent lot-to-lot variation
  • Interfering substances in the sample matrix
  • Carry-over from high-concentration samples
  • Incorrect reference interval or assigned value

Bias vs imprecision: Bias shifts the entire distribution of results. Imprecision spreads it. A biased method gives results that cluster around the wrong value. An imprecise method gives results scattered around the right value. Total error combines both effects.

Bias requires a reference — something with a known or agreed value to compare your result against. The three reference types each answer a slightly different question and carry different trade-offs.

Choosing between references

In practice, labs use a combination. The choice depends on what exists for the analyte and what question you are answering:

  • New method validation (in-house, modified, or comparator replacement): full method comparison against the current routine method — CLSI EP09-A3, ≥ 40 paired patient samples spanning the measuring range. Supplement with a CRM measurement if one exists for the analyte.
  • Commercial IVD verification: bias against EQA (ongoing participation) or CRM — the bias check defined in CLSI EP15-A3 (run alongside the EP15 precision verification). A full EP09 is not required unless the manufacturer's bias data cannot be applied to your population.
  • Ongoing monitoring (routine use): EQA participation is the default. Frequency depends on the scheme — typically 4–6 rounds per year for most clinical chemistry, more frequent for haematology and coagulation. Each round covers multiple analytes at once. Trends over several rounds detect slow drift that a single round cannot.
  • Investigating a suspected bias: use at least two independent references — a bias seen in EQA but not in CRM points to a matrix issue; a bias seen in both points to a real method problem.

Pitfalls that trip up real studies

  • Commutability — probably the single biggest pitfall. Control materials, EQA samples, and even some CRMs can behave differently from patient samples on your analyser. A non-commutable material shows apparent bias that is entirely a matrix artefact. Verify commutability before drawing conclusions; prefer patient-based references (EP09) for definitive bias assessment.
  • Wrong concentration level — a CRM only tells you about bias at its own level. A method can be unbiased at the medical decision point and biased at the top or bottom of the measuring range. Use multi-level references or run method comparison across the full range.
  • Peer-group groupthink — all labs using the same reagent lot, same calibrator, or same method family can share the same bias. EQA agreement with your peers does not guarantee trueness — it only guarantees you agree with them.
  • Method-specific CRMs across platforms — a CRM assigned by one specific assay (common in immunoassays) will show false bias when measured on a different platform. Check the CRM certificate for the assigned method; JCTLM publishes a list of commutable, SI-traceable CRMs.
  • Insufficient sample numbers — a mean bias from 5 paired samples has a CI wide enough to hide clinically significant bias. EP09 recommends ≥ 40 samples for method comparison; fewer is acceptable only for verification.
  • Bias varies with time — lot changes, calibrator drift, and reagent ageing can introduce or remove bias mid-study. A single point-in-time bias estimate should be confirmed by ongoing EQA trending.

What "passing" looks like

Each measured bias is compared against the allowable bias defined in the APS:

  • Statistical pass/fail: measured bias is within the APS acceptance criterion (e.g. |Bias%| ≤ 2.0%).
  • Confidence interval view: the 95% CI on the bias should sit inside the allowable range. A bias whose CI straddles the acceptance criterion is ambiguous — repeat with more samples before concluding.
  • Clinical view: even a statistically passing bias must make clinical sense. A bias at the medical decision point has more impact than a bias at the extreme ends of the measuring range.

If bias fails: investigate the root cause (calibration, matrix, interference, method specificity) before re-running. A bias correction factor may be applied if the correction is stable, documented, and validated — but the underlying cause must be understood first.

16. Total Allowable Error (TAE) and Total Error (TE)

Recall the framing from : the question is whether the total noise in a result is small enough that the clinical signal still gets through. Total noise is biological variation (the unavoidable baseline) plus the two analytical components — imprecision (random error) and bias (systematic error). The lab cannot do anything about biological variation, so the focus here is the analytical part. A patient result is affected by both imprecision and bias at the same time, and Total Error (TE) combines them into the maximum likely analytical error in any one result — the single number that tells you how much of the clinical signal could be obscured by the method on a given measurement.

From signal/noise to a concrete analytical requirement: the smallest clinical change a method needs to detect (the signal) sets the ceiling on the analytical noise it can carry. If a clinically meaningful change in HbA1c is, say, 5 mmol/mol, then the method's TE must be small enough that a true 5 mmol/mol shift is not lost inside the analytical noise. Total Allowable Error (TAE) is exactly that ceiling — the largest TE a method may have while still letting the clinical signal through. The condition TE ≤ TAE is the formal version of "noise small enough that the signal gets through".

NEWOne framing point before the formulas: ISO 15189 mandates Measurement Uncertainty (MU). It does not mandate TAE. TAE is the CLSI/Westgard/EFLM convention that historically dominates clinical chemistry; many labs use it alongside MU because it drives QC strategy more naturally. ISO accepts either, as long as the lab's chosen framework defines acceptance criteria, measures performance against them, and documents the result. The rest of this section uses TAE because that is what the QC tooling and Sigma-metric framework are built on; MU is its GUM/ISO companion (see the glossary entry for Measurement Uncertainty).

Total Allowable Error (TAE)

TAE is the formal name for the ceiling introduced above — the maximum total analytical error the clinical use of the result can tolerate before the signal is at risk of being lost in the noise. It is set before the method is evaluated, derived from biological variation (preferred) or fixed by regulatory requirements (e.g. CLIA), and acts as the laboratory's error budget for that analyte. The method is fit for clinical use when TE ≤ TAE.

Total Error (TE)

TE is the actual maximum analytical error the method delivers in any one patient result, given its current bias and imprecision. While TAE is the ceiling set before the study, TE is the number you get out of the study — the worst plausible single-result error your method actually has, combining both error types into one figure. Whether the method passes is decided by comparing the two: TE ≤ TAE → fit for clinical use.

Calculating TE

TE is calculated as:

Most common (CLSI/Westgard — 95% one-tailed): TE = |Bias%| + 1.65 × CV%
Alternative (97.5% one-tailed / stricter): TE = |Bias%| + 2.0 × CV%

Why two formulas? What z-score should you use?

The multiplier (1.65 or 2.0) is a z-score from the standard normal distribution. It defines what proportion of individual patient results you want to protect against exceeding the TAE:

MultiplierDistribution coverageWhat it meansWhen to use
1.65 95% one-tailed
(or 90% two-tailed)		 Up to 5% of results may exceed the TAE boundary due to random error — in the worst direction Standard CLSI/Westgard approach. Most published goals and validation criteria use this. Appropriate for routine chemistry where some error is tolerated.
2.0 ≈ 97.5% one-tailed
(or 95% two-tailed)		 Only 2.5% of results may exceed TAE — more protective against outliers More conservative laboratories, or analytes where a single erroneous result could cause serious patient harm (e.g. drug levels, coagulation, critical values).
1.96 97.5% one-tailed The formal 95% two-tailed z-score — sometimes used for consistency with other statistical tests Some ISO and EQA frameworks use 1.96; check your accreditation body's requirements.

The one-tailed vs two-tailed distinction: we use a one-tailed z-score because we care about errors in the worst direction (results that are too high OR too low — whichever pushes past the TAE limit). The bias is already included separately, so we only need to model how far random error can push a result in one additional direction. That is why 1.65 (not 1.96) corresponds to 95%. Practical guidance: use 1.65 when following CLSI EP15/Westgard protocols, biological variation goals, or Six Sigma calculations — this is the international standard. Use 2.0 if your laboratory policy or accreditation body requires greater assurance, or for high-stakes analytes. Be consistent — use the same multiplier throughout your validation and your TAE definition.

The method passes if TE ≤ TAE. Interpreting TE: bias and imprecision both consume part of the error budget, so a method with large bias has less room for imprecision and vice versa. A method can fail on total error even if both bias and imprecision individually appear acceptable — because they accumulate.

18. The Sigma metric

The Sigma metric is borrowed from Six Sigma industrial quality management. It expresses how many standard deviations of your method's imprecision fit within the allowable error budget after accounting for bias.

Sigma metric: Sigma = (TAE% − |Bias%|) / CV%

Note on the implicit multiplier: Sigma is the Six Sigma counterpart of the TE pass condition. It is built on the same one-tailed 95% z-score (1.65) used in TE = |Bias%| + 1.65 × CV%, which is the international standard (CLSI EP15, Westgard, biological-variation goals, Six Sigma). Some labs use 2.0 instead when local policy or accreditation requires greater assurance — if you do, use the same multiplier consistently in both TE and Sigma.

Sigma tells you how much room is left in the error budget for random variation:

SigmaPerformance levelInterpretation
≥ 6World classMinimal QC needed. Very rarely produces errors.
4 – 6ExcellentGood method. Standard QC is sufficient.
3 – 4AcceptableMore intensive QC required to catch errors.
< 3PoorMethod improvement needed. High QC burden to maintain safety.

19. What is compliance — and why does it matter?

MOVEDThe concepts section taught you what to measure and why. This compliance section teaches you how — the specific protocols, standards, and workflows that regulatory bodies require.

Understanding precision, bias, and total error is, at minimum, a professional standard — and in most jurisdictions, a regulatory requirement. Accreditation to ISO 15189 is common across the Nordic countries (DANAK, SWEDAC, Norsk Akkreditering, FINAS, ISAC) and much of Europe, but the legal picture is not uniform: in some Nordic countries it is no longer strictly mandatory and hospital laboratories accredit voluntarily as a quality standard. The US operates under CLIA as the federal floor, with ISO 15189 / CAP accreditation remaining optional, and in many other countries accreditation is voluntary altogether. Even where it is not legally required, the same statistical principles apply — because a laboratory result is a medical act that directly influences patient treatment, and the logic of why methods must be validated does not depend on the country.

Why labs choose ISO 15189 and CLSI even when it is not mandatory:

  • Patient safety and clinical trust — accredited labs demonstrate that their results are traceable, validated, and fit for clinical use, which underpins clinician trust in the numbers they act on.
  • Defensibility — when a result is questioned (clinically, legally, or in a complaint), a documented ISO 15189 / CLSI-compliant validation is the clearest defence the lab has.
  • Inter-laboratory comparability — results from accredited labs are directly comparable because they share the same validation logic and metrological traceability. A patient transferred between hospitals gets consistent interpretation.
  • International recognition — ISO 15189 accreditation is mutually recognised across participating countries (via ILAC), so results travel with the patient across borders.
  • Structured method evaluation — the CLSI EP protocols give a concrete, statistically sound framework instead of each lab reinventing the wheel. Less work, fewer errors, better defensibility.
  • Procurement and tender requirements — many buyers (insurers, public health systems, industry partners, clinical trials) require accredited results before contracting with the lab.
  • Staff training and audit readiness — accreditation creates an internal quality culture; every method has an owner, a protocol, and a review cycle. The cost is real, but so is the operational benefit.

20. ISO 15189 — Validation and Verification Requirements

ISO 15189 (Medical laboratories — Requirements for quality and competence) is the international accreditation standard for clinical laboratories. It demands that every measurement procedure is shown to be fit for clinical use before results are reported, and that the evidence stays current over the life of the method. Two axes determine what that evaluation looks like in practice: the type of method (what prior validation exists — full, partial, or none?) and the event that triggers the work (new install? lot change? relocation?). Both must be answered before a lab can say which CLSI protocol to run and how much of it.

A third, looser layer sits on top. ISO 15189 mandates the requirement: for every method in clinical use, the lab must hold a (re-)verification or validation record — a written study with its data, analysis, and pass/fail conclusion — and that record must show the method met its pre-defined specifications. What ISO 15189 does not do is prescribe the statistics or the study design that produced the record. The CLSI EP protocols are the de facto standard for producing that record — widely used, widely accepted, but not themselves law. A lab is free to use an equivalent protocol if it can defend the statistics. What genuinely binds a lab is its own SOP: once the lab has written "we use EP15 after a reagent lot change", auditors will hold it to that. In short: ISO says do it; CLSI says here is how; your SOP says this is what we do.

The four layers at a glance

Before diving in, here is the structural map everything below expands on:

  • ISO 15189 — says when a validation or verification is required, and the mandatory menu of performance characteristics to evaluate (precision, bias, measurement uncertainty (MU, §7.3.4), measuring range, reference interval, interferences). It does not specify the acceptance criteria or the protocol. Note: ISO mandates MU, not TAE — TAE is the CLSI/Westgard convention used alongside MU; see .
  • APS (Analytical Performance Specification) — a written document stating the method's intended clinical use and the resulting acceptance criteria (allowable CVa, bias, TAE, allowable MU, required LoD, etc.). Each criterion is derived from the Milan hierarchy; the document is finalised before any study is run.
  • CLSI EP protocols + GUM — the standard protocols for investigating each performance characteristic. CLSI EP covers the empirical studies (EP05 precision, EP09 method comparison, EP06 linearity, EP07 interferences, EP17 detection capability, EP25 stability, EP28 reference intervals …). GUM (the ISO Guide to the Expression of Uncertainty in Measurement) is the protocol for computing MU from the EP study data via quadrature combination. The lab can substitute an equivalent design if it can be defended statistically.
  • Pass/fail — the verdict works at two levels. Per characteristic: each performance characteristic in the APS is tested independently against its own acceptance criterion — a meets specification: yes / no verdict for each (precision CVa, bias, combined TE ≤ TAE, linearity, LoD, interferences, stability, measuring range, and so on). Overall: the method is fit for clinical use only when every per-characteristic verdict passes. There is no averaging or trade-off between characteristics.
    Note on bias, precision, and TAE — two philosophies: The Westgard view treats TAE as a single combined budget — as long as TE ≤ TAE, the method passes; the individual bias and CVa limits are guidelines for balancing the budget. The EFLM / Milan view keeps each characteristic as its own independent verdict — precision, bias, and TAE all have to pass on their own; excessive bias is a fail even when TE stays within TAE, because bias is systematic and correctable. Most European accreditation bodies follow the EFLM / Milan view. A lab that prefers the Westgard combined-budget approach must document that choice in its APS and defend it at audit.

Read in order: ISO tells you what is required, APS sets the acceptance criteria, the protocol is used to evaluate the method's performance, and you reach a pass/fail verdict by comparing each result to its acceptance criterion.

ISO 15189 in practice

ISO 15189 requires documented evidence that every method in clinical use meets its performance specifications. It says what must exist — an APS, a validation plan, and a validation report — but does not prescribe how (study design, statistics, or report format). The APS fulfils the acceptance-criteria requirement (the specification itself, with justification — see the APS subsection below). The validation plan and report fulfil the evidence requirement: how the method was tested and what the result was.

ISO 15189 §7.3 lists the performance characteristics that must be evaluated, regardless of whether the scope is verification or validation. This is the checklist auditors work from:

  • Precision — repeatability and intermediate precision
  • Trueness / bias — against CRM, EQA, or reference method
  • Measurement uncertainty — combined estimate of all error sources
  • Analytical measurement range (AMR) — the reportable range
  • Reference intervals — verified or established for the local population
  • Interferences — HIL and common drugs

ISO 15189 also specifies the minimum content of the validation plan and the validation report — the plan written before data collection, the report after:

  • Study design and justification of acceptance criteria defined before data collection
  • Raw data and statistical analysis
  • Pass/fail conclusion against each acceptance criterion
  • Authorisation by the laboratory director before clinical release

Pre-defined acceptance criteria are where auditors focus. A criterion adjusted after seeing results is a direct non-conformity finding — the lab must show that allowable bias and CV% targets were set in the validation plan, not derived from the data itself.

The APS in practice

The APS is a written document with two parts: a description of the method's intended clinical use (patient population, the decision the result will drive, the medical decision points the method must discriminate across), and the justification of the acceptance criteria derived from it — the quantitative acceptance criteria the validation will be measured against (allowable TAE, allowable CVa and bias, required measuring range, required LoD/LoQ at the medical decision point), together with the source used to derive each (biological variation, clinical outcome, state-of-the-art, or regulatory minimum). The audit test is simple: can you show an auditor that the APS was written before any data was collected? A study run with targets adjusted after seeing the data is a classic non-conformity finding.

21. Typical validation workflow (in-house, modified, or full establishment)

When the method has no prior IVD validation to lean on — it is in-house, modified, or being established from scratch — the work follows a standard three-step sequence. Each step builds on the previous one: you need CV% before you can calculate sigma, and you need Bias% to complete the picture.

1
Precision study — measure CV%

Run your method repeatedly under controlled conditions to quantify random error. Use intermediate precision (day-to-day) rather than repeatability for a realistic CV%.

  • Quick check — 20+ patient samples or QC material over multiple days (informal, not a CLSI protocol) → use the Precision Monitor tool
  • Full precision study (EP05-A3) — 20 days × 2 runs × 2 replicates = 80 measurements per level → use the Precision Study (EP05 / EP15) tool (EP05 validation mode)
  • Verification (EP15-A3) — 5 days × 5 replicates = 25 measurements per level → use the Precision Study (EP05 / EP15) tool (EP15 verification mode)
Output: CV% (total intermediate precision)
2
Bias study — measure systematic error

Compare your method against a known reference to quantify systematic error (trueness).

  • CRM (gold standard) — measure a certified reference material with a known assigned value. Most reliable estimate of true bias.
  • EQA — compare your result against peer group mean. Less reliable: peer consensus is not a true reference value.
  • Method comparison — compare your method against a reference method using patient samples (CLSI EP09). Gives a method-derived bias estimate.
Output: Mean Bias% (absolute value)
3
Total Error & Sigma — combine and evaluate

Enter CV% + Bias% + TAE% (from EFLM or your specification) to calculate:

  • TE = |Bias%| + 1.65 × CV% — does total error fit within the allowable limit?
  • Sigma metric = (TAE% − |Bias%|) / CV% — how much margin does your method have?
  • QC strategy — sigma ≥ 6: minimal QC needed; sigma 4–6: standard Westgard rules; sigma < 4: tighten QC or improve method.
Output: TE%, sigma metric, QC rule recommendation

22. EP05 — Precision Study Protocol (CLSI EP05-A3)

CLSI EP05 defines the standard protocol for evaluating the precision of a quantitative measurement procedure. Its purpose is to estimate repeatability (Sw) and intermediate precision (St) under controlled, real-world conditions before a method is released for clinical use.

Study design

The recommended design is 2 levels × 2 replicates × 20 days:

  • 2 concentration levels — typically a low-normal and a high-pathological concentration, ideally at clinical decision points
  • 2 replicates per run — measured in the same analytical run, back-to-back
  • 20 days — spread over at least 20 working days (not necessarily consecutive)

This gives 40 measurements per level. A shorter design (5 days × 4 replicates) is allowed for verification studies (EP15).

What is estimated

  • Sw (within-run SD) — purely from the duplicate pairs within each run. Captures random error within a single analytical session
  • Sb (between-run SD) — variation attributable to run-to-run differences (reagent lots, calibration drift, operator effects)
  • St (total/intermediate precision SD) — combines within-run and between-run: St = √(Sw² + Sb²)
Why 20 days? Precision is estimated under routine conditions — including normal day-to-day sources of variation such as reagent changes, different operators, maintenance, and environmental drift. Fewer days underestimates this variation.

Why nested ANOVA — not one-way or two-way?

You may already know one-way ANOVA (one grouping factor, e.g. compare means across days) and two-way ANOVA (two crossed factors, e.g. day × operator — every combination measured). EP05 uses neither. It uses nested ANOVA, and the reason matters:

DesignStructureProblem for EP05
One-way ANOVAGroups days onlyLumps within-run and between-run variation together — you cannot separate Sw from Sb
Two-way ANOVADay × run, fully crossedRequires the same run labels to mean the same thing across days — they don't. "Run 1" on day 1 is not the same run as "Run 1" on day 3
Nested ANOVAReplicates inside runs, runs inside days— correct model. Runs are unique within each day; replicates are unique within each run

The nested structure reflects reality: each run is a distinct analytical session that happens to fall on a particular day. The calculation partitions variance into within-run (Sw) — from the replicate pairs — and between-run (Sb) — from how run means scatter around the grand mean. This is the only way to get both components separately, which is exactly what EP05 requires.

In practice: you never need to run nested ANOVA yourself — the EP05 tool does it automatically when you enter your data. Understanding the structure just helps you design the study correctly: replicates must be measured within the same run, and runs must be genuinely separate analytical sessions.

Acceptance criteria

Compare your CV% against the allowable imprecision derived from your performance specification:

  • Biological variation (desirable): allowable CV% ≤ 0.5 × CVi
  • EFLM / manufacturer claim: stated CV% from the IFU or EFLM database
  • Sigma-based: CV% ≤ (TAE − |Bias|) / desired sigma level

23. EP15 — Precision Verification Protocol (CLSI EP15-A3)

EP15 is a verification protocol — not a full evaluation. Its purpose is to confirm that a commercially available method performs in your laboratory as the manufacturer has claimed. It is shorter than EP05 and is the standard approach for clinical laboratories implementing a new analyser or reagent system.

  • Minimum 2 concentration levels — low and high, ideally at clinical decision points
  • 3–5 replicates per run

Acceptance criterion

The observed CV% must be within the manufacturer's stated CV% claim, verified using a chi-squared test on the ratio of observed variance to claimed variance:

A result that barely fails should always be evaluated against both the CI and the clinical impact, not the point estimate alone.

EP15 does not replace EP05. If no manufacturer claim exists, or if you are modifying the method, or if the method is developed in-house, a full validation (EP05 or equivalent) is required.

24. EP09 — Method Comparison (bias against another method)

CLSI EP09 defines the standard protocol for comparing two measurement procedures using patient samples. Its goal is to estimate systematic bias between methods and determine whether they are clinically interchangeable — i.e., whether a new method can replace an existing one without affecting patient care.

Study design

  • Minimum 40 patient samples — at least 40 paired results (some sources recommend 100 for robust regression)
  • Span the full clinical measurement range — samples should cover low, normal, and high concentrations including clinical decision levels
  • Native patient samples preferred — processed or stabilised materials may introduce matrix effects
  • Both methods measured on the same day — ideally within a short time window to minimise pre-analytical differences
  • Single measurement per sample per method — replicates are not required; EP09 uses sample-to-sample variation to assess bias, not within-sample precision

Statistical analysis

EP09 recommends a two-stage approach:

  1. Bland-Altman plot — visualise and quantify the magnitude and pattern of differences. Check for constant bias (horizontal band) vs proportional bias (differences increase with concentration)
  2. Regression analysis — Passing-Bablok regression is preferred (non-parametric, no distributional assumptions). Deming regression is acceptable when both methods have similar imprecision. OLS is not appropriate when both methods have random error

Bland-Altman assumptions

Bland-Altman assumes the differences between the two methods are approximately normally distributed. This matters because the limits of agreement (mean ± 1.96 × SD) rely on the normal distribution — if the differences are skewed or contain outliers, the 1.96 factor is no longer valid and the LoA will be misleading.

AssumptionWhy it mattersHow to check
Normality of differences LoA = mean ± 1.96 × SD requires normality. Skewed differences produce LoA that do not capture 95% of cases. Shapiro-Wilk test on the differences; histogram or Q-Q plot
Homoscedasticity A single SD is only meaningful if the spread of differences is constant across the range. A funnel shape (larger differences at higher concentrations) means the SD is understated at high values and overstated at low values. Bland-Altman plot — look for a funnel shape. If present, use log-transformed data or express differences as % of the mean (proportional Bland-Altman).
The method is fairly robust to mild departures from normality, especially with n > 40. The Shapiro-Wilk test in the tool tests the differences directly — if it fails, inspect the plot before reporting LoA at face value.

25. EP15 trueness — bias against a CRM NEW

CLSI EP15-A3 is primarily a precision verification protocol, but it also defines a standard design for trueness verification — confirming that a method's results agree with a certified reference material (CRM) whose assigned value is traceable to an SI unit or a reference measurement procedure. It is the formal way to answer the question: "Is my method correct in absolute terms?"

Study design

  • CRM with a certified assigned value — traceability and uncertainty must be stated on the certificate of analysis.
  • ≥ 2 concentration levels when CRMs are available at more than one level (e.g. low and high clinical decision points).
  • 5 days × 5 replicates per level (the EP15 5 × 5 design) — same structure as precision verification, which is why they are usually run together.
  • Single analyser, single reagent lot, single calibration during the study.

Statistical analysis

The mean of all measurements is compared against the certified value:

  • Observed bias = mean − certified value.
  • 95% CI on the bias is built from the intermediate-precision SD (from the same 5 × 5 runs) and the certificate's uncertainty.
  • Verdict: the method passes if the 95% CI on the observed bias contains zero and the point estimate is within the APS-defined allowable bias.

Pitfalls

  • Commutability — a non-commutable CRM shows apparent bias that does not exist for patient samples. Check the certificate for a commutability statement.
  • Method-specific CRMs (often immunoassay) are only valid on the assigned method family. Measuring on a different platform will show false bias.
  • Wrong concentration level — a CRM only tells you about bias at its own level. If your APS demands unbiased performance at multiple decision points, use multi-level CRMs or combine with EP09 method comparison.

26. EQA bias monitoring — bias against peer consensus NEW

Where EP09 compares against a specific alternative method and EP15 trueness compares against an absolute CRM, External Quality Assessment (EQA) compares against the consensus of many laboratories. It is the primary mechanism for ongoing bias monitoring in routine clinical laboratories and a mandatory part of ISO 15189 accreditation.

Governing standards

  • ISO/IEC 17043 — sets the competence requirements for proficiency-testing providers (sample homogeneity, statistical treatment, z-scores, reporting). Accredits the provider.
  • CLSI GP27-A3 — lab-side guidance on how to use PT data: interpreting reports, setting acceptance limits, investigating unacceptable results, and documenting corrective actions.
  • ISO 15189 §7.3.7.2 — requires the lab to participate in interlaboratory comparisons covering all accredited examinations.
  • National accreditation bodies (DANAK, SWEDAC, Norsk Akkreditering, FINAS, ISAC) enforce ongoing EQA participation as a condition of accreditation.

How an EQA round works

The provider distributes blind samples of unknown concentration to all participating laboratories. Each lab measures the samples under routine conditions and reports the results back. The provider then calculates a peer-group mean (or an assigned target value) and returns a report showing each lab's result and its deviation from the consensus.

  • Sample design is the provider's responsibility under ISO/IEC 17043 — the lab does not design the study, only runs the samples.
  • Frequency varies by scheme — typically 4–6 rounds per year for clinical chemistry, more frequent for haematology and coagulation. Each round usually covers multiple analytes in the same samples.
  • Scoring is typically a z-score or a percentage deviation from the peer-group mean, compared against a pre-defined acceptance window.

Statistical analysis — what the lab does

The lab does not run a regression or a paired study. Instead, each round produces a single bias value per analyte:

  • Per-round bias = (your result − peer group mean) / peer group mean × 100%.
  • Trend over rounds — plot the per-round bias over time. A consistent offset from zero suggests true systematic bias; scatter around zero is normal between-lab variation.
  • Rolling mean over several rounds is more reliable than any single round.
  • Verdict per round — the provider's report calls each result acceptable or unacceptable based on the scheme's pre-defined window.

Pitfalls

  • Peer consensus is not a true value — a whole peer group can share the same bias (e.g. all labs using the same reagent lot). EQA agreement with peers does not guarantee trueness.
  • Commutability of EQA material — especially for immunoassays, EQA samples may be non-commutable. Apparent bias may be a matrix artefact.
  • Peer-group stratification — most schemes break results down by method/instrument group; compare against the right peer group (your method family), not the all-lab average.
  • Single-round investigations — do not over-react to a single unacceptable round; investigate over 2–3 rounds to distinguish random scatter from real drift.

25. Reference Intervals — EP28 BETA REVIEW

A reference interval (commonly called "normal range") defines the central 95% of results expected from a reference population — individuals selected by defined inclusion and exclusion criteria. EP28 avoids the word "healthy" because it is subjective. Instead, the standard uses a formal process:

Defining the reference group — inclusion and exclusion criteria

The quality of a reference interval depends entirely on the quality of the reference group. EP28 requires that every individual is screened against pre-defined criteria before their sample is included. "Healthy" is not a self-reported status — it must be verified.

Example — establishing a fasting glucose reference interval: You recruit 120 adults aged 18–65. Each must: have no diabetes diagnosis, take no glucose-lowering medication, fast ≥ 8 hours, have no acute illness, and have a confirmed HbA1c < 42 mmol/mol. Blood is drawn between 07:00–09:00 after 15 minutes seated rest. Only then is the glucose value included in the reference interval calculation.

"Healthy" must be verified, not assumed. A reference individual is someone who has been screened against all defined criteria — not someone who simply says they are healthy. A single unrecognised diabetic in a group of 120 can shift the upper reference limit significantly.

Why 120 samples?

The reference interval covers the central 95% of results (from the 2.5th to the 97.5th percentile). But how precisely have you estimated those two limits? That precision is expressed as a 90% confidence interval around each limit — a separate concept from the 95% interval itself. With fewer samples, these confidence intervals become very wide. CLSI EP28 requires 120 as the minimum for the non-parametric approach because this is the smallest n that gives acceptably narrow 90% CIs around both limits.

Reference interval (95%) 90% CI around each limit Estimated limit
Non-parametric reference interval Lower limit = rank position ⌊0.025 × (n + 1)⌋ of sorted data
Upper limit = rank position ⌈0.975 × (n + 1)⌉ of sorted data

Parametric reference interval (if data are Gaussian) Lower limit = mean − 1.96 × SD
Upper limit = mean + 1.96 × SD

Non-parametric vs. parametric

  • Non-parametric (rank-based) — no assumption about data distribution. Simply sort values and pick the 2.5th and 97.5th percentile positions. Recommended by EP28 as the default method.
  • Parametric (mean ± 1.96 SD) — assumes data follow a Gaussian (normal) distribution. Only use if normality is confirmed (e.g., Shapiro-Wilk test). Can be applied to transformed data (e.g., log-transformed) if the original data are skewed.

Outlier detection

Outliers in reference data can distort the calculated interval. EP28 recommends screening for outliers before calculating limits:

  • Tukey's method — values below Q1 − 1.5 × IQR or above Q3 + 1.5 × IQR are flagged. Robust and widely used.
  • Dixon's Q test — tests the most extreme value against the range of the data. Better for small sample sizes (n < 30). Only tests one outlier at a time.
  • Always investigate flagged outliers clinically before removing — they may represent unrecognised pathology in a "healthy" individual.
Outlier removal is not automatic. If a value is flagged, check whether the individual truly met the inclusion criteria (healthy, fasting, no medication, etc.). Blindly removing statistical outliers can narrow the interval and increase false-positive flags in routine use.

Partitioning — when to split by subgroups

Many analytes differ by age, sex, or ethnicity. EP28 recommends testing for statistically and clinically significant differences between subgroups. If the difference is large enough, separate reference intervals should be established for each subgroup.

  • Common partitions: male/female, paediatric/adult/geriatric, pre-/post-menopausal
  • Each partition requires its own 120 reference individuals
  • If the difference between groups is < 0.25 × combined range, a single interval may suffice

Verification with 20 samples

When adopting a published or manufacturer's reference interval, EP28 allows a simplified verification:

  1. Collect samples from 20 healthy individuals matching the target population.
  2. Analyse all samples with your method on your instrument.
  3. Count how many results fall outside the claimed reference interval.
  4. If ≤ 2 out of 20 are outside → the interval is verified (acceptable for your laboratory).
  5. If ≥ 3 out of 20 are outside → the interval is not transferable. Investigate bias or establish your own interval.
Example: A reagent manufacturer claims glucose reference interval 4.1–5.9 mmol/L. You test 20 healthy volunteers: 18 fall within 4.1–5.9, 2 are outside → interval verified. If 4 are outside → interval not verified — check for systematic bias on your analyser.
Key insight: Reference intervals are not just about statistics — they depend on the reference population, pre-analytical conditions (fasting, posture, time of day), and the analytical method. An interval established on one platform cannot be assumed valid on another without verification.

NEWWhen the population reference interval is not enough — recall the Index of Individuality

Everything above assumes that comparing a patient's value to a population-based interval is clinically informative. For some analytes, it isn't. Recall the Index of Individuality (II) = CVi / CVg from : when II < 0.6, each person sits inside their own narrow band within the population range, so a value can change substantially for that individual while still appearing "normal" against the population RI.

For low-II analytes — CRP, ferritin, troponin, and others — the population RI is structurally insufficient. The more meaningful reference is the patient's own baseline, combined with the Reference Change Value (RCV) to decide whether a change is real. A "normal" troponin can still represent a clinically important rise from that patient's prior value.

NEWComparing a patient result to the RI — what MU adds

The RI is a pair of numbers (lower and upper limit), but the patient result is also uncertain — every measured value carries a measurement uncertainty (MU): a 95% interval around the reported number expressing how precisely the true value is known. ISO 15189 §7.3.4 requires the laboratory to estimate MU for every quantitative result, and to make it available when the clinician needs to interpret a borderline value. (MU is covered in depth alongside TAE later in the module — see ; this subsection covers the minimum needed to use it at the bedside.)

What MU adds to RI interpretation: when a patient result sits close to a reference limit, the strict GUM-aware reading is "the result is above the RI only if (result − U) > RI upper limit". In other words, the result must be above the limit by more than its own measurement uncertainty before you can call it unambiguously abnormal.

Worked example:

  • Patient potassium result: 5.7 mmol/L, with measurement uncertainty U = 0.3 mmol/L (so the 95% interval is 5.4–6.0 mmol/L)
  • Reference interval upper limit: 5.5 mmol/L
  • Naive read: 5.7 > 5.5 → "elevated potassium" → clinical action
  • GUM-aware read: result − U = 5.7 − 0.3 = 5.4, which is not above 5.5 → the result could plausibly be inside the RI; consider repeating before acting on it

Most clinicians do not do the GUM-aware comparison — they read 5.7 as "above 5.5" and act on it. But the lab is required to have MU available for exactly this kind of decision, particularly when:

  • The result sits near a clinical decision threshold (RI limit, critical value, treatment cut-off)
  • A clinician asks for confirmation that a borderline result is real
  • An audit reviews how the lab supports clinicians making decisions on edge-case results

NEWComparing two consecutive results — what RCV adds

The same logic extends to comparing two consecutive results on the same patient. The clinical question shifts from "is this single result above/below the limit?" to "has this patient changed in a real way?" The right tool here is the Reference Change Value (RCV) — the smallest difference between two consecutive measurements that can be considered statistically real (rather than just noise).

Reference Change Value: RCV = 2.77 × √(CVa² + CVi²)

RCV combines analytical noise (CVa, your method's CV%) and within-person biological noise (CVi, from the EFLM database) into a single threshold. The factor 2.77 comes from √2 × 1.96: the √2 because two independent measurements each contribute their own variation (so the variance doubles), and 1.96 is the two-tailed 95% z-score (a change can go up or down).

Worked example — creatinine:

  • Patient creatinine: today 88 µmol/L, last week 75 µmol/L → observed change of 17%
  • Method CVa = 3%, creatinine CVi ≈ 5.9% (EFLM)
  • RCV = 2.77 × √(3² + 5.9²) = 2.77 × √43.81 ≈ 18.3%
  • Observed change (17%) is less than RCV (18.3%) → the change is not statistically significant; it could plausibly be noise. Confirm with a repeat sample before acting on it.

RCV is especially important for low-II analytes (CRP, ferritin, troponin) where the population RI is structurally insufficient and changes within an individual carry the clinical meaning. Together, the MU+RI tool above and RCV here cover the two most common bedside interpretation questions: "is this single result above/below the limit?" (MU+RI) and "is this change real?" (RCV).

26. Categorical Method Comparison — Kappa and McNemar

When two methods report a qualitative (pos/neg) result rather than a measured value, you cannot use Bland-Altman or regression. Instead, agreement is evaluated using a 2×2 contingency table and two complementary statistics: Cohen's Kappa and McNemar's test.

The 2×2 table

Method B: PositiveMethod B: Negative
Method A: Positivea — both positive (agree)b — A+, B− (discordant)
Method A: Negativec — A−, B+ (discordant)d — both negative (agree)
  • a + d = concordant pairs — used by Kappa
  • b + c = discordant pairs — used by McNemar

Cohen's Kappa — overall concordance

Kappa measures agreement corrected for chance. Two methods with very different positive rates can agree frequently by chance alone — Kappa removes that component.

κ = (Po − Pe) / (1 − Pe)

  • Po = observed agreement = (a + d) / N
  • Pe = expected agreement by chance = [(a+b)(a+c) + (c+d)(b+d)] / N²
κ valueInterpretation (Landis & Koch 1977)
< 0.00Poor — less than chance
0.00–0.20Slight
0.21–0.40Fair
0.41–0.60Moderate
0.61–0.80Substantial
0.81–1.00Almost perfect
Important: Kappa is sensitive to prevalence. A low proportion of positives can produce a low κ even with high observed agreement. Always report Po (observed agreement %) alongside κ.

McNemar's test — systematic tilt

McNemar's test uses only the discordant pairs (b and c) to ask: are disagreements symmetric, or does one method systematically call more positives than the other?

H₀: b = c (no systematic bias)  |  H₁: b ≠ c (one method biased)

  • When b + c ≥ 25: χ² = (|b − c| − 1)² / (b + c) with continuity correction
  • When b + c < 25: exact binomial test (more accurate for small discordant counts)
  • p ≥ α: no systematic bias — disagreements are random
  • p < α, b > c: Method A calls more positives — may indicate lower specificity or genuine over-detection
  • p < α, c > b: Method B calls more positives
Key principle: Kappa and McNemar answer different questions and must be read together. High κ + significant McNemar = systematic but small bias. Low κ + non-significant McNemar = random but frequent disagreement.

Application: qualitative stability studies

For qualitative stability studies, the same panel of known samples is tested at T0 (fresh reference) and at each storage time point (T1, T3, T6, T12 months). Each time point produces one 2×2 table — run the full Kappa + McNemar analysis at each.

  • Plot κ vs time — a declining κ indicates degrading agreement as material ages
  • Track McNemar direction — significant tilt usually means the aged material misses positives (loss of sensitivity)
  • The functional expiry is the last time point at which κ ≥ your pre-defined criterion AND McNemar p ≥ α
  • Pre-define acceptance criteria before the study (e.g. κ ≥ 0.80 and McNemar p ≥ 0.05)

27. Stability Studies — CLSI EP25-A

Stability testing is conceptually separate from analytical performance. All other sections in this document evaluate the method — assuming the material is stable. Stability testing evaluates the material — assuming the method is stable. A stability result (e.g. 8% change at T6) is a material signal, not an analytical error. You cannot apply sigma metrics or compare stability % change against your TAE. See the dedicated Stability & Time section for the full conceptual guide and tools.

A stability study evaluates whether a reagent, test kit, or biological material maintains its intended performance over time under defined storage conditions. The goal is to determine functional expiry — the point at which performance is no longer acceptable.

Study design principles

  • Panel of samples: use a fixed panel covering the clinically relevant range (typically 10–20 samples for quantitative; 20–60 for qualitative)
  • T0 = fresh reference: all samples are measured at baseline before any aging begins
  • Time points: typically T0, T1, T3, T6, T9, T12 months — select based on the intended shelf life
  • Same panel at each time point: paired design maximises sensitivity to detect real changes
  • Pre-define acceptance criteria: decide the criterion before collecting data — do not change it after seeing results

Quantitative stability — % change from T0

For analytes that produce a measured value, performance is tracked as % change from the T0 baseline.

% change at Ti for sample j = (Ti,j − T0j) / T0j × 100

For the full sample panel at each time point:

  • Calculate mean % change and its 95% CI (paired t-test against 0)
  • Apply acceptance criterion: commonly ±10% (CLSI EP25-A general recommendation) or ±5% for high-precision analytes
  • A time point passes if the mean % change is within the criterion
  • The functional expiry is the last passing time point

Trend analysis

Regression of mean % change vs time (months) gives the drift rate (% per month). A significant slope indicates progressive degradation. Use the regression to extrapolate when the material would cross the acceptance limit — but note this is indicative only beyond measured time points.

28. Qualitative Limit of Detection — EP17 BETA

Qualitative and semi-quantitative methods (e.g., rapid antigen tests, PCR-based assays, lateral flow immunoassays) report a binary result — positive (detected) or negative (not detected). The Limit of Detection (LoD) is the lowest analyte concentration at which the method reliably detects the target.

What LoD means for qualitative methods

CLSI EP17-A2 defines LoD as the concentration at which the method detects the analyte in a specified proportion of replicates — typically ≥ 95% of the time. Unlike quantitative LoD (defined via precision at low concentrations), qualitative LoD is based on a hit rate: the fraction of positive results at each concentration level.

Hit rate testing — the basic approach

  1. Prepare a series of concentration levels spanning the expected detection limit (e.g., 5–8 levels).
  2. Test each level with multiple replicates (at least 20 per level recommended; more gives tighter confidence intervals).
  3. Record the number of positive results at each level.
  4. Calculate the hit rate: Hit rate = positives / total replicates × 100%
  5. The LoD is the lowest concentration at which the hit rate meets or exceeds the target (e.g., 95%).
Hit rate Hit rate (%) = (number of positive results / total replicates) × 100

LoD criterion LoD = lowest concentration where hit rate ≥ target (typically 95%)

Probit analysis (optional refinement)

Rather than picking the first level that crosses the 95% threshold, probit regression fits a sigmoid curve to the hit rate data. This models the probability of detection as a function of concentration and interpolates the exact concentration at which the predicted hit rate equals the target. Probit analysis:

  • Provides a more precise LoD estimate than simple inspection of hit rates
  • Produces a confidence interval around the LoD
  • Works best with 5+ concentration levels and ≥ 20 replicates per level
  • Requires at least some levels below and above the target hit rate for reliable curve fitting

Confidence intervals

Each hit rate is a proportion, so its uncertainty is estimated using a binomial confidence interval (e.g., Clopper-Pearson exact method). With only 20 replicates and 19 positives:

Example: 19/20 = 95% hit rate. But the 95% CI is approximately 75–100%. With 60 replicates and 57 positives: 95% hit rate with 95% CI ≈ 86–99%. More replicates → narrower CI → more confident that you have truly reached the LoD.

Study design considerations

  • Blank / negative samples — always include a zero-concentration level to confirm specificity (false positive rate).
  • Concentration spacing — use closely spaced levels near the expected LoD for precise estimation. Widely spaced levels may miss the true LoD.
  • Matrix — use clinically relevant sample matrices (serum, urine, swab, etc.) rather than buffer. Matrix effects can shift the LoD.
  • Replicates — EP17 recommends a minimum of 20 replicates per level, but 40–60 gives much better precision.
Common pitfall: Testing only 3–5 replicates per level. With so few replicates, the difference between 80% and 100% hit rate is just 1 result — the confidence interval is too wide to draw meaningful conclusions.

29. ROC & Diagnostic Accuracy REVIEW

A Receiver Operating Characteristic (ROC) curve evaluates how well a quantitative test discriminates between two clinical states — typically disease-positive and disease-negative. It is central to analytical performance because it answers: at what cut-off does this measurement become a useful diagnostic tool?

The 2×2 decision table

At any chosen cut-off, test results are classified into four categories:

Disease +Disease −
Test +True Positive (TP)False Positive (FP)
Test −False Negative (FN)True Negative (TN)

Key performance metrics

  • Sensitivity (TPR) = TP / (TP + FN) — proportion of true positives correctly identified. High sensitivity → few missed cases.
  • Specificity (TNR) = TN / (TN + FP) — proportion of true negatives correctly identified. High specificity → few false alarms.
  • PPV (Positive Predictive Value) = TP / (TP + FP) — probability that a positive result is truly positive. Depends on prevalence.
  • NPV (Negative Predictive Value) = TN / (TN + FN) — probability that a negative result is truly negative. Depends on prevalence.
PPV and NPV depend on prevalence. A test with 95% sensitivity and 95% specificity has PPV = 50% when prevalence is 5%, but PPV = 95% when prevalence is 50%. Always interpret predictive values in the context of the population being tested.

The ROC curve

The ROC curve plots sensitivity (y-axis) vs 1 − specificity (x-axis) across all possible cut-off values. Each point on the curve represents one threshold. The curve shows the trade-off: moving the cut-off to catch more positives (higher sensitivity) inevitably increases false positives (lower specificity).

  • A perfect test passes through the top-left corner (100% sensitivity, 100% specificity) — AUC = 1.0
  • A useless test follows the diagonal — AUC = 0.5 (no better than coin-flip)
  • The further the curve bows towards the top-left, the better the discriminatory power

AUC — Area Under the Curve

The AUC summarises the overall discriminatory ability of the test in a single number between 0.5 and 1.0. It can be interpreted as: the probability that a randomly chosen disease-positive individual has a higher test value than a randomly chosen disease-negative individual.

AUCInterpretation
0.90 – 1.00Excellent discrimination
0.80 – 0.90Good discrimination
0.70 – 0.80Fair discrimination
0.60 – 0.70Poor discrimination
0.50 – 0.60Fail — no useful discrimination

Choosing an optimal cut-off

The "best" cut-off depends on the clinical context:

  • Youden's index — maximises (sensitivity + specificity − 1). Gives the point on the ROC curve furthest from the diagonal. A balanced approach.
  • High sensitivity priority — for screening tests where missing a case is dangerous (e.g., cancer screening). Accept more false positives.
  • High specificity priority — for confirmatory tests where false positives have serious consequences (e.g., unnecessary surgery). Accept more false negatives.
  • Cost-based — if you can quantify the cost of a false positive vs. a false negative, the optimal cut-off minimises total cost.
Example: A biomarker for sepsis has AUC = 0.88. At Youden's optimal cut-off of 12.5 ng/mL: sensitivity = 85%, specificity = 80%. But for ICU screening, you might lower the cut-off to 8 ng/mL (sensitivity 95%, specificity 60%) — missing sepsis is worse than running extra confirmatory tests.

Comparing two ROC curves

When comparing two biomarkers or two methods, you can test whether their AUCs are significantly different using the DeLong test. This accounts for the correlation between the two curves when they are measured on the same patients.

Logistic regression and combined markers

When a single analyte has moderate discrimination, logistic regression can combine multiple analytes into a single predicted probability. The ROC curve of the combined model often outperforms any individual marker. The tool in this app supports entering multiple analytes and building a combined ROC curve.

Key insight: AUC tells you how good a test can be — but clinical usefulness depends on the cut-off you choose, the prevalence in your population, and the consequences of errors. A test with AUC = 0.85 can be clinically useless if the prevalence is very low (PPV collapses) or if there is no actionable treatment for detected cases.

30. Antibiotic Susceptibility Agreement — EUCAST/CLSI BETA REVIEW

In clinical microbiology, a new antimicrobial susceptibility testing (AST) method is validated by comparing it against a reference method (typically broth microdilution). Unlike quantitative method comparison, the outcome is a categorical classification: Susceptible (S), Intermediate / Susceptible-Increased exposure (I), or Resistant (R).

The 3×3 agreement matrix

Results from the test method and the reference method are cross-tabulated in a 3×3 matrix. Diagonal cells represent agreement; off-diagonal cells represent discrepancies classified by clinical severity:

Error typeDefinitionClinical impact
Very Major Error (VME)Reference = R, Test = SMost dangerous — a resistant organism is reported as susceptible. Patient may receive ineffective treatment.
Major Error (ME)Reference = S, Test = RSusceptible organism reported as resistant — patient denied effective, possibly first-line therapy.
Minor Error (mE)Either method reports I when the other reports S or RLess critical — treatment may still be appropriate but interpretation differs.

Key metrics

  • Category Agreement (CA) — proportion of isolates where both methods agree on S/I/R. Target: ≥ 90%.
  • Essential Agreement (EA) — proportion of MIC results within ±1 dilution of the reference. Applies only when MICs are available. Target: ≥ 90%.
  • VME rate — calculated as VME / total Reference-R isolates. Target: ≤ 3%.
  • ME rate — calculated as ME / total Reference-S isolates. Target: ≤ 3%.
  • mE rate — calculated as mE / total isolates. Target: ≤ 10% (less stringent).
VME and ME rates use different denominators. VME is divided by the number of reference-R isolates, ME by the number of reference-S isolates — not total N. This means even a single VME can produce a high rate if few resistant isolates are tested.

Study design considerations

  • CLSI recommends testing at least 100 isolates per species–drug combination, with a mix of S, I, and R organisms.
  • If resistant isolates are rare, the VME rate becomes unreliable — a single misclassification from 5 R isolates = 20% VME rate.
  • For disk diffusion vs. broth microdilution: classify MIC results using the same breakpoint table (EUCAST or CLSI) applied to both methods.
  • Report results per antibiotic and per organism — aggregate rates can mask species-specific problems.
Example: 100 isolates tested. Reference: 50 S, 10 I, 40 R. Test method: 1 VME (R→S), 2 ME (S→R), 3 mE. CA = 94/100 = 94% ✓ · VME = 1/40 = 2.5% ✓ · ME = 2/50 = 4.0% ✗ (exceeds 3%). Even though overall agreement is high, the ME rate flags a systematic problem — investigate which drug–organism pairs are affected.
EUCAST vs CLSI breakpoints: Both organisations define S/I/R categories but may use different MIC or zone-diameter cut-offs. Always state which breakpoint table you applied — mixing tables invalidates the comparison.
