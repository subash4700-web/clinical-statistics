1. Why accurate results matter

Every clinical decision — diagnosis, treatment, monitoring — depends on a laboratory result. When a clinician reads "potassium 6.2 mmol/L," they act on it: they may cancel surgery, administer emergency treatment, or order repeat testing. If that result is wrong, the patient may receive unnecessary treatment or, worse, miss treatment they need.

What happens when results are wrong?

Error typeClinical consequenceExample
False highUnnecessary treatment, delayed procedures, patient anxietyPotassium falsely elevated due to haemolysis → patient receives insulin/glucose they don't need
False lowMissed diagnosis, delayed treatmentTroponin falsely low → AMI missed → patient sent home
ImpreciseUnreliable monitoring, inconsistent follow-up decisionsHbA1c varies ±8% between runs → clinician can't tell if diabetes treatment is working
Consistently biasedSystematic misclassification of patientsGlucose reads 0.5 mmol/L too high → healthy patients flagged as pre-diabetic
The laboratory's responsibility: You do not just produce numbers — you produce clinical decisions. Every imprecision and every bias translates directly into a risk to patient care. Analytical performance evaluation exists to quantify that risk and ensure it stays within clinically acceptable limits.

This module covers the concepts and tools you need to evaluate whether your method is fit for clinical use. We start with understanding where variation comes from, then move through the methods for measuring and controlling it.

2. Types of variation — biological and analytical

Every laboratory result is a combination of two independent sources of variation:

SourceWhat it isCan you control it?
Biological variation (from the patient)The patient's own analyte levels fluctuate naturally — from hour to hour, day to day, and between individuals. This is not error; it is physiology.No — it is inherent to the patient. But you must understand it to set meaningful performance limits.
Analytical variation (from the instrument)The measurement system introduces its own variation: pipetting, reagent stability, detector noise, calibration drift. This is the error your laboratory can and must control.Yes — through quality control, calibration, and method validation.
The total variation in a result is the sum of biological and analytical variation. A clinician cannot tell them apart — they only see the final number. Your job is to ensure that the analytical component is small enough that it does not obscure the clinical signal.

Signal vs noise

If a patient's glucose rises from 5.0 to 5.4 mmol/L, is that a real clinical change or just normal biological fluctuation combined with measurement imprecision? The signal is the true clinical change you want to detect; the noise is all the random variation (biological + analytical) that obscures it. If the noise is larger than the signal, you cannot tell them apart.

Smaller biological variation → stricter analytical requirements. An analyte with a narrow CVi (e.g. sodium, CVi ≈ 0.6%) leaves almost no room for analytical error — even a 1% CV from the instrument would dominate the total variation. In contrast, an analyte with wide CVi (e.g. CRP, CVi ≈ 42%) is far more tolerant of analytical imprecision because the patient's own variation is already large. This is why the EFLM biological variation database gives different allowable CV% for every analyte — the limits are derived directly from CVi.

3. Biological variation — CVi and CVg explained

Biological variation describes the natural fluctuation in analyte concentrations in the human body. It has two components:

CVi — Intra-individual biological variation

CVi (also written CVI) is the variation within a single person over time, when they are in a stable physiological state. Even a completely healthy person's blood analytes fluctuate from day to day and hour to hour due to normal biological rhythms, hydration, posture, and circadian effects.

CVi example — Potassium: CVi ≈ 4.6% → potassium fluctuates by ≈ 4.6% within the same person week to week

CVi is measured in studies where the same individual is sampled repeatedly over time, under controlled conditions. The EFLM Biological Variation Database publishes CVi estimates for hundreds of analytes.

CVg — Inter-individual biological variation

CVg (also written CVG) is the variation between different people. Even in a healthy population, people have different set-points for the same analyte. CVg is typically larger than CVi.

CVg example — Potassium: CVg ≈ 5.6% → different healthy people have potassium levels that differ by ≈ 5.6% between individuals

Why CVi and CVg matter for analytical quality

CVi and CVg are used to derive allowable limits for your analytical method — this is about setting quality goals for your analyser, not about interpreting individual patient results.

The reasoning behind the formulas:

  • Imprecision vs CVi: If your analytical CV% is large relative to CVi, analytical noise will obscure real biological signals — you cannot tell whether a change in a patient's result reflects true biology or measurement variation. The goal CVa ≤ 0.5 × CVi ensures your analytical noise is less than half the natural biological noise within a person.
  • Bias vs CVi + CVg: If your method has systematic bias, it shifts results away from the true value. If that shift is large relative to the combined biological variation (CVi and CVg), it can displace results incorrectly relative to the population reference range. The goal Bias ≤ 0.25 × √(CVi² + CVg²) keeps bias small enough not to distort clinical interpretation.

The EFLM/Westgard hierarchy of quality goals uses CVi and CVg to derive these specific, clinically meaningful allowable limits:

Allowable imprecision (desirable goal): CVa ≤ 0.5 × CVi
Allowable bias (desirable goal): Bias_a ≤ 0.25 × √(CVi² + CVg²)
Allowable Total Error (desirable goal): TAE_a ≤ 1.65 × CVa + Bias_a

The factor 0.5 for imprecision means: your analytical noise should be less than half the natural biological noise within a person. If it is, analytical imprecision does not meaningfully obscure biological signals.

The factor 0.25 for bias uses both CVi and CVg because bias shifts results relative to both the patient's own set-point and the population-based reference interval.

Three levels of biological variation goals

LevelImprecision (CVa)BiasWhen to use
Desirable0.5 × CVi0.25 × √(CVi² + CVg²)Target for most clinical analytes
Minimum0.75 × CVi0.375 × √(CVi² + CVg²)Acceptable if desirable is not achievable
Optimum0.25 × CVi0.125 × √(CVi² + CVg²)Highest clinical demand (e.g. therapeutic drug monitoring)
Index of Individuality (II) = CVi / CVg: When II < 0.6, population-based reference intervals are poor at detecting changes within an individual patient. In this case, individual baseline values (reference change values) are more clinically useful than population reference ranges. Low II analytes include CRP, ferritin, and troponin.

4. Sources of analytical error

Every measurement a laboratory makes contains some error. Understanding the type of error is the first step in knowing what to do about it.

Error typeWhat it meansExample
Random errorUnpredictable variation around the true value. Changes direction each measurement.Pipetting variation, detector noise
Systematic error (bias)Consistent shift in one direction. Results are always too high or always too low.Mis-calibration, reagent lot change

5. Pre-analytical, analytical, and post-analytical errors

A laboratory result passes through three phases before it reaches the clinician. Errors can occur at any stage:

PhaseWhat happensError examplesFrequency
Pre-analyticalEverything before the sample reaches the analyser: patient preparation, sample collection, transport, centrifugation, storageHaemolysis, wrong tube, insufficient fasting, delayed transport, incorrect labelling~60–70% of all lab errors
AnalyticalThe measurement itself: calibration, reagent performance, instrument precision, interferenceCalibration drift, reagent degradation, matrix effects, pipetting imprecision~10–15% of all lab errors
Post-analyticalEverything after the measurement: result validation, reporting, interpretation, communicationTranscription errors, wrong reference interval applied, delayed reporting, misinterpretation~20–25% of all lab errors

This module focuses on the analytical phase — the part where you can quantify and control error using statistical methods. When we say "error" in the following sections, we mean analytical error unless stated otherwise.

Even though analytical errors account for the smallest proportion, they are the most systematically measurable — and the only ones you can evaluate before a method goes into clinical use.

6. Why we do controls

A laboratory cannot measure every patient sample twice. Instead, it runs control materials — samples with a known or expected value — alongside patient samples. If the control result is within acceptable limits, the patient results measured in the same run are considered reliable.

QuestionControl typeWhat it tells you
Is my method precise today?Internal QC (IQC) — same material measured every runWhether within-run and between-run variation is stable over time
Is my method accurate compared to others?External QA (EQA/EQAS) — unknown sample from a proficiency programWhether your result agrees with peer laboratories
Is my method true?Certified Reference Material (CRM) — value assigned by a reference methodWhether your result matches the "true" value
Analogy: IQC is like checking your bathroom scale every morning with a known weight — you detect if it drifts. EQA is like weighing yourself on a different scale at the doctor's office — you detect if your scale is systematically off compared to everyone else's.
Controls are not patient samples. A control material may behave differently from a real patient sample due to matrix effects (stabilisers, preservatives, animal-sourced proteins). A method can pass all QC checks and still produce biased results on patient samples.

7. CRM vs EQA — two ways to detect bias

Bias can only be measured if you know the true value. There are two main ways to establish this in the clinical laboratory:

Certified Reference Material (CRM)

A material with a known, certified assigned value — traceable to a reference method or SI units. You measure it on your analyser and compare your result to the certified value.

  • Advantage: Direct, objective comparison to a known true value.
  • Limitation: CRMs are expensive. Commutability must be verified — the CRM matrix should behave the same as real patient samples on your method.

External Quality Assessment (EQA)

Also called proficiency testing (e.g. DEKS in Denmark, UKNEQAS in the UK). The same sample is sent to all participating laboratories. Your result is compared against the peer group mean or an assigned target value.

  • Advantage: Independent external check. Reveals systematic bias that internal QC would never detect — because internal QC only tells you if you are consistent, not if you are correct.
  • Limitation: EQA materials are often lyophilized or stabilized. Their matrix may differ from real patient samples, so apparent bias may be a matrix effect rather than a true analytical problem.
Key difference: CRM gives you an absolute bias against a known true value. EQA gives you a relative bias compared to your peers. Both are valuable — use them together. A bias confirmed in both CRM and EQA is almost certainly real.

8. Matrix effects

The matrix is everything in a sample other than the analyte being measured — water, proteins, lipids, salts, stabilizers. Different matrices can affect how an instrument responds to the same concentration of analyte.

This is why a bias seen in EQA or with QC material does not always mean your method is wrong. The material's matrix may simply behave differently from real patient samples on your analyser.

Never conclude there is a bias based on a single material alone. Confirm bias using a commutable material — one whose matrix behaves like real patient samples on your method. Patient-based bias estimation (e.g. method comparison against a reference method) is the most reliable approach.

Precision can be studied using two types of material — and they answer different questions.

MaterialAdvantagesLimitations
Patient samples

QC / reference material

10. Precision — what it means and how to measure it

Precision describes how reproducible your measurements are — how close repeated measurements of the same sample are to each other. It does not tell you whether the results are correct, only whether they are consistent.

Precision is expressed as:

  • SD (Standard Deviation) — in the same units as the measurement. Useful for fixed-concentration QC material.
  • CV% (Coefficient of Variation) — SD expressed as a percentage of the mean. Allows comparison across different concentration levels and analytes.
CV% formula: CV% = (SD / Mean) × 100
Precision ≠ accuracy. A method can be very precise (low CV%) but systematically biased. Think of a scale that always reads 0.5 kg too high — consistent but wrong. Both precision and bias must be evaluated independently.

11. Repeatability, intermediate precision, and reproducibility

These three terms describe precision under different conditions. The conditions you vary during your study determine which type you are measuring.

🟢 Repeatability (within-run)
Same sample · same day · same run · same operator · same reagents · same calibration
This is the tightest precision you will ever achieve. It represents only random pipetting and detector variation.
🟡 Intermediate precision (within-laboratory)
Same laboratory · different days · possibly different operators, reagent lots, or calibrations
This is what real-world performance looks like. Day-to-day drift is captured here. This is what patients experience.
🔴 Reproducibility (between-laboratory)
Different laboratories · different instruments · different everything
Used in method standardisation and reference interval studies. Broadest variation.
Important: There is no single "intermediate precision." What you vary defines what you measure. If you vary days only, you get day-to-day intermediate precision. If you also vary operators and reagent lots, you get a broader measure. Always state what conditions were varied when reporting intermediate precision.

Intermediate precision is always ≥ repeatability because it includes all the within-run variation plus additional between-day sources.

12. Practical study design — the duplicate method

The simplest precision study is to measure the same sample many times in one run and calculate the SD. This gives you repeatability — but nothing more.

To capture real-world laboratory performance, you need to spread measurements across multiple days and runs. The formal protocol for this is CLSI EP05-A3:

CLSI EP05-A3 design: Measure at least 2 concentration levels over 20 days × 2 runs/day × 2 replicates/run = 80 measurements per level. This structured design allows nested ANOVA to partition within-run variation (Sw) from between-run variation (Sb).

What this gives you depends on how the days are spread:

DesignWhat you measureUse for
All measurements on one dayRepeatability only (Sw)Best-case precision estimate
Measurements spread across multiple daysIntermediate precision (St = √(Sw² + Sb²))Real-world performance estimate

The replicates within each run give you the within-run SD (Sw). The variation of run means around the grand mean gives you the between-run component (Sb). Combined, they give you the intermediate precision SD (St).

Recommendation: Always spread your precision study across multiple days. A one-day study will make your method look more precise than it actually is under real laboratory conditions.

13. Why you cannot add standard deviations

When combining two sources of variation, it is tempting to simply add their SDs. This is incorrect and will always overestimate the true combined precision.

The correct approach is to add the variances (SD²), then take the square root:

Wrong — do not do this: St = Sw + Sb
Correct — combine variances: St = √(Sw² + Sb²)

Example: Sw = 2.0 and Sb = 2.0

  • Wrong: 2.0 + 2.0 = 4.0
  • Correct: √(2.0² + 2.0²) = √8 = 2.83

In this example the incorrect addition gives a result 41% higher than the correct value. The overestimate depends on the relative size of the two components — it is largest when they are equal, and smaller when one dominates — but it is always an overestimate. This is not a small rounding difference — it is a fundamental statistical error.

Rule: Variances add. Standard deviations do not. This applies everywhere in statistics — not just to precision calculations.

14. Bias — systematic error

Bias is a consistent, directional difference between your method's results and the true value. Unlike random error, bias does not cancel out over many measurements — it shifts all results in the same direction.

Absolute bias: Bias = Your mean − True (assigned) value
Relative bias (%): Bias% = ((Your mean − True value) / True value) × 100

Common causes of bias

  • Incorrect calibration or calibrator lot change
  • Reagent lot-to-lot variation
  • Interfering substances in the sample matrix
  • Carry-over from high-concentration samples
  • Incorrect reference interval or assigned value
Bias vs imprecision: Bias shifts the entire distribution of results. Imprecision spreads it. A biased method gives results that cluster around the wrong value. An imprecise method gives results scattered around the right value. Total error combines both effects.

16. Total Allowable Error (TAE) and Total Error (TE)

A patient result contains both random and systematic error. Total Error is the maximum likely error in any single result, accounting for both bias and imprecision together.

Total Allowable Error (TAE)

The maximum total error that is clinically acceptable. It is a pre-defined limit based on biological variation or regulatory requirements. It represents the error budget — how much total error the clinical use of the result can tolerate.

Calculating Total Error (TE)

TE is calculated as:

Most common (CLSI/Westgard — 95% one-tailed): TE = |Bias%| + 1.65 × CV%
Alternative (97.5% one-tailed / stricter): TE = |Bias%| + 2.0 × CV%

Why two formulas? What z-score should you use?

The multiplier (1.65 or 2.0) is a z-score from the standard normal distribution. It defines what proportion of individual patient results you want to protect against exceeding the TAE:

MultiplierDistribution coverageWhat it meansWhen to use
1.65 95% one-tailed
(or 90% two-tailed)		 Up to 5% of results may exceed the TAE boundary due to random error — in the worst direction Standard CLSI/Westgard approach. Most published goals and validation criteria use this. Appropriate for routine chemistry where some error is tolerated.
2.0 ≈ 97.5% one-tailed
(or 95% two-tailed)		 Only 2.5% of results may exceed TAE — more protective against outliers More conservative laboratories, or analytes where a single erroneous result could cause serious patient harm (e.g. drug levels, coagulation, critical values).
1.96 97.5% one-tailed The formal 95% two-tailed z-score — sometimes used for consistency with other statistical tests Some ISO and EQA frameworks use 1.96; check your accreditation body's requirements.
The one-tailed vs two-tailed distinction: We use a one-tailed z-score because we care about errors in the worst direction (results that are too high OR too low — whichever pushes past the TAE limit). The bias is already included separately, so we only need to model how far random error can push a result in one additional direction. That is why 1.65 (not 1.96) corresponds to 95%.
Practical guidance: Use 1.65 when following CLSI EP15/Westgard protocols, biological variation goals, or Six Sigma calculations — this is the international standard. Use 2.0 if your laboratory policy or accreditation body requires greater assurance, or for high-stakes analytes. Be consistent — use the same multiplier throughout your validation and your TAE definition.

The method passes if: TE ≤ TAE

Interpreting TE: Bias and imprecision both consume part of the error budget. A method with large bias has less room for imprecision, and vice versa. A method can fail on total error even if both bias and imprecision individually appear acceptable — because they accumulate.

Bias1.5%

17. Setting TAE locally — approach and considerations

Yes — TAE can and sometimes should be set locally by the laboratory. While external sources (biological variation databases, regulatory bodies) provide a starting point, the laboratory often needs to adapt or define its own TAE based on clinical context and technical reality.

The hierarchy — start here before setting TAE locally

1st choice: Biological variation (EFLM database)
Directly links analytical quality to clinical impact. Desirable goals should be the default for most analytes.
2nd choice: Regulatory or external requirements
National accreditation (e.g. DANAK/ISO 15189 in Denmark), CLIA, or EQA scheme criteria. These are mandatory minimums, not aspirational targets.
3rd choice: State of the art / peer group performance
What the best comparable methods achieve. Used when biological variation goals are technically unachievable.

When should you set TAE locally?

  • No biological variation data exists for the analyte (e.g. novel markers, calculated parameters)
  • Biological variation goals are unachievable with any current instrument — state of the art becomes the practical ceiling
  • Clinical use is more demanding than the published general goal (e.g. therapeutic monitoring, critical decisions)
  • Analyte has a non-analytical intended use (e.g. a screening test where the threshold matters more than the exact value)
  • Your patient population is specific — e.g. neonatal, ICU, or paediatric samples where biological variation data may not apply

Factors to consider when setting TAE locally

FactorQuestion to askEffect on TAE
Clinical decision limitsAt what concentration does clinical action change? How large an error at that point is acceptable?Tighter TAE if the decision limit is clinically critical
Concentration levelIs the error requirement the same across the measurement range, or does it vary?May need separate TAE at low, medium, high concentrations
Within-patient monitoringAre results used to detect trends over time in the same patient?Tighter TAE is needed — analytical imprecision must not mask real biological change
Method capabilityWhat is the best CV% achievable with available instruments?TAE must be achievable — unachievable goals are not useful
Available reference materialsCan bias be verified against a certified reference? Or is EQA the only option?Affects certainty of bias estimate; uncertainty propagates to TAE
Regulatory floorWhat is the mandatory minimum? Local goals cannot be looser than this.TAE ≥ regulatory minimum; can be stricter
Practical recommendation: Start with the EFLM desirable goal. If it is achievable, use it. If not, check regulatory requirements and use state-of-the-art data. Document your rationale — ISO 15189 requires that quality goals are defined and justified. A locally set TAE with clear clinical reasoning is better than an unachievable external goal that is ignored in practice.

18. The Sigma metric

The Sigma metric is borrowed from Six Sigma industrial quality management. It expresses how many standard deviations of your method's imprecision fit within the allowable error budget after accounting for bias.

Sigma metric: Sigma = (TAE% − |Bias%|) / CV%

Sigma tells you how much room is left in the error budget for random variation:

SigmaPerformance levelInterpretation
≥ 6World classMinimal QC needed. Very rarely produces errors.
4 – 6ExcellentGood method. Standard QC is sufficient.
3 – 4AcceptableMore intensive QC required to catch errors.
< 3PoorMethod improvement needed. High QC burden to maintain safety.

Example

ParameterValue
TAE%6.0%
|Bias%|1.5%
CV%2.0%
Sigma = (6.0 − 1.5) / 2.02.25 Poor
Even if bias and imprecision individually pass their allowable limits, the Sigma metric can still be poor if they together consume too much of the error budget. A high Sigma means your QC can be simple. A low Sigma means you need to run more QC to protect patients.
Practical use: The Sigma metric is a single number that summarises the overall quality of your method. It can be used to design a rational QC strategy — methods with high Sigma need less QC, methods with low Sigma need more.

19. Categorical Method Comparison — Kappa and McNemar

When two methods report a qualitative (pos/neg) result rather than a measured value, you cannot use Bland-Altman or regression. Instead, agreement is evaluated using a 2×2 contingency table and two complementary statistics: Cohen's Kappa and McNemar's test.

The 2×2 table

Method B: PositiveMethod B: Negative
Method A: Positivea — both positive (agree)b — A+, B− (discordant)
Method A: Negativec — A−, B+ (discordant)d — both negative (agree)
  • a + d = concordant pairs — used by Kappa
  • b + c = discordant pairs — used by McNemar

Cohen's Kappa — overall concordance

Kappa measures agreement corrected for chance. Two methods with very different positive rates can agree frequently by chance alone — Kappa removes that component.

κ = (Po − Pe) / (1 − Pe)

  • Po = observed agreement = (a + d) / N
  • Pe = expected agreement by chance = [(a+b)(a+c) + (c+d)(b+d)] / N²

0.41–0.60Moderate
0.61–0.80Substantial
0.81–1.00Almost perfect
Important: Kappa is sensitive to prevalence. A low proportion of positives can produce a low κ even with high observed agreement. Always report Po (observed agreement %) alongside κ.

McNemar's test — systematic tilt

McNemar's test uses only the discordant pairs (b and c) to ask: are disagreements symmetric, or does one method systematically call more positives than the other?

H₀: b = c (no systematic bias)  |  H₁: b ≠ c (one method biased)

  • When b + c ≥ 25: χ² = (|b − c| − 1)² / (b + c) with continuity correction
  • When b + c < 25: exact binomial test (more accurate for small discordant counts)
  • p ≥ α: no systematic bias — disagreements are random
  • p < α, b > c: Method A calls more positives — may indicate lower specificity or genuine over-detection
  • p < α, c > b: Method B calls more positives
Key principle: Kappa and McNemar answer different questions and must be read together. High κ + significant McNemar = systematic but small bias. Low κ + non-significant McNemar = random but frequent disagreement.

Application: qualitative stability studies

For qualitative stability studies, the same panel of known samples is tested at T0 (fresh reference) and at each storage time point (T1, T3, T6, T12 months). Each time point produces one 2×2 table — run the full Kappa + McNemar analysis at each.

  • Plot κ vs time — a declining κ indicates degrading agreement as material ages
  • Track McNemar direction — significant tilt usually means the aged material misses positives (loss of sensitivity)
  • The functional expiry is the last time point at which κ ≥ your pre-defined criterion AND McNemar p ≥ α
  • Pre-define acceptance criteria before the study (e.g. κ ≥ 0.80 and McNemar p ≥ 0.05)

20. Stability Studies — CLSI EP25-A

Stability testing is conceptually separate from analytical performance. All other sections in this document evaluate the method — assuming the material is stable. Stability testing evaluates the material — assuming the method is stable. A stability result (e.g. 8% change at T6) is a material signal, not an analytical error. You cannot apply sigma metrics or compare stability % change against your TAE. See the dedicated Stability & Time section for the full conceptual guide and tools.

A stability study evaluates whether a reagent, test kit, or biological material maintains its intended performance over time under defined storage conditions. The goal is to determine functional expiry — the point at which performance is no longer acceptable.

Study design principles

  • Panel of samples: use a fixed panel covering the clinically relevant range (typically 10–20 samples for quantitative; 20–60 for qualitative)
  • T0 = fresh reference: all samples are measured at baseline before any aging begins
  • Time points: typically T0, T1, T3, T6, T9, T12 months — select based on the intended shelf life
  • Same panel at each time point: paired design maximises sensitivity to detect real changes
  • Pre-define acceptance criteria: decide the criterion before collecting data — do not change it after seeing results

Quantitative stability — % change from T0

For analytes that produce a measured value, performance is tracked as % change from the T0 baseline.

% change at Ti for sample j = (Ti,j − T0j) / T0j × 100

For the full sample panel at each time point:

  • Calculate mean % change and its 95% CI (paired t-test against 0)
  • Apply acceptance criterion: commonly ±10% (CLSI EP25-A general recommendation) or ±5% for high-precision analytes
  • A time point passes if the mean % change is within the criterion
  • The functional expiry is the last passing time point

Trend analysis

Regression of mean % change vs time (months) gives the drift rate (% per month). A significant slope indicates progressive degradation. Use the regression to extrapolate when the material would cross the acceptance limit — but note this is indicative only beyond measured time points.

21. ROC & Diagnostic Accuracy REVIEW

A Receiver Operating Characteristic (ROC) curve evaluates how well a quantitative test discriminates between two clinical states — typically disease-positive and disease-negative. It is central to analytical performance because it answers: at what cut-off does this measurement become a useful diagnostic tool?

The 2×2 decision table

At any chosen cut-off, test results are classified into four categories:

Disease +Disease −
Test +True Positive (TP)False Positive (FP)
Test −False Negative (FN)True Negative (TN)

Key performance metrics

  • Sensitivity (TPR) = TP / (TP + FN) — proportion of true positives correctly identified. High sensitivity → few missed cases.
  • Specificity (TNR) = TN / (TN + FP) — proportion of true negatives correctly identified. High specificity → few false alarms.
  • PPV (Positive Predictive Value) = TP / (TP + FP) — probability that a positive result is truly positive. Depends on prevalence.
  • NPV (Negative Predictive Value) = TN / (TN + FN) — probability that a negative result is truly negative. Depends on prevalence.
PPV and NPV depend on prevalence. A test with 95% sensitivity and 95% specificity has PPV = 50% when prevalence is 5%, but PPV = 95% when prevalence is 50%. Always interpret predictive values in the context of the population being tested.

The ROC curve

The ROC curve plots sensitivity (y-axis) vs 1 − specificity (x-axis) across all possible cut-off values. Each point on the curve represents one threshold. The curve shows the trade-off: moving the cut-off to catch more positives (higher sensitivity) inevitably increases false positives (lower specificity).

  • A perfect test passes through the top-left corner (100% sensitivity, 100% specificity) — AUC = 1.0
  • A useless test follows the diagonal — AUC = 0.5 (no better than coin-flip)
  • The further the curve bows towards the top-left, the better the discriminatory power

AUC — Area Under the Curve

The AUC summarises the overall discriminatory ability of the test in a single number between 0.5 and 1.0. It can be interpreted as: the probability that a randomly chosen disease-positive individual has a higher test value than a randomly chosen disease-negative individual.

AUCInterpretation
0.90 – 1.00Excellent discrimination
0.80 – 0.90Good discrimination
0.70 – 0.80Fair discrimination
0.60 – 0.70Poor discrimination
0.50 – 0.60Fail — no useful discrimination

Choosing an optimal cut-off

The "best" cut-off depends on the clinical context:

  • Youden's index — maximises (sensitivity + specificity − 1). Gives the point on the ROC curve furthest from the diagonal. A balanced approach.
  • High sensitivity priority — for screening tests where missing a case is dangerous (e.g., cancer screening). Accept more false positives.
  • High specificity priority — for confirmatory tests where false positives have serious consequences (e.g., unnecessary surgery). Accept more false negatives.
  • Cost-based — if you can quantify the cost of a false positive vs. a false negative, the optimal cut-off minimises total cost.
Example: A biomarker for sepsis has AUC = 0.88. At Youden's optimal cut-off of 12.5 ng/mL: sensitivity = 85%, specificity = 80%. But for ICU screening, you might lower the cut-off to 8 ng/mL (sensitivity 95%, specificity 60%) — missing sepsis is worse than running extra confirmatory tests.

Comparing two ROC curves

When comparing two biomarkers or two methods, you can test whether their AUCs are significantly different using the DeLong test. This accounts for the correlation between the two curves when they are measured on the same patients.

Logistic regression and combined markers

When a single analyte has moderate discrimination, logistic regression can combine multiple analytes into a single predicted probability. The ROC curve of the combined model often outperforms any individual marker. The tool in this app supports entering multiple analytes and building a combined ROC curve.

Key insight: AUC tells you how good a test can be — but clinical usefulness depends on the cut-off you choose, the prevalence in your population, and the consequences of errors. A test with AUC = 0.85 can be clinically useless if the prevalence is very low (PPV collapses) or if there is no actionable treatment for detected cases.

22. Antibiotic Susceptibility Agreement — EUCAST/CLSI BETA REVIEW

In clinical microbiology, a new antimicrobial susceptibility testing (AST) method is validated by comparing it against a reference method (typically broth microdilution). Unlike quantitative method comparison, the outcome is a categorical classification: Susceptible (S), Intermediate / Susceptible-Increased exposure (I), or Resistant (R).

The 3×3 agreement matrix

Results from the test method and the reference method are cross-tabulated in a 3×3 matrix. Diagonal cells represent agreement; off-diagonal cells represent discrepancies classified by clinical severity:

Error typeDefinitionClinical impact
Very Major Error (VME)Reference = R, Test = SMost dangerous — a resistant organism is reported as susceptible. Patient may receive ineffective treatment.
Major Error (ME)Reference = S, Test = RSusceptible organism reported as resistant — patient denied effective, possibly first-line therapy.
Minor Error (mE)Either method reports I when the other reports S or RLess critical — treatment may still be appropriate but interpretation differs.

Key metrics

  • Category Agreement (CA) — proportion of isolates where both methods agree on S/I/R. Target: ≥ 90%.
  • Essential Agreement (EA) — proportion of MIC results within ±1 dilution of the reference. Applies only when MICs are available. Target: ≥ 90%.
  • VME rate — calculated as VME / total Reference-R isolates. Target: ≤ 3%.
  • ME rate — calculated as ME / total Reference-S isolates. Target: ≤ 3%.
  • mE rate — calculated as mE / total isolates. Target: ≤ 10% (less stringent).
VME and ME rates use different denominators. VME is divided by the number of reference-R isolates, ME by the number of reference-S isolates — not total N. This means even a single VME can produce a high rate if few resistant isolates are tested.

Study design considerations

  • CLSI recommends testing at least 100 isolates per species–drug combination, with a mix of S, I, and R organisms.
  • If resistant isolates are rare, the VME rate becomes unreliable — a single misclassification from 5 R isolates = 20% VME rate.
  • For disk diffusion vs. broth microdilution: classify MIC results using the same breakpoint table (EUCAST or CLSI) applied to both methods.
  • Report results per antibiotic and per organism — aggregate rates can mask species-specific problems.
Example: 100 isolates tested. Reference: 50 S, 10 I, 40 R. Test method: 1 VME (R→S), 2 ME (S→R), 3 mE. CA = 94/100 = 94% ✓ · VME = 1/40 = 2.5% ✓ · ME = 2/50 = 4.0% ✗ (exceeds 3%). Even though overall agreement is high, the ME rate flags a systematic problem — investigate which drug–organism pairs are affected.
EUCAST vs CLSI breakpoints: Both organisations define S/I/R categories but may use different MIC or zone-diameter cut-offs. Always state which breakpoint table you applied — mixing tables invalidates the comparison.

Understanding precision, bias, and total error is not optional — it is a regulatory requirement. Clinical laboratories operate under strict quality frameworks that mandate how methods must be validated, verified, and monitored. These standards exist because a laboratory result is a medical act: it directly influences patient treatment.

24. Typical validation workflow

In practice, analytical performance validation follows a standard three-step sequence. Each step builds on the previous one — you need CV% before you can calculate sigma, and you need Bias% to complete the picture.

1
Precision study — measure CV%

Run your method repeatedly under controlled conditions to quantify random error. Use intermediate precision (day-to-day) rather than repeatability for a realistic CV%.

  • Quick study — 20+ patient samples or QC material over multiple days → use the Precision — Quick tool
  • Full precision study — CLSI EP05 design (5 days × 2 runs × 2 replicates minimum) → use the Precision (EP05) tool
Output: CV% (total intermediate precision)
2
Bias study — measure systematic error

Compare your method against a known reference to quantify systematic error (trueness).

  • CRM (gold standard) — measure a certified reference material with a known assigned value. Most reliable estimate of true bias.
  • EQA — compare your result against peer group mean. Less reliable: peer consensus is not a true reference value.
  • Method comparison — compare your method against a reference method using patient samples (CLSI EP09). Gives a method-derived bias estimate.
Output: Mean Bias% (absolute value)
3
Total Error & Sigma — combine and evaluate

Enter CV% + Bias% + TAE% (from EFLM or your specification) to calculate:

  • TE = |Bias%| + 1.65 × CV% — does total error fit within the allowable limit?
  • Sigma metric = (TAE% − |Bias%|) / CV% — how much margin does your method have?
  • QC strategy — sigma ≥ 6: minimal QC needed; sigma 4–6: standard Westgard rules; sigma < 4: tighten QC or improve method.
Output: TE%, sigma metric, QC rule recommendation

25. ISO 15189 — Validation and Verification Requirements

ISO 15189 (Medical laboratories — Requirements for quality and competence) is the international accreditation standard for clinical laboratories. It requires that all measurement procedures be evaluated for fitness for purpose before clinical use — but distinguishes clearly between validation and verification depending on the type of method.

The core rule

SituationRequirementTypical protocol
Commercially available, used as intended by manufacturerVerificationEP15 (precision) + bias check
Modified commercial method (different sample type, dilution, range)ValidationEP05 + EP09 + full performance panel
In-house developed methodFull validationEP05 + EP09 + linearity + interferences + reference interval
Method transferred from another laboratoryVerificationConfirm performance matches originating lab

What must be evaluated

ISO 15189 (clause 7.3) requires documented evidence for:

  • Precision — repeatability and intermediate precision (EP05 or EP15)
  • Trueness / Bias — against CRM, EQA, or reference method (EP09, bias study)
  • Measurement uncertainty — combined estimate of all error sources
  • Analytical measurement range (AMR) — the reportable range of the method
  • Reference intervals — verified or established for the local population
  • Interferences — haemolysis, lipaemia, icterus (HLI), common drugs
Verification scope: For a commercial method, ISO 15189 accepts a reduced verification package — typically precision (EP15), bias against EQA or CRM, and confirmation of the reportable range. Full interference and reference interval studies are only required if the manufacturer's data cannot be applied to your patient population.

Practical decision guide — validation or verification?

Ask these questions in order:

1. Is this method commercially available and used exactly as the manufacturer intends?
→ Yes: verification is sufficient.

2. Have you changed anything? (sample matrix, pre-treatment, calibrators, reportable range, instrument platform)
→ Yes to any: validation is required for the changed parameter(s).

3. Did you develop this method yourself?
→ Yes: full validation is required.

4. Is the manufacturer's performance data applicable to your population?
→ If not (e.g. paediatric reference intervals in an adult study): establish your own.

Documentation requirements

ISO 15189 requires a written validation/verification plan before starting, and a report after completion including:

  • Study design and acceptance criteria (defined before data collection)
  • Raw data and statistical analysis
  • Pass/fail conclusion against each acceptance criterion
  • Authorisation by the laboratory director before clinical release
Pre-define your acceptance criteria. ISO 15189 auditors specifically check that criteria were set before data collection — not adjusted after seeing the results. Always document your allowable bias and CV% targets in the validation plan before running the study.

26. EP05 — Precision Study Protocol (CLSI EP05-A3)

CLSI EP05 defines the standard protocol for evaluating the precision of a quantitative measurement procedure. Its purpose is to estimate repeatability (Sw) and intermediate precision (St) under controlled, real-world conditions before a method is released for clinical use.

Study design

The recommended design is 2 levels × 2 replicates × 20 days:

  • 2 concentration levels — typically a low-normal and a high-pathological concentration, ideally at clinical decision points
  • 2 replicates per run — measured in the same analytical run, back-to-back
  • 20 days — spread over at least 20 working days (not necessarily consecutive)

This gives 40 measurements per level. A shorter design (5 days × 4 replicates) is allowed for verification studies (EP15).

What is estimated

  • Sw (within-run SD) — purely from the duplicate pairs within each run. Captures random error within a single analytical session
  • Sb (between-run SD) — variation attributable to run-to-run differences (reagent lots, calibration drift, operator effects)
  • St (total/intermediate precision SD) — combines within-run and between-run: St = √(Sw² + Sb²)
Why 20 days? Precision is estimated under routine conditions — including normal day-to-day sources of variation such as reagent changes, different operators, maintenance, and environmental drift. Fewer days underestimates this variation.

Why nested ANOVA — not one-way or two-way?

You may already know one-way ANOVA (one grouping factor, e.g. compare means across days) and two-way ANOVA (two crossed factors, e.g. day × operator — every combination measured). EP05 uses neither. It uses nested ANOVA, and the reason matters:

DesignStructureProblem for EP05
One-way ANOVAGroups days onlyLumps within-run and between-run variation together — you cannot separate Sw from Sb
Two-way ANOVADay × run, fully crossedRequires the same run labels to mean the same thing across days — they don't. "Run 1" on day 1 is not the same run as "Run 1" on day 3
Nested ANOVAReplicates inside runs, runs inside days— correct model. Runs are unique within each day; replicates are unique within each run

The nested structure reflects reality: each run is a distinct analytical session that happens to fall on a particular day. The calculation partitions variance into within-run (Sw) — from the replicate pairs — and between-run (Sb) — from how run means scatter around the grand mean. This is the only way to get both components separately, which is exactly what EP05 requires.

In practice: you never need to run nested ANOVA yourself — the EP05 tool does it automatically when you enter your data. Understanding the structure just helps you design the study correctly: replicates must be measured within the same run, and runs must be genuinely separate analytical sessions.

Acceptance criteria

Compare your CV% against the allowable imprecision derived from your performance specification:

  • Biological variation (desirable): allowable CV% ≤ 0.5 × CVi
  • EFLM / manufacturer claim: stated CV% from the IFU or EFLM database
  • Sigma-based: CV% ≤ (TAE − |Bias|) / desired sigma level

EP15 is a verification protocol — not a full evaluation. Its purpose is to confirm that a commercially available method performs in your laboratory as the manufacturer has claimed. 28. EP09 — Method Comparison and Bias Estimation (CLSI EP09-A3)

CLSI EP09 defines the standard protocol for comparing two measurement procedures using patient samples. Its goal is to estimate systematic bias between methods and determine whether they are clinically interchangeable — i.e., whether a new method can replace an existing one without affecting patient care.

Study design

  • Minimum 40 patient samples — at least 40 paired results (some sources recommend 100 for robust regression)
  • Span the full clinical measurement range — samples should cover low, normal, and high concentrations including clinical decision levels
  • Native patient samples preferred — processed or stabilised materials may introduce matrix effects
  • Both methods measured on the same day — ideally within a short time window to minimise pre-analytical differences
  • Single measurement per sample per method — replicates are not required; EP09 uses sample-to-sample variation to assess bias, not within-sample precision

Statistical analysis

EP09 recommends a two-stage approach:

  1. Bland-Altman plot — visualise and quantify the magnitude and pattern of differences. Check for constant bias (horizontal band) vs proportional bias (differences increase with concentration)
  2. Regression analysis — Passing-Bablok regression is preferred (non-parametric, no distributional assumptions). Deming regression is acceptable when both methods have similar imprecision. OLS is not appropriate when both methods have random error

Bland-Altman assumptions

Bland-Altman assumes the differences between the two methods are approximately normally distributed. This matters because the limits of agreement (mean ± 1.96 × SD) rely on the normal distribution — if the differences are skewed or contain outliers, the 1.96 factor is no longer valid and the LoA will be misleading.

AssumptionWhy it mattersHow to check
Normality of differences LoA = mean ± 1.96 × SD requires normality. Skewed differences produce LoA that do not capture 95% of cases. Shapiro-Wilk test on the differences; histogram or Q-Q plot
Homoscedasticity A single SD is only meaningful if the spread of differences is constant across the range. A funnel shape (larger differences at higher concentrations) means the SD is understated at high values and overstated at low values. Bland-Altman plot — look for a funnel shape. If present, use log-transformed data or express differences as % of the mean (proportional Bland-Altman).
The method is fairly robust to mild departures from normality, especially with n > 40. The Shapiro-Wilk test in the tool tests the differences directly — if it fails, inspect the plot before reporting LoA at face value.

  • Passing-Bablok — does not minimise any distance. The slope is the median of all pairwise slopes, making it robust to outliers. Residuals are typically shown as vertical distances for simplicity.

Key insight: Each regression method produces its own CI that reflects its own assumptions. OLS confidence bands are always the narrowest (because they ignore X-error) — this does not mean OLS is more precise; it means OLS is overconfident.

Acceptance criteria

Define criteria before the study. A method passes when:

  • Mean bias ≤ allowable bias (from biological variation or clinical requirement)
  • 95% confidence interval of the bias lies entirely within ± allowable bias
  • Regression slope: 95% CI includes 1.0 (no proportional bias)
  • Regression intercept: 95% CI includes 0 (no constant bias)
  • Total Error (|bias| + 1.65 × SD of differences) ≤ TAE
Correlation is not agreement. A high Pearson r or R² does not mean the methods agree — it only means they track each other. Two methods can correlate perfectly yet show clinically unacceptable systematic differences. Always base your conclusion on Bland-Altman and bias assessment, not r alone.
EP09 vs method comparison in general: EP09 is specifically for patient sample comparisons. If you are comparing against a certified reference material (CRM), use EP15 (trueness verification) instead. EP09 is appropriate when both methods measure real patient samples under routine conditions.

29. Reference Intervals — EP28 BETA REVIEW

A reference interval (commonly called "normal range") defines the central 95% of results expected from a healthy reference population. It is used every day in clinical laboratories to flag abnormal patient results. CLSI EP28-A3c describes how to establish and verify these intervals.

Establishing vs. verifying

ApproachWhenSample sizeMethod
Establishing (de novo)New analyte, new population, no prior data≥ 120 reference individualsCalculate 2.5th and 97.5th percentiles directly
Verification (transference)Adopting a manufacturer's or published interval20 reference individualsCheck that ≤ 2 of 20 fall outside the claimed interval

Why 120 samples?

The 2.5th and 97.5th percentiles are estimated from the tails of the distribution. With fewer samples, the 90% confidence intervals around these limits become very wide. CLSI EP28 requires 120 as the minimum for a non-parametric approach because this is the smallest n that gives acceptable confidence intervals around both limits.

Non-parametric reference interval Lower limit = rank position ⌊0.025 × (n + 1)⌋ of sorted data
Upper limit = rank position ⌈0.975 × (n + 1)⌉ of sorted data

Parametric reference interval (if data are Gaussian) Lower limit = mean − 1.96 × SD
Upper limit = mean + 1.96 × SD

Non-parametric vs. parametric

  • Non-parametric (rank-based) — no assumption about data distribution. Simply sort values and pick the 2.5th and 97.5th percentile positions. Recommended by EP28 as the default method.
  • Parametric (mean ± 1.96 SD) — assumes data follow a Gaussian (normal) distribution. Only use if normality is confirmed (e.g., Shapiro-Wilk test). Can be applied to transformed data (e.g., log-transformed) if the original data are skewed.

Outlier detection

Outliers in reference data can distort the calculated interval. EP28 recommends screening for outliers before calculating limits:

  • Tukey's method — values below Q1 − 1.5 × IQR or above Q3 + 1.5 × IQR are flagged. Robust and widely used.
  • Dixon's Q test — tests the most extreme value against the range of the data. Better for small sample sizes (n < 30). Only tests one outlier at a time.
  • Always investigate flagged outliers clinically before removing — they may represent unrecognised pathology in a "healthy" individual.
Outlier removal is not automatic. If a value is flagged, check whether the individual truly met the inclusion criteria (healthy, fasting, no medication, etc.). Blindly removing statistical outliers can narrow the interval and increase false-positive flags in routine use.

Partitioning — when to split by subgroups

Many analytes differ by age, sex, or ethnicity. EP28 recommends testing for statistically and clinically significant differences between subgroups. If the difference is large enough, separate reference intervals should be established for each subgroup.

  • Common partitions: male/female, paediatric/adult/geriatric, pre-/post-menopausal
  • Each partition requires its own 120 reference individuals
  • If the difference between groups is < 0.25 × combined range, a single interval may suffice

Verification with 20 samples

When adopting a published or manufacturer's reference interval, EP28 allows a simplified verification:

  1. Collect samples from 20 healthy individuals matching the target population.
  2. Analyse all samples with your method on your instrument.
  3. Count how many results fall outside the claimed reference interval.
  4. If ≤ 2 out of 20 are outside → the interval is verified (acceptable for your laboratory).
  5. If ≥ 3 out of 20 are outside → the interval is not transferable. Investigate bias or establish your own interval.
Example: A reagent manufacturer claims glucose reference interval 4.1–5.9 mmol/L. You test 20 healthy volunteers: 18 fall within 4.1–5.9, 2 are outside → interval verified. If 4 are outside → interval not verified — check for systematic bias on your analyser.
Key insight: Reference intervals are not just about statistics — they depend on the reference population, pre-analytical conditions (fasting, posture, time of day), and the analytical method. An interval established on one platform cannot be assumed valid on another without verification.

30. Qualitative Limit of Detection — EP17 BETA REVIEW

Qualitative and semi-quantitative methods (e.g., rapid antigen tests, PCR-based assays, lateral flow immunoassays) report a binary result — positive (detected) or negative (not detected). The Limit of Detection (LoD) is the lowest analyte concentration at which the method reliably detects the target.

What LoD means for qualitative methods

CLSI EP17-A2 defines LoD as the concentration at which the method detects the analyte in a specified proportion of replicates — typically ≥ 95% of the time. Unlike quantitative LoD (defined via precision at low concentrations), qualitative LoD is based on a hit rate: the fraction of positive results at each concentration level.

Hit rate testing — the basic approach

  1. Prepare a series of concentration levels spanning the expected detection limit (e.g., 5–8 levels).
  2. Test each level with multiple replicates (at least 20 per level recommended; more gives tighter confidence intervals).
  3. Record the number of positive results at each level.
  4. Calculate the hit rate: Hit rate = positives / total replicates × 100%
  5. The LoD is the lowest concentration at which the hit rate meets or exceeds the target (e.g., 95%).
Hit rate Hit rate (%) = (number of positive results / total replicates) × 100

LoD criterion LoD = lowest concentration where hit rate ≥ target (typically 95%)

Probit analysis (optional refinement)

Rather than picking the first level that crosses the 95% threshold, probit regression fits a sigmoid curve to the hit rate data. This models the probability of detection as a function of concentration and interpolates the exact concentration at which the predicted hit rate equals the target. Probit analysis:

  • Provides a more precise LoD estimate than simple inspection of hit rates
  • Produces a confidence interval around the LoD
  • Works best with 5+ concentration levels and ≥ 20 replicates per level
  • Requires at least some levels below and above the target hit rate for reliable curve fitting

Confidence intervals

Each hit rate is a proportion, so its uncertainty is estimated using a binomial confidence interval (e.g., Clopper-Pearson exact method). With only 20 replicates and 19 positives:

Example: 19/20 = 95% hit rate. But the 95% CI is approximately 75–100%. With 60 replicates and 57 positives: 95% hit rate with 95% CI ≈ 86–99%. More replicates → narrower CI → more confident that you have truly reached the LoD.

Study design considerations

  • Blank / negative samples — always include a zero-concentration level to confirm specificity (false positive rate).
  • Concentration spacing — use closely spaced levels near the expected LoD for precise estimation. Widely spaced levels may miss the true LoD.
  • Matrix — use clinically relevant sample matrices (serum, urine, swab, etc.) rather than buffer. Matrix effects can shift the LoD.
  • Replicates — EP17 recommends a minimum of 20 replicates per level, but 40–60 gives much better precision.
Common pitfall: Testing only 3–5 replicates per level. With so few replicates, the difference between 80% and 100% hit rate is just 1 result — the confidence interval is too wide to draw meaningful conclusions.
