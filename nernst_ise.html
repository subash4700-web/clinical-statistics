How does this work?

An ion-selective electrode (ISE) works like a pH meter — but for other ions such as Na⁺, K⁺, or Ca²⁺. Inside the electrode is a membrane that is selectively permeable to one ion. When the sample touches the membrane, ions move across it and create a voltage (potential, measured in mV). The higher the ion concentration, the larger the voltage shift. The Nernst equation is the formula that converts that voltage into a concentration.

Ion in sample → Selective membrane → Voltage (mV) → Nernst equation → Concentration (mmol/L)
Key concept — the Nernst slope: The voltage doesn't change in proportion to concentration — it changes with the logarithm of concentration. For Na⁺ at body temperature (37°C), a 10× increase in sodium concentration raises the voltage by ~61.5 mV. This is the Nernst slope. For Ca²⁺ (charge +2), the slope is exactly half: ~30.8 mV. For Cl⁻ (charge −1), the slope is negative: the voltage decreases as concentration rises.

The Nernst equation — explained click to collapse
The Nernst equation
E = E₀ + S · log₁₀(c)
E — the voltage the electrode reads for your sample (mV)
E₀ — the electrode's "zero point" voltage, found by calibration (mV). It is different for every electrode.
S — the Nernst slope: how many mV the voltage shifts per 10× change in concentration
c — the ion concentration we want to find (mmol/L)

Rearranging to find concentration:  c = 10(E − E₀) / S

The slope S depends on the ion's charge (z) and temperature:
S = (2.303 · R · T) / (z · F)    where R = 8.314, F = 96 485, T in Kelvin

At 37°C (body temperature):
Na⁺, K⁺ (z = +1): S = +61.54 mV/decade  |  Ca²⁺ (z = +2): S = +30.77 mV/decade  |  Cl⁻ (z = −1): S = −61.54 mV/decade
How an ISE works — step by step
1. Selective membrane: The electrode tip contains a membrane that only lets one type of ion through (e.g. only Na⁺). Different membranes use different materials — the Na⁺ electrode uses a glass membrane, the K⁺ electrode uses a valinomycin ionophore membrane.

2. Ion partitioning: When the sample touches the membrane, ions from the sample move across it toward the internal reference solution (or vice versa). This movement of charge creates a voltage across the membrane.

3. Voltage measurement: The analyser measures this voltage in millivolts. The higher the Na⁺ concentration in the sample (compared to the internal reference), the higher the voltage.

4. Nernst conversion: The measured voltage is plugged into the Nernst equation to give a concentration. But first, E₀ must be known — this is why calibration is essential.

In the ABL900: Na⁺, K⁺, and ionised Ca²⁺ are measured with ISEs. H⁺ (pH) is measured with a glass electrode, which is also an ISE. Cl⁻ uses a solid-state ISE. All use the same Nernst principle.
Accuracy & limitations: ISEs measure ion activity, not concentration. Activity = γ × c, where γ is the activity coefficient. In clinical practice, γ is assumed constant and absorbed into E₀ during calibration with standards of known ionic strength. This means results are valid only when the sample matrix (ionic strength, protein content, pH) resembles the calibrators. Protein-rich or lipid-rich samples can alter the liquid junction potential and introduce systematic error.
Why does the electrode need calibrating?

Every electrode is slightly different — and it changes over time as the membrane ages. The Nernst slope tells us the theoretical voltage-to-concentration relationship, but the electrode's actual zero-point (E₀) cannot be predicted from theory alone. Two-point calibration measures the electrode's real response using two solutions with known concentrations, then calculates the true slope and E₀ for that specific electrode at that moment.

Standard 1 (known c₁, measured E₁) + Standard 2 (known c₂, measured E₂) → Real slope S and intercept E₀
Electrode efficiency compares the measured slope to the theoretical Nernst slope. A new, healthy electrode should give ≥95% efficiency. Below 90% means the electrode is degraded and results will be unreliable — the electrode should be reconditioned or replaced. The ABL900 checks this automatically during each calibration cycle.
