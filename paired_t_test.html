Hypothesis Visualisation In report ▼
Red curve — sampling distribution of d̄ (width = SE = sᵈ/√n). The blue shading and CI bar show the 95% CI of the mean difference: where the true mean difference likely lies.
Gray curve — distribution of individual differences (width = sᵈ). The gray shading and bar show the 95% reference range: where 95% of individual differences fall. The gray curve and reference range are included to illustrate the difference between individual variability and uncertainty about the mean — they are not part of the test.

Accuracy & limitations: The red curve shows the theoretical sampling distribution of the mean difference d̄. It assumes the within-pair differences are approximately normally distributed — or that n ≥ 30 (CLT). With small n and non-normal differences, the curve is approximate. Check the assumption section below; if normality is violated, prefer the Wilcoxon signed-rank test.
How it works click to expand
95% Confidence Interval of the Mean Difference
CI = d̄ ± tn−1, α/2 × SE   where   SE = sᵈ / √n
This interval answers: "Where does the true mean difference likely lie?" It narrows as n increases — more pairs means more certainty about the mean difference. This is the quantity tested by the paired t-test.
95% Reference Range (for illustration only — not part of the test)
Reference range = d̄ ± tn−1, α/2 × sᵈ
This interval answers: "Where do 95% of individual differences fall?" It does not narrow with larger n — individual differences always scatter around the mean. It is shown here solely to contrast with the CI and make the distinction visual.
Key insight: The CI and reference range use the same formula shape, but SE (= sᵈ/√n) instead of sᵈ. With n = 30 pairs, SE is ~5.5× smaller than sᵈ — which is why the red curve is much narrower than the gray curve.
