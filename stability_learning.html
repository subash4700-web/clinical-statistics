🧪 Understanding Stability Studies (Holdbarhedsforsøg)

Simulate reagent degradation over time — learn how to detect drift, interpret Bland-Altman and Kappa+McNemar results, and determine functional expiry.

What stability testing actually measures

Stability testing asks: "Is my material still the same?"
It tests the material — a reagent, stored sample panel, or calibrator — by assuming the method is stable and looking for change over time.

This is the opposite of analytical performance studies (precision, bias), which assume the material is stable and study how much the method varies.

ANALYTICAL PERFORMANCE
Fixed: stable QC material
Studied: method variation
Output: CV%, bias%, sigma
STABILITY & TIME
Fixed: stable, consistent method
Studied: material change over time
Output: % change, Kappa, functional expiry

⚠ A stability result (e.g. 8% change at T6) is a material signal, not an analytical error. You cannot apply sigma metrics or compare it to your TAE.

Exercise 3
Overcall — reagent reads higher
Positive drift — aged material over-detects. Common with some immunoassay reagents. Still a stability failure.
Mode: Quantitative Drift = +2%/mo Limit = ±10%
Exercise 4
Qualitative: stable Kappa
Pos/neg agreement stays high across all time points. Kappa ≥ 0.80 and McNemar non-significant at each point.
Mode: Qualitative κ drops slowly κ limit = 0.80

Qualitative: kappa failing at T6
Aged material starts missing positives. Kappa drops and McNemar becomes significant. Functional expiry is reached.
