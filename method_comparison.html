Lin's Concordance Correlation Coefficient (ρc)

Lin, L.I. (1989) — Agreement between two methods of measurement

The problem with Pearson r: Two methods can correlate perfectly (r = 1.0) yet one consistently reads twice as high as the other. Pearson r measures how tightly points follow any straight line — not whether that line is y = x. Lin's CCC tests agreement with the identity line (y = x) specifically.

Formula: ρc = r × Cb

Where r = Pearson correlation (precision — how tightly points follow a line) and Cb = bias correction factor (accuracy — how close that line is to y = x). Cb = 1 means perfect identity; Cb < 1 means there is location or scale shift.

Interpretation (McBride 2005):

ρcStrength of agreement
> 0.99Almost perfect
0.95 – 0.99Substantial
0.90 – 0.95Moderate
< 0.90Poor

What it detects that R² misses: A high R² with a low CCC means the methods correlate but do not agree — e.g. systematic bias (y = x + 5) or proportional bias (y = 1.2x). CCC penalises both scatter and deviation from identity.

Relationship to Bland-Altman: They are complementary. Bland-Altman shows where disagreement occurs across the range. CCC gives a single number summarising overall agreement — useful for reporting and comparing studies.

Clinical use: In EP09 method comparison, CCC is appropriate because it accounts for both random error (imprecision) and systematic error (bias), directly testing whether methods are interchangeable.
