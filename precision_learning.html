Study design
True population parameters — unknown in real life
Study design: You have 2 concentration levels — the same materials, measured in duplicate each day, repeated over 5 days = 20 measurements per level.
Important: You do not use a different patient each day. The same QC material or pooled sample is re-measured repeatedly — only the method varies.
How intermediate precision is calculated
Step 1 — within-run SD from duplicates:
Sw = √( Σ(x₁ᵢ − x₂ᵢ)² / 2n )

Step 2 — between-day variance:
Sb² = max(0, S²_day_means − Sw²/m)

Step 3 — intermediate precision SD:
Wrong: St = Sw + Sb
Correct: St = √(Sw² + Sb²)

What material should you use for an EP05 precision study?

Both commercial QC material and patient samples are acceptable for EP05 — but they answer slightly different questions.

Material Advantage Limitation
Commercial QC material Stable over 20 days; same lot throughout; convenient and reproducible May not be commutable — matrix additives can behave differently from real patient samples on some analysers
Patient samples (pooled, fresh) Commutable — closest to real-world performance; best for comparing against biological variation goals Requires fresh stable pools; freeze-thaw effects can introduce extra variation; harder to manage over 20 days
Practical rule: Use commercial QC material for routine validation and ISO 15189 documentation. Use fresh patient samples or certified reference materials when verifying performance against biological variation goals or when commutability is a known concern. Always use at least 2 concentration levels covering the clinical decision range, regardless of material type.

Commutability means the material behaves the same way on your analyser as a real patient sample would. Some commercial QC materials contain additives or stabilisers that alter the matrix — causing the analyser to give results that do not reflect true patient-sample precision.
