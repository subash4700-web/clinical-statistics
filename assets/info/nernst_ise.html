Potentiometry — Voltage to Concentration

Ion-selective electrodes (ISEs) measure the concentration of specific ions by detecting the voltage (potential) that develops across a selective membrane. This is the same principle as a pH electrode, extended to Na⁺, K⁺, Ca²⁺, Cl⁻, and other ions. ISEs are used in blood gas analysers (e.g. ABL900) to measure electrolytes in whole blood.

The Nernst Equation

The Nernst equation relates electrode potential to ion concentration:

E = E₀ + S · log₁₀(c)

  • E — measured electrode potential (mV) for the sample
  • E₀ — intercept potential (mV); the electrode's zero-point, determined by calibration
  • S — Nernst slope (mV per decade); how much E changes per 10× change in concentration
  • c — ion concentration (mmol/L)

Rearranged to find concentration:

c = 10(E − E₀) / S

The Nernst Slope

The theoretical slope depends on the ion's charge (z) and temperature (T in Kelvin):

S = (2.303 · R · T) / (z · F)

where R = 8.314 J·mol⁻¹·K⁻¹ and F = 96 485 C·mol⁻¹.

At 37°C (body temperature):

  • Na⁺, K⁺, Li⁺ (z = +1): S = +61.54 mV/decade
  • Ca²⁺ (z = +2): S = +30.77 mV/decade — half as sensitive as monovalent ions
  • Cl⁻ (z = −1): S = −61.54 mV/decade — voltage falls as concentration rises

The slope changes linearly with absolute temperature. This is why blood gas analysers thermostatically control the measuring cell at 37°C — even a few degrees of drift would cause systematic error in the reported concentration.

Two-Point Calibration

E₀ cannot be predicted from theory — it depends on the specific electrode, its membrane condition, and its history. Two-point calibration establishes E₀ and the real (measured) slope for the electrode using two standards with known concentrations:

Smeasured = (E₂ − E₁) / (log₁₀(c₂) − log₁₀(c₁))
E₀ = E₁ − Smeasured · log₁₀(c₁)

Electrode efficiency compares the measured slope to the theoretical Nernst slope:

Efficiency = |Smeasured| / |Stheoretical| × 100%

  • ≥ 95% — acceptable; electrode in good condition
  • 90–95% — reduced precision; consider reconditioning
  • < 90% — unreliable results; electrode reconditioning or replacement required

Activity vs. Concentration

Strictly, the Nernst equation relates potential to ion activity, not concentration. Activity = γ × c, where γ is the activity coefficient (affected by ionic strength, pH, and protein binding).

In clinical practice, γ is assumed constant and is absorbed into E₀ during calibration with standards of controlled ionic strength. This means ISE results are valid when the sample matrix resembles the calibrators — which is true for normal plasma/serum but can be affected by:

  • Severe hyperlipidaemia or hyperproteinaemia (alters ionic strength)
  • Changes in pH (affects Ca²⁺ ionisation and activity)
  • Drugs that bind the ion of interest

The ABL900 and Clinical ISE Analysers

The ABL900 (Radiometer) is an integrated blood gas and electrolyte analyser. It uses ISEs for Na⁺, K⁺, ionised Ca²⁺, and Cl⁻ alongside a pH glass electrode (also an ISE for H⁺) and amperometric sensors for pO₂ and pCO₂. The system performs automatic two-point calibration at set intervals and checks electrode efficiency before each measurement.

Results are reported in seconds from a whole-blood sample — which is only possible because the ISE principle requires no sample preparation: the electrode responds directly to the free ion activity in the sample.

Accuracy and Limitations

  • ISEs measure free (ionised) ion activity. Protein-bound or complexed ions are not detected.
  • Pseudo-hyponatraemia can occur with severe hyperlipidaemia when using flame photometry or indirect ISE, but direct ISE (as in ABL900) is unaffected.
  • Ionised calcium is pH-dependent: alkalosis decreases free Ca²⁺ (more protein binding). ABL900 corrects to pH 7.40 if requested.
  • Electrode fouling by proteins or blood cells between calibrations can introduce drift. Regular maintenance cycles are essential.
  • Temperature deviating from 37°C during measurement changes the slope and will give incorrect results.
