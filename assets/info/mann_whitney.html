Mann–Whitney U Test

Purpose

The Mann–Whitney U test (Wilcoxon rank-sum test) compares two independent groups when the outcome is ordinal or the normality assumption cannot be met. It is the non-parametric alternative to the independent two-sample t-test.

When to Use

  • Independent samples with continuous or ordinal outcome
  • Small samples or clearly non-normal distributions
  • Skewed data or data with outliers that cannot be removed
  • When parametric assumptions are violated

Key Idea

All observations from both groups are combined and ranked together. The test asks: are the ranks distributed randomly between the two groups, or does one group tend to have higher ranks? U is the count of how many times a Group 1 observation outranks a Group 2 observation.

Hypotheses

Two-sided: H₀: The two distributions are identical  |  H₁: One distribution is shifted

One-sided (greater): H₀: Group 1 ≤ Group 2  |  H₁: Group 1 > Group 2 (in rank)

One-sided (less): H₀: Group 1 ≥ Group 2  |  H₁: Group 1 < Group 2 (in rank)

Test Statistic

U₁ = n₁n₂ + n₁(n₁+1)/2 − R₁

U₂ = n₁n₂ − U₁

where R₁ is the sum of ranks for Group 1.

For large samples, U is converted to a standardized z-score using a normal approximation with tie correction.

Computation methods: Exact p-values enumerate all possible rank assignments (feasible for small n). For larger datasets, the normal approximation with tie correction is used automatically.

Effect Size

Rank-biserial correlation (r):

r = 1 − (2U) / (n₁ × n₂)

  • |r| < 0.3 = small  |  0.3–0.5 = medium  |  > 0.5 = large

Assumptions

  • Groups are independent (different subjects)
  • Outcome is at least ordinal (rankable)
  • Observations within each group are independent
  • Both groups come from populations with the same shape (for location-shift interpretation)

Limitations

  • Less powerful than the t-test when data are truly normally distributed
  • Ties reduce the precision of exact p-values
  • Tests distributional shift broadly — not strictly a test of medians unless distributions have the same shape

Decision Rule

Reject H₀ if p < α.

Visualisation Notes

Boxplots show the actual data distributions for each group — they can be skewed or non-normal, and that is expected. Medians, quartiles, and outliers are displayed.

The normal curve (when shown) represents the sampling distribution of the test statistic under the null hypothesis — not the distribution of your raw data. It only appears when using the normal approximation for large samples. The exact method does not require or show this approximation.

Clinical Interpretation

A significant result indicates that one group tends to have systematically higher values than the other. Report the median and IQR for each group alongside the p-value and effect size. Statistical significance alone does not establish clinical relevance.

Reporting Example

A Mann–Whitney U test showed a significant difference between groups, U = X.XX, z = X.XX, p = 0.0X, r = X.XX. Group 1 median = X.X (IQR: X.X–X.X); Group 2 median = X.X (IQR: X.X–X.X).

Outlier Handling

The tool flags potential outliers using the IQR rule (values below Q1 − 1.5×IQR or above Q3 + 1.5×IQR). You can include or exclude individual values using the interactive data table, and results update in real time. This is useful for sensitivity analysis and identifying data entry errors.

Generating Sample Data

Expandable "Generate Group 1 sample data" and "Generate Group 2 sample data" panels appear in the Data & Assumptions tab. Specify sample size, distribution (skewed/log-normal, uniform, or exponential), median, and spread parameter for each group. Click Generate to preview and Use in analysis to load the data.

Tip: Non-normal distributions (skewed, exponential) demonstrate scenarios where Mann–Whitney is preferable to the t-test.
