Bacterial Growth BETA

Growth Phases

  • Lag — Cells adapt to new medium. No increase in number. Duration depends on inoculum history.
  • Log (exponential) — Constant doubling. Straight line on a semi-log plot. This is the phase you measure.
  • Stationary — Growth = death. Nutrients depleted, waste accumulates.
  • Death — Viable count declines. Cells lyse or become VBNC.

Growth Rate (r)

r = (ln N₂ − ln N₁) / (t₂ − t₁)

  • Use data points from log phase only. Including lag or stationary phase will underestimate the true rate.
  • Units: per hour (h−1) or per minute (min−1).

Generation Time (Doubling Time)

g = ln(2) / r ≈ 0.693 / r

  • E. coli ≈ 20 min. M. tuberculosis ≈ 15–20 hours. S. aureus ≈ 30 min.

Measuring Growth

  • OD600 (turbidity) — Fast, real-time. Measures live + dead cells. Non-linear above OD ≈ 1.0.
  • CFU (viable count) — Gold standard for viable cells. Slow (overnight incubation).
  • Direct count — Microscopy or flow cytometry. Includes dead cells unless using viability stain.

Semi-log Plot

  • Plot ln(N) or log₁₀(N) vs time. Log phase appears as a straight line.
  • Slope of the linear region = growth rate (r for ln, or r/2.303 for log₁₀).

Log Phase Detection

  • Identify the linear region on the ln(N) vs time plot — manually or by fitting.
  • Exclude lag phase (initial flat region) and stationary phase (plateau).
  • Minimum 3–4 points in the linear region for a reliable slope estimate.

Common Mistakes

  • Using all data points — must select log phase only. Including lag or stationary biases the rate.
  • OD > 1.0 — Beer-Lambert breaks down. Dilute and re-measure, or use a calibration curve.
  • Wrong log base — ln (base e) for growth rate. Mixing ln and log₁₀ gives a 2.3× error.
  • Inadequate time resolution — too few time points makes it hard to identify log phase boundaries.

Clinical Relevance

  • Doubling time affects antibiotic dosing intervals — fast growers need more frequent dosing.
  • Slow growers (e.g. M. tuberculosis) require prolonged treatment courses.
  • Growth rate in blood culture systems predicts time-to-positivity, which correlates with bacterial load.
