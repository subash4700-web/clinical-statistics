Purpose

Method comparison analysis evaluates agreement between two quantitative measurement methods. It determines whether a new or alternative method produces results that are clinically equivalent to a reference method — i.e. whether the two methods can be used interchangeably in clinical practice.

When to Use

  • Validation of a new laboratory method or analyser before clinical release
  • Comparison of point-of-care vs. central laboratory instruments
  • Reagent lot or calibrator lot verification
  • Instrument replacement or relocation studies
  • Verification of manufacturer's performance claims (CLSI EP09)

Key Idea

Two methods may be highly correlated yet systematically disagree. Agreement analysis asks not "do they correlate?" but "how large are the differences between methods, and are those differences clinically acceptable?" The Bland-Altman approach directly analyses the magnitude and distribution of pairwise differences.

Bland-Altman Analysis

The Bland-Altman plot displays, for each sample, the difference between methods (Y-axis) against the mean of the two methods (X-axis). This reveals whether disagreement is constant across the measurement range or increases with concentration (proportional bias).

Bias (mean difference):

d̄ = mean(Method A − Method B)

  • Positive d̄: Method A reads higher on average
  • Negative d̄: Method A reads lower on average
  • Compare d̄ against your predefined allowable bias (from performance specifications)

Relative (%) Bland-Altman: Use when differences increase proportionally with concentration. Differences are expressed as (A − B) / mean × 100%. Preferred for analytes with wide concentration ranges.

Limits of Agreement (LoA)

LoA = d̄ ± Z × SDd

  • Z = 1.96 → 95% LoA (most common)
  • Z = 2.58 → 99% LoA
  • SDd = standard deviation of the individual differences

The LoA describe the range within which 95% of individual differences between the two methods are expected to fall. For clinical interchangeability, the entire LoA interval should lie within the predefined acceptable difference limit.

LoA are estimates — they carry uncertainty. For small n, consider reporting 95% CIs for the LoA bounds themselves. The tool displays the Bland-Altman plot with LoA lines, TAE bands, and optional total error band overlaid.

Total Error (TE)

Total error combines systematic error (bias) and random error (imprecision) into a single performance metric:

TE = |d̄| + Z × SDd

  • Z = 1.65 (90th percentile one-sided) is conventional for TE calculations
  • Compare TE against TAE — Total Allowable Error from your performance specification
  • If TE ≤ TAE: analytical performance is acceptable

TAE is derived from biological variation (desirable: TE ≤ 0.5 CVI + 1.65 × CVI × 0.5 + |bias|), from EQAS/RIQAS peer targets, or from regulatory/manufacturer specifications.

Regression Analysis

Regression quantifies the mathematical relationship between methods, revealing proportional and constant systematic bias.

y = slope × x + intercept

  • slope = 1, intercept = 0: perfect agreement
  • slope ≠ 1: proportional bias — disagreement grows with concentration
  • intercept ≠ 0: constant bias — fixed offset across the range

Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) — minimises vertical residuals. Assumes the X-method is error-free. Appropriate only when Method X is a true reference standard with negligible measurement error.

Deming Regression — accounts for measurement error in both methods (uses variance ratio λ). Preferred in clinical laboratory method comparisons where both methods have imprecision. Set λ = ratio of variance of Method Y to variance of Method X.

Passing-Bablok Regression — a non-parametric method: no assumptions about the distribution of errors, robust to outliers, and does not require a variance ratio. It is the recommended regression method in CLSI EP09 for routine method comparison and is widely used in published validation studies. Slope and intercept are reported with 95% confidence intervals.

Residuals & CI BETA

  • OLS residuals — vertical (minimises vertical distance). CI band is hyperbolic, widens at extremes.
  • Deming residuals — perpendicular to the line (minimises orthogonal distance). CI from jackknife resampling.
  • Passing-Bablok residuals — vertical (no distance is minimised; slope is median of pairwise slopes). CI from rank-based bounds.
  • OLS CI is always narrowest — because it ignores X-error. This is overconfidence, not higher precision.

Lin's Concordance Correlation Coefficient (ρc)

Lin's CCC measures agreement with the identity line (y = x) — not just correlation. Two methods can have near-perfect R² yet poor CCC if one reads systematically higher or if the slope deviates from 1.

ρc = r × Cb

  • r = Pearson correlation (precision — how tightly points follow a line)
  • Cb = bias correction factor (accuracy — how close that line is to y = x)
  • Cb = 1 means the regression line passes exactly through the origin with slope 1
  • Cb < 1 means location shift (mean bias) or scale shift (slope ≠ 1) is present
ρcAgreement
> 0.99Almost perfect
0.95 – 0.99Substantial
0.90 – 0.95Moderate
< 0.90Poor

A high R² with a lower CCC signals that the methods correlate but do not agree — indicating systematic or proportional bias. Use CCC alongside Bland-Altman and regression for a complete picture of method agreement.

CV on Differences

In a method comparison study, CV on differences reflects the combined variability of both methods together — it is not the precision of either method alone. It can be used as a rough indicator of overall measurement scatter across the pair. For judging whether the methods agree clinically, use the Bland-Altman limits of agreement and the TAE comparison instead.

√2 correction does not apply here. CVwithin = SDd / (√2 × x̄) is only valid when both measurements in a pair come from the same method (CLSI EP5/EP15 duplicate design). In method comparison the two columns come from different methods, so the variance decomposition Var(d) = 2σ² does not hold.

CV on differences expresses the variability of pairwise differences relative to the grand mean of all measurements, independently of between-sample biological variation.

CV = SDd / x̄ × 100%

For duplicate pairs (same sample, same method), apply the √2 correction to recover the single-measurement within-method SD:

CVwithin = (SDd / √2) / x̄ × 100%

The √2 correction applies because Var(d) = Var(x₁ − x₂) = 2σ², so σ = SDd / √2.

Correlation — Not Agreement

Pearson r and R² measure association, not agreement. Two methods can have near-perfect correlation (r ≈ 1) yet show clinically unacceptable systematic differences. Always use Bland-Altman or regression — not r alone — to judge method interchangeability.

  • R² = proportion of variance in Y explained by X
  • Spearman ρ = rank-based association (robust to outliers)

Normality of Differences

Bland-Altman LoA are valid when the differences are approximately normally distributed. The tool provides a Q-Q plot and histogram of differences to assess this assumption.

Mild non-normality has little impact on LoA with n ≥ 30. For severely skewed differences, consider log-transforming the data before analysis or reporting nonparametric LoA (2.5th–97.5th percentile of differences).

Performance Specifications

Define your acceptance criteria before running the comparison. Sources:

  • Biological variation — desirable allowable bias: ≤ 0.25 × √(CVI² + CVG²)
  • EQAS / RIQAS targets — peer group performance benchmarks
  • Manufacturer's claim — stated bias and imprecision from the IFU
  • Regulatory / accreditation body — e.g. CLIA, ISO 15189 requirements
  • Clinical decision thresholds — bias that would not alter a clinical decision

Assumptions

  • Paired measurements on the same samples — same patient material, same time point
  • Samples should span the full clinical measurement range — at least 40 samples is recommended (CLSI EP09)
  • Both methods must measure the same measurand
  • Differences are approximately normally distributed (for LoA interpretation)
  • Independent observations — no repeated use of the same sample

Clinical Interpretation

Address these questions in order:

  • Is the mean bias clinically acceptable (within the allowable bias specification)?
  • Are the LoA entirely within the acceptable difference limit?
  • Is TE ≤ TAE?
  • Is there evidence of proportional bias (slope ≠ 1) or constant bias (intercept ≠ 0)?
  • Does the Bland-Altman plot show a trend — does disagreement increase with concentration?

Statistical significance of bias does not equal clinical unacceptability. A very small bias may be statistically significant with n = 100 but clinically irrelevant. Always interpret alongside the CI and LoA.

Reporting Example

Method comparison (n = 50) showed a mean bias of 0.12 mmol/L (95% CI: 0.04–0.20). Limits of agreement ranged from −0.31 to 0.55 mmol/L, within the predefined acceptable difference limit of ±0.60 mmol/L. Calculated total error was 0.43 mmol/L, below the TAE of 0.60 mmol/L. Deming regression (λ = 1) showed no significant proportional bias (slope = 1.02, 95% CI: 0.98–1.06) and negligible constant bias (intercept = 0.04, 95% CI: −0.03–0.11). The new method demonstrates acceptable clinical agreement.
