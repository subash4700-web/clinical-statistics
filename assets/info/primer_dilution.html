Primer Dilution

The Primer Dilution tool calculates how to prepare working stock solutions from lyophilised or concentrated primer stocks. It also calculates per-reaction primer volumes when used together with PCR Setup.

When to use this tool

  • Reconstituting a lyophilised primer to a standard stock concentration (e.g. 100 µM)
  • Preparing a working dilution from the stock (e.g. 100 µM → 10 µM)
  • Calculating how much primer to add to each PCR reaction to reach a target final concentration

Typical primer concentrations

Stage Typical concentration Notes
Manufacturer stock (lyophilised) nmol supplied on tube Add TE buffer: nmol × 10 µL = 100 µM stock
Working stock 10 µM Dilute 1:10 from 100 µM stock; freeze in aliquots
Final in reaction 200–500 nM For a 25 µL reaction, 0.5–1.25 µL of 10 µM working stock per primer

Reconstituting from lyophilised primer

Manufacturers supply the amount of primer in nmol. To make a 100 µM stock, add:

Volume of TE (µL) = nmol × 10

Example: 25 nmol primer → add 250 µL TE buffer → 100 µM stock.

C₁V₁ = C₂V₂ — working dilution

To prepare a 10 µM working stock from a 100 µM stock:

V₁ = (C₂ × V₂) / C₁ = (10 µM × 200 µL) / 100 µM = 20 µL stock + 180 µL TE

PCR Setup integration

When opened from the PCR Setup tab, the total reaction volume is automatically transferred. The tool calculates per-reaction primer volume and total volume for the entire batch including 10% dead-volume overage.

Storage recommendations

  • Store stock solutions (100 µM) at −20 °C as single-use or small aliquots to avoid repeated freeze-thaw
  • Store working stocks (10 µM) at −20 °C; keep a small working aliquot at 4 °C for active use
  • Avoid exposing primers to UV light — nucleic acids absorb at 260 nm

Accuracy & limitations

Accuracy & limitations: Calculations assume the nmol value from the manufacturer is accurate and that the primer is fully dissolved. Pipetting volumes below 1 µL carry significant error — pre-dilute stocks further where necessary. Final concentrations in the reaction are approximate; optimise empirically if amplification efficiency is suboptimal.
