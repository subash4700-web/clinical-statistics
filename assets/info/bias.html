Purpose

Bias and trueness analysis quantifies the systematic error of a measurement method — the consistent tendency to measure higher or lower than the true value. Trueness is the closeness of the mean of an infinite series of measurements to the true value; bias is the numerical expression of the departure from trueness.

This tool compares measured results against a known reference value (e.g. a certified reference material or EQA assigned value) and determines whether the systematic deviation is clinically acceptable. For comparing two methods without a reference standard, use the Method Comparison tool (Bland-Altman).

When to Use

  • Verifying a new method against a certified reference material (CRM) or assigned target value
  • Interpreting External Quality Assurance (EQA/EQAS/RIQAS) results — comparing your mean to the peer or assigned target
  • Trueness verification before clinical release of a new analyser or reagent lot
  • Monitoring systematic drift over time (trending against a stable reference)
  • Demonstrating compliance with manufacturer bias claims or regulatory limits

Key Idea

Even a highly precise method (low random error) can have significant bias — it reliably returns the wrong answer. Bias analysis specifically isolates and quantifies this systematic component. A method passes trueness evaluation when its mean bias, along with its uncertainty (confidence interval), lies entirely within the predefined allowable bias.

Absolute vs Relative (%) Bias

Absolute bias — difference in the same units as the measurement:

bias = x̄measured − reference

Use when the allowable limit is defined in concentration units (e.g. ±0.3 mmol/L for glucose).

Relative (%) bias — bias expressed as a fraction of the reference value:

bias% = (x̄measured − reference) / reference × 100

Use when the allowable limit is defined as a percentage (e.g. ±5% from EQA target). Preferred for analytes where measurement uncertainty scales proportionally with concentration.

Formulas

Mean % bias across n measurements:

d̄ = (1/n) × Σ [(xᵢ − rᵢ) / rᵢ × 100]

SD of bias = SD of individual % differences

  • xᵢ = measured result for sample i
  • rᵢ = reference (true) value for sample i
  • When a single reference value is used, rᵢ = r for all i

When per-sample reference values are provided (e.g. multi-level EQA), the tool calculates individual % bias for each sample and averages them. This correctly handles cases where the reference value differs by concentration level.

Confidence Interval

The 95% CI for mean bias is calculated using the t-distribution:

d̄ ± tn−1, 0.025 × (SD / √n)

This CI answers: "Where does the true systematic bias likely lie?" For a method to demonstrate acceptable trueness, the entire 95% CI should fall within the allowable bias limits — not just the point estimate.

Requiring the full CI (rather than just the mean) to be within the allowable limit is the more conservative and recommended approach, analogous to CLSI EP09 guidance for method comparison.

Significance Test

A one-sample t-test evaluates whether the mean bias differs significantly from zero (H₀: μbias = 0):

t = d̄ / (SD / √n), df = n − 1

Statistical significance (p < 0.05) means the bias is detectable given the sample size. It does not mean the bias is clinically important. With large n, even a trivially small bias becomes statistically significant. Always evaluate magnitude (CI and allowable bias) alongside the p-value.

Performance Specifications

Define your allowable bias before running the evaluation. Sources:

  • Biological variation — desirable allowable bias: ≤ 0.25 × √(CVI² + CVG²), where CVI = intra-individual and CVG = inter-individual biological variation coefficients
  • EQA / RIQAS peer targets — the allowable deviation from the assigned peer value, published by your EQA scheme
  • Manufacturer's claim — stated trueness/bias from the IFU or package insert
  • Regulatory requirements — e.g. CLIA total allowable error limits (TEa), which implicitly constrain allowable bias
  • Clinical decision thresholds — bias that would not alter a clinical decision (e.g. reclassify a patient across a diagnostic cut-off)

Assumptions

  • The reference value is sufficiently accurate (reference uncertainty is small relative to allowable bias)
  • Measurements are independent (no repeated use of the same sample in a way that inflates n)
  • The measured material is commutable with the reference standard (matrix equivalence)
  • The % bias values are approximately normally distributed (required for CI and t-test validity)
  • Pre-analytical conditions are controlled and consistent across replicates

Non-commutability — where the matrix of a certified reference material behaves differently from native patient samples — is a common source of apparent bias. Verify with native patient specimens when possible.

Decision Rule

  • PASS: |mean bias%| ≤ allowable bias AND (optionally) the entire 95% CI lies within ± allowable bias
  • FAIL: |mean bias%| exceeds the allowable limit, or the CI extends beyond the limit
  • If no allowable bias is specified, report the mean bias and CI without a PASS/FAIL verdict

Clinical Interpretation

Address these questions in order:

  • Is the mean bias within the allowable limit from your performance specification?
  • Does the 95% CI lie entirely within the allowable limits?
  • Is the bias statistically significant? If yes, is it also clinically meaningful?
  • Does bias vary across concentration levels (look for concentration-dependent patterns in the individual bias plot)?
  • Are any individual samples strongly outlying — possibly indicating pre-analytical or matrix effects?

A PASS verdict at one concentration level does not guarantee acceptable trueness across the full measurement range. Always evaluate at clinically relevant decision points (e.g. low, normal, and high medical decision concentrations).

Reporting Example

Trueness was evaluated using n = 20 measurements against an EQA assigned value of 5.0 mmol/L. The mean bias was +1.8% (95% CI: +0.9% to +2.7%), with SD of bias 1.6%. The bias was statistically significant (t = 4.14, p < 0.001). However, the entire 95% CI fell within the allowable bias of ±5.0% (EQAS performance criterion), and the method was classified as PASS for trueness at this concentration level.
