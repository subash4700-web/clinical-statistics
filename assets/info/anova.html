Purpose

One-Way ANOVA tests whether the means of three or more independent groups differ significantly.

When to Use

  • Comparing laboratory values across multiple patient groups
  • Evaluating different treatment conditions simultaneously
  • Comparing more than two analytical methods

Key Idea

ANOVA compares variability between groups to variability within groups. If between-group variability is large relative to within-group variability, evidence suggests that not all group means are equal. A significant ANOVA only tells you that at least one group differs — post-hoc tests identify which ones.

Hypotheses

H₀: μ₁ = μ₂ = ... = μk

H₁: At least one group mean differs from the others.

Variance Decomposition

Total variability is partitioned into:

SSTotal = SSBetween + SSWithin

  • SSBetween — variation attributable to group differences
  • SSWithin — variation within groups (residual / error)

Test Statistic

F = MSBetween / MSWithin

  • MSBetween = SSBetween / dfbetween
  • MSWithin = SSWithin / dfwithin

Large F indicates group means differ more than expected by chance.

Degrees of Freedom

dfbetween = k − 1

dfwithin = N − k

where k = number of groups, N = total sample size.

Effect Size

Eta squared (η²):

η² = SSBetween / SSTotal

  • 0.01 = small  |  0.06 = medium  |  0.14 = large

η² tends to overestimate the population effect in small samples. Partial η² or ω² are preferred for unbiased estimates.

Post-Hoc Tests

A significant ANOVA result indicates that groups differ but does not specify which pairs. Post-hoc tests control the familywise error rate across multiple comparisons:

  • Tukey's HSD — recommended for equal group sizes; controls familywise error well
  • Bonferroni — conservative; divides α by the number of comparisons
  • Games-Howell — preferred when variances are unequal

Each post-hoc comparison produces a mean difference, confidence interval, and adjusted p-value.

Assumptions

  • Groups are independent
  • Outcome is continuous
  • Residuals are approximately normally distributed within each group
  • Homogeneity of variances across groups (Levene's test)

Normality Check

Normality is assessed on residuals (observed − group mean) using Shapiro–Wilk and Q–Q plots per group.

For n ≥ 30 per group, ANOVA is robust to mild non-normality due to the Central Limit Theorem. Variance homogeneity (Levene's test) is more critical than normality for valid F-tests.

If Assumptions Are Violated

  • Use Welch's ANOVA if group variances are unequal (more robust than standard ANOVA)
  • Consider the Kruskal–Wallis test as a non-parametric alternative
  • Inspect residual plots and check for outliers

Decision Rule

Reject H₀ if p < α. Then apply post-hoc tests to identify differing pairs.

Clinical Interpretation

A significant ANOVA result indicates that at least one group mean differs from the others. It does not identify which groups differ — post-hoc comparisons are required. Consider effect size (η²) and the magnitude of differences alongside the p-value.

Visualisation Notes

The visualisation shows the distribution for each group using their actual standard deviations. Wider curves indicate higher within-group variability; narrower curves indicate lower variability. The grand mean (dashed line) represents the overall mean across all groups under H₀. Arrows indicate the between-group spread relative to within-group spread — the conceptual basis of the F-ratio.

Reporting Example

A one-way ANOVA showed a significant difference between groups, F(dfbetween, dfwithin) = X.XX, p = 0.0X, η² = X.XX. Post-hoc Tukey comparisons revealed that Group A differed significantly from Group C (p = 0.0X).
