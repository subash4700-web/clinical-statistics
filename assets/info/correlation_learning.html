Understanding Correlation

What is correlation?

Correlation measures the strength and direction of a linear relationship between two continuous variables. Pearson's r ranges from −1 (perfect negative relationship) through 0 (no relationship) to +1 (perfect positive relationship). The square of r (R²) tells you the proportion of variance in one variable explained by the other.

Pearson r vs Spearman ρ

  • Pearson r — assumes both variables are normally distributed and the relationship is linear. Sensitive to outliers.
  • Spearman ρ — rank-based, non-parametric. Use when data is skewed, ordinal, or contains outliers. More robust in clinical data.

Why correlation does NOT mean agreement

This is the most important concept in this section:

  • Two methods can have r = 0.99 but one always reads 20% higher — they correlate perfectly but do not agree.
  • Correlation is about the shape of the relationship. Agreement is about the size of the differences.
  • For method comparison, always use Bland-Altman (not r) to evaluate whether two methods can be used interchangeably.

Interpreting r

In clinical lab work, high r (>0.95) between two methods tells you the methods are measuring the same analyte. It does NOT tell you they give the same numbers. A well-designed method comparison will always have high r — the interesting question is the Bland-Altman plot.

Common mistake

Reporting r as evidence of method agreement. "r = 0.98, therefore the methods agree" is incorrect and a common error in lab validation reports. r only tells you the relationship is strong and linear — not that the values are interchangeable.
