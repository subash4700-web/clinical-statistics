Purpose

This tool evaluates agreement between two qualitative (pos/neg) methods applied to the same samples. It answers two questions simultaneously:

  • How much do the methods agree overall? — Cohen's Kappa (concordance)
  • When they disagree, is the disagreement random or systematic? — McNemar's test (tilt)

Together they give a complete picture of method performance that neither test provides alone.

When to Use

  • Comparison of two qualitative laboratory methods (e.g. two immunoassays, two agglutination tests)
  • Validation or verification of a new pos/neg method against a reference method
  • Stability studies (holdbarhedsforsøg) — comparing fresh vs aged reagents or test cards
  • Inter-rater or inter-laboratory agreement studies
  • Point-of-care vs central laboratory qualitative comparison
  • Blood bank: comparing card generations, lot changes, or storage conditions

The 2×2 Contingency Table

Each sample is classified by both methods, producing four counts:

Method B: Positive Method B: Negative
Method A: Positive a — both positive (agree) b — A+, B− (discordant)
Method A: Negative c — A−, B+ (discordant) d — both negative (agree)
  • a + d = concordant pairs (agreement) — used by Kappa
  • b + c = discordant pairs (disagreement) — used by McNemar
  • b = Method A calls positive, Method B calls negative
  • c = Method A calls negative, Method B calls positive

Cohen's Kappa — Concordance

Kappa measures agreement corrected for chance. Two methods with very different positive rates might agree often by chance alone — Kappa removes that chance component.

κ = (Pₒ − Pₑ) / (1 − Pₑ)

  • Pₒ = observed agreement = (a + d) / N
  • Pₑ = expected agreement by chance = [(a+b)(a+c) + (c+d)(b+d)] / N²
  • κ = 1: perfect agreement
  • κ = 0: agreement no better than chance
  • κ < 0: agreement worse than chance

Kappa is sensitive to prevalence — a very low or very high proportion of positives can produce a low κ even with high observed agreement. Always report Pₒ alongside κ.

McNemar's Test — Systematic Tilt

McNemar's test uses only the discordant pairs (b and c) to ask: are the disagreements symmetric, or does one method systematically call more positives than the other?

H₀: b = c (discordances are random — no systematic bias)

H₁: b ≠ c (one method consistently calls more positives)

When b + c ≥ 25: χ² = (|b − c| − 1)² / (b + c) with continuity correction
When b + c < 25: exact binomial test (more accurate for small numbers of discordant pairs)

  • p ≥ α: no systematic bias — disagreements are random
  • p < α and b > c: Method A calls more positives than Method B
  • p < α and c > b: Method B calls more positives than Method A

McNemar does NOT measure how much the methods agree — only whether the direction of disagreement is symmetric. A significant McNemar with a high Kappa means systematic but small bias. A non-significant McNemar with a low Kappa means random but frequent disagreement.

Using Both Together

The two tests answer different questions and must be read together:

Kappa McNemar Interpretation
High (≥ 0.80) Not significant Ideal. Methods agree well, no systematic bias.
High (≥ 0.80) Significant Systematic but small bias. One method consistently over- or under-calls. Clinically relevant only if the positive rate difference matters.
Moderate (0.40–0.79) Not significant Random disagreement. Methods differ, but neither is systematically biased — investigate analytical imprecision or sample handling.
Low (< 0.40) Significant Poor agreement with systematic bias. Methods should not be used interchangeably.

Stability Studies (Holdbarhedsforsøg) — Qualitative

For qualitative stability studies, the same panel of known samples is tested with a fresh reference (T0) and with aged/stored material at each time point (T1, T3, T6 months, etc.). Each time point produces one 2×2 table — run the full Kappa + McNemar analysis at each.

  • Plot κ vs time — a declining κ indicates degrading agreement as the material ages
  • Track McNemar p-value and direction — a significant tilt indicates the aged material is systematically missing positives (most common) or generating false positives
  • The functional expiry is the time point at which κ falls below your pre-defined acceptance criterion or McNemar becomes significant
  • Pre-define acceptance criteria before the study (e.g. κ ≥ 0.80 and McNemar p ≥ 0.05)

For quantitative stability studies (where the outcome is a measured value rather than pos/neg), use a bias/drift approach: % change from baseline, paired comparison at each time point, and regression of result vs time — aligned with CLSI EP25-A.

Assumptions

  • Paired observations — the same samples are tested by both methods
  • Dichotomous outcome — each result is strictly positive or negative
  • Independent pairs — each sample contributes one pair of results
  • McNemar requires b + c ≥ 5 for meaningful results (exact binomial preferred when b + c < 25)
  • Kappa is not appropriate for ordinal categories (e.g. reaction strength 0–4+) — use Weighted Kappa instead

Reporting Example

Qualitative agreement between new and aged DAT cards was assessed on 60 paired samples. Cohen's κ = 0.78 (95% CI: 0.63–0.93), indicating substantial agreement (Pₒ = 91.7%). McNemar's test was not significant (p = 0.18), confirming that the discordances were symmetric with no evidence of systematic bias. The aged cards met the pre-defined acceptance criterion of κ ≥ 0.70 and McNemar p ≥ 0.05.
