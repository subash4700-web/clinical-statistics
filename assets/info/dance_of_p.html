Dance of the P-values

This simulation tool illustrates one of the most misunderstood concepts in statistics: p-values and confidence intervals vary from experiment to experiment, even when the true effect never changes.

Purpose

By repeatedly sampling from a fixed population with known parameters, you can directly observe how much sampling variability affects the outcome of a single experiment. The "dance" refers to how CIs and p-values jump around while the true effect remains constant in the background.

When to use this tool

  • Understanding why a single significant p-value is not proof of an effect
  • Seeing how sample size affects the proportion of significant results (power)
  • Visualising the difference between statistical and clinical significance
  • Teaching frequentist inference: what a 95% CI actually means

Key concepts

What a p-value is

The p-value is the probability of obtaining a result at least as extreme as the observed one, assuming the null hypothesis is true. It is not the probability that H₀ is true, and it does not measure the size or importance of an effect.

What a confidence interval is

A 95% CI is a procedure: if you repeat the experiment many times and compute a CI each time, 95% of those intervals will contain the true parameter. It does not mean there is a 95% probability that the true value lies in this specific interval.

The forest plot in this tool makes this concrete — you can watch individual intervals miss the true mean, and count how often this happens.

Power

Power = P(significant result | H₀ false) = the long-run fraction of experiments that correctly detect a real effect. After enough repetitions, the proportion of p < α runs estimates the power of the study design you have set up.

Cohen's δ (effect size)

δ = (μ₁ − μ₂) / σ — the standardised difference between the two true means. Unlike a p-value, it does not change with sample size. Small: 0.2, Medium: 0.5, Large: 0.8 (Cohen 1988).

What to observe

  • Under H₀ (δ = 0): p-values are uniformly distributed on (0, 1); roughly 5% fall below 0.05 by chance alone — these are false positives (Type I errors).
  • Under H₁ (δ ≠ 0): p-values pile up near 0; the fraction below α estimates power. The larger the effect or the bigger the sample, the tighter this pile.
  • Small n: Wide CIs, low power — many experiments miss the effect even though it is real.
  • Large n, tiny δ: Nearly every experiment is significant, but the effect may be too small to matter clinically.

Accuracy & limitations

Accuracy & limitations: Simulations assume normally distributed data and use a two-sample (or one-sample) t-test. Estimated power from the simulation converges to the true analytical power as the number of experiments increases. The simulation is not a replacement for prospective power calculations — it is a visualisation tool. Real studies have one sample, not the luxury of repeated draws from a known population.
