Photometry — Absorbance to Concentration

This tool converts absorbance measurements from a spectrophotometer into ion or molecule concentrations using two complementary approaches: the Lambert-Beer law (for known absorptivity) and linear calibration curves (for unknown or complex samples).

Lambert-Beer Law

The Lambert-Beer law describes how light is absorbed as it passes through a solution:

A = ε · l · c

  • A — absorbance (dimensionless; A = log₁₀(I₀/I))
  • ε — molar absorptivity (L·mol⁻¹·cm⁻¹); specific to each substance and wavelength
  • l — path length (cm); typically 1 cm for standard cuvettes
  • c — concentration (mol/L or mmol/L depending on ε units)

Rearranged to find concentration:

c = (A − A₀) / (ε · l)

where A₀ is the blank absorbance (reagent, cuvette, and solvent background).

Absorbance and Transmittance

Absorbance is a logarithmic measure. A = 1.0 means 90% of light is absorbed (10% transmitted). A = 2.0 means 99% absorbed (1% transmitted). The logarithmic relationship is why Beer's law is linear in concentration.

  • A < 0.02 — very low; reading may be unreliable (noise dominated)
  • A 0.1–1.0 — optimal linear range for most spectrophotometers
  • A > 1.0 — approaching the instrument's upper limit; stray light causes negative deviation
  • A > 1.5 — likely outside linear range; result unreliable. Dilute and re-measure.

Blanking

The blank (A₀) corrects for absorbance from the reagent, cuvette, and solvent — not the analyte. Always subtract the blank before calculating concentration. A zero blank (A₀ = 0) assumes the spectrophotometer was zeroed against the blank — check your instrument protocol.

Units: mol/L vs g/L

When ε is given in L·mol⁻¹·cm⁻¹, concentration is calculated in mol/L (or mmol/L if ε is in L·mmol⁻¹·cm⁻¹). To convert to g/L:

c (g/L) = c (mol/L) × MW (g/mol)

This is useful for proteins (e.g. haemoglobin, MW ≈ 64 458 g/mol) where clinical results are reported in g/L rather than mmol/L.

Calibration Curve

When ε is unknown, or when the sample matrix introduces variability, a calibration curve is used. Standards with known concentrations are measured, and a linear regression line (A vs. c) is fitted. Unknown sample concentrations are then read from this line.

A = slope · c + intercept

The quality of the calibration is assessed by R² (coefficient of determination):

  • R² ≥ 0.999 — excellent; suitable for quantitative clinical work
  • R² 0.99–0.999 — acceptable for many applications
  • R² < 0.99 — poor linearity; investigate standards or reagent quality
  • R² < 0.95 — unacceptable; do not use for reporting

Clinical Applications

  • Haemoglobin (Drabkin's method) — HiCN absorbance at 540 nm; ε = 44 000 L·mol⁻¹·cm⁻¹; result in g/L
  • Total protein (Biuret) — absorbance at 540 nm; calibration curve from protein standards
  • Glucose (enzymatic) — absorbance at 505 nm; calibration curve approach
  • Bilirubin — absorbance at 455 nm; dual-wavelength correction for interference
  • Creatinine (Jaffé method) — absorbance at 510 nm; subject to interferences

Accuracy and Limitations

  • Beer's law assumes monochromatic light, dilute solutions, and no chemical interactions between solute molecules. These assumptions break down at high concentrations.
  • Turbidity (lipemia, haemolysis) increases apparent absorbance and introduces positive bias.
  • Temperature can affect enzyme-based colorimetric reactions — ensure temperature control.
  • Micro-cuvettes with shorter path lengths (e.g. l = 0.2 cm) require corresponding adjustment of ε or path length in the formula.
  • Always check that the unknown sample's absorbance falls within the range of the calibration standards.

For educational and research use. Always verify results against institutional SOPs and reference method guidelines before reporting clinical results.
