Bacterial Identification BETA

Why Identify?

  • Correct ID → correct antibiotic → better patient outcome. Empiric therapy is a starting point, not the endpoint.

Gram Stain

  • Gram-positive (GP) — purple/blue. Thick peptidoglycan layer retains crystal violet.
  • Gram-negative (GN) — pink/red. Thin peptidoglycan + outer membrane. Decolorizes, takes up safranin.
  • Most important first step. Immediately narrows the differential by half.

Morphology

  • Cocci — clusters (Staphylococcus), chains (Streptococcus), diplococci (Neisseria, Pneumococcus).
  • Rods (bacilli) — large GP rods (Bacillus, Clostridium), small GN rods (Enterobacteriaceae, Pseudomonas).
  • Arrangement + Gram reaction together give a strong initial clue.

Catalase

  • Drop H₂O₂ on colony. Bubbles = positive (O₂ gas from catalase).
  • Staphylococcus (+) vs Streptococcus/Enterococcus (−).
  • Do not test on blood agar — RBC catalase gives false positives.

Coagulase

  • Differentiates staphylococci. S. aureus = coagulase positive. All others (CoNS) are negative.
  • Slide test (bound coagulase, clumping factor) — rapid but misses some strains. Confirm negatives with tube test.
  • Tube test (free coagulase) — definitive. Clot formation in 4 h (or up to 24 h).

Oxidase

  • Detects cytochrome c oxidase. Positive: Pseudomonas, Neisseria, Vibrio, Campylobacter.
  • Negative: Enterobacteriaceae. Key test to separate non-fermenters from enterics.
  • Use fresh reagent. Read within 10–30 seconds to avoid false positives from autooxidation.

Haemolysis (on Blood Agar)

  • Alpha (α) — green/brown zone. Partial lysis. S. pneumoniae, viridans streptococci.
  • Beta (β) — clear zone. Complete lysis. S. pyogenes (GAS), S. agalactiae (GBS), S. aureus.
  • Gamma (γ) — no haemolysis. Enterococcus (some), non-haemolytic streptococci.

Key Biochemical Tests

  • Optochin — S. pneumoniae sensitive; viridans streptococci resistant.
  • Bacitracin — S. pyogenes (GAS) sensitive; other beta-haemolytic strep resistant.
  • CAMP test — S. agalactiae (GBS) positive. Arrow-shaped haemolysis near S. aureus streak.
  • Bile esculin — Enterococcus and Group D strep positive (black precipitate).
  • 6.5% NaCl tolerance — Enterococcus grows; Group D strep does not.
  • Novobiocin — S. saprophyticus resistant; other CoNS sensitive.
  • Lactose fermentation — E. coli, Klebsiella (+, pink on MacConkey). Salmonella, Shigella (−).
  • Indole — E. coli (+), Klebsiella (−). Tests tryptophan metabolism.
  • Citrate — Klebsiella, Enterobacter (+, blue). E. coli (−).
  • H₂S production — Salmonella (+), Shigella (−). Visible on TSI/KIA or XLD agar.
  • Urease — Proteus (rapid +), Klebsiella (slow +). E. coli (−).

Limitations

  • Biochemical ID is presumptive. Atypical strains can give misleading results.
  • MALDI-TOF MS — rapid, accurate protein fingerprint. Now the standard in many clinical labs.
  • 16S rRNA sequencing — definitive for unusual or non-culturable organisms. Slower and more expensive.
  • Automated panels (VITEK, Phoenix) combine multiple biochemicals but still have misidentification rates for rare species.

Educational use only; always verify with SOPs and validated methods.
