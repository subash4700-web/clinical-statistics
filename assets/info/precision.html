Purpose

Precision analysis quantifies how reproducible a measurement method is — how close repeated measurements of the same sample are to one another. It is not about correctness (that is trueness/bias), but about consistency and random error.

Poor precision means results fluctuate unpredictably: a patient's glucose could appear normal one run and elevated the next, purely due to analytical variation. Precision evaluation is required before releasing any new method, reagent lot, or instrument into clinical use.

When to Use

  • Validation of a new laboratory method or analyser before clinical release
  • Reagent or calibrator lot verification
  • Ongoing internal QC monitoring (Levey-Jennings, Westgard rules)
  • Verification of manufacturer's precision claims (CLSI EP15-A3)
  • ISO 15189 / CAP accreditation documentation

CLSI EP05-A3 Standard

EP05-A3 (Evaluation of Precision of Quantitative Measurement Procedures) is the CLSI guideline that defines how precision must be measured and reported in clinical laboratories. It is recognized by ISO 15189, CAP, and EQAS programs globally.

Recommended experiment design: 5 days × 2 runs/day × 2 replicates/run = 20 measurements minimum per sample level. Use at least two concentration levels (low and high medical decision points).

For a simplified verification (EP15-A3), fewer replicates are required when verifying a manufacturer's claim rather than establishing precision from scratch.

Precision Types

Repeatability (Sw) — variation within a single run under identical measurement conditions (same operator, same reagent lot, same instrument).

Between-run SD (Sb) — variation between analytical runs due to run-to-run effects (e.g. calibration, reagent batches, time).

Intermediate precision (St) — total within-laboratory variation combining repeatability and between-run sources; this reflects routine clinical performance and is the most clinically relevant figure for method validation.

Select Repeatability when all measurements come from a single run; select Intermediate Precision when measurements are grouped by day or run.

Formulas

Repeatability (single run):

Sw = √[ Σ(xᵢ − x̄)² / (n − 1) ]
CV% = (Sw / x̄) × 100

Intermediate precision (ANOVA decomposition — multiple runs):

MSw = SSw / dfW     (within-run mean square)
MSb = SSb / (k − 1)   (between-run mean square)
n₀ = effective group size (harmonic mean)

Sw = √MSw          (within-run SD = repeatability)
Sb = √max(0, (MSb − MSw) / n₀)   (between-run SD)
St = √(Sw² + Sb²)   (intermediate precision SD)

CV% = (St / x̄) × 100

Sb is set to zero if the variance component estimate is negative (a known artefact of ANOVA at small sample sizes). St is the key figure for method validation — it reflects all within-laboratory sources of variation.

95% CI for SD (chi-square based):

Lower = SD × √(df / χ²₀.₉₇₅)
Upper = SD × √(df / χ²₀.₀₂₅)

The chi-square CI is asymmetric: it extends further above the point estimate than below, reflecting that the distribution of sample variances is right-skewed.

Fixed Control Limits

Fixed limits let you evaluate new measurements against a pre-established target mean and SD, rather than deriving them from the current dataset. This is the standard approach for ongoing internal QC monitoring.

  • Target mean — the assigned expected value for your control material, derived from historical data or the manufacturer's value assignment.
  • Target SD (σ) — the historical within-laboratory SD used to define control chart limits (±1σ, ±2σ, ±3σ lines on the Levey-Jennings chart).

When fixed limits are set, the calculated mean and SD of the current dataset are still shown for comparison, but all Westgard rule checks and chart lines use the fixed σ.

Fixed limits should only be established after at least 20 measurements across ≥ 5 days. Using limits derived from fewer measurements can produce unduly tight or loose control boundaries.

Westgard Rules

Westgard rules are sequential decision criteria applied to control measurements to distinguish random error from systematic error or instrument malfunction.

RuleTypeDefinitionDetects
1₂s Warning 1 measurement exceeds mean ± 2SD Random error warning — do not reject on this rule alone; use as a trigger to inspect subsequent rules.
1₃s Rejection 1 measurement exceeds mean ± 3SD Large random error or systematic shift. Run should be rejected and investigated.
2₂s Rejection 2 consecutive measurements both exceed mean + 2SD or both below mean − 2SD Systematic error — both controls shifted in the same direction, suggesting calibration drift or reagent issue.
R₄s Rejection Consecutive pair spans > 4SD: one exceeds +2SD while the next exceeds −2SD Large random error — extreme scatter within a consecutive pair indicating instrument instability.
4₁s Rejection 4 consecutive measurements all exceed mean + 1SD or all below mean − 1SD Systematic bias — gradual drift of control values away from the mean.
10x Rejection 10 consecutive measurements all on the same side of the mean Sustained systematic bias — strong evidence of a shift in the measurement process (new reagent lot, recalibration needed).

A warning (1₂s) triggers inspection but does not require run rejection. Rejection rules indicate the run should be repeated and the cause investigated before releasing patient results. Document all violations per ISO 15189 requirements. In a precision study context, use violations to identify potential outliers — do not exclude observations without documented justification.

Excluding Observations

Use the checkboxes in the Observations table to exclude individual measurements (repeatability) or entire groups (intermediate precision) from the statistical calculation. All statistics, Westgard evaluation, and the chart update automatically. Excluded points remain visible on the chart as grey hollow circles for transparency.

Exclusions should always be clinically or analytically justified and documented. Common valid reasons: verified instrument malfunction, known sample handling error, transcription mistake, or power interruption during a run.

Outputs

  • Sw — within-run SD. Variation within a single run under identical measurement conditions.
  • Sb — between-run SD. Variation between analytical runs due to run-to-run effects (e.g. calibration, reagent batches, time).
  • St — total intermediate precision SD = √(Sw² + Sb²). Total within-laboratory variation combining repeatability and between-run sources; this reflects routine clinical performance. Compare against your allowable imprecision goal.
  • CV% — (SD / mean) × 100. Normalises precision relative to concentration.
  • 95% CI for SD — chi-square based interval. Wider intervals reflect less data.
  • PASS / FAIL — total CV% versus your allowable imprecision. PASS means the method meets the quality goal at this concentration level.

Allowable Imprecision

Define your acceptance criteria before running the evaluation. Sources:

  • Biological variation — desirable CV% ≤ 0.5 × CVI (intra-individual variation). E.g. HbA1c: CVI ≈ 1.9% → allowable CV% ≤ 0.95%
  • State-of-the-art — published peer performance from EQAS / RIQAS programs
  • Manufacturer's claim — stated precision specification in the IFU / package insert
  • Clinical decision threshold — defined so analytical variation does not influence clinical decision-making
  • Regulatory / accreditation — CLIA, ISO 15189, or national requirements

Run separate precision evaluations at each medical decision point. A method may pass at normal concentrations but fail at critical low or high values.

Assumptions

  • Measurements are made under controlled, documented conditions
  • All replicates measure the same stable control material
  • Values are approximately normally distributed within each run
  • For intermediate precision: groups (runs/days) are independent
  • No pre-analytical variation (same preparation, storage, and handling)

Reporting Example

Precision was evaluated using a 5-day × 2-run × 2-replicate design (n = 20) at two concentration levels. At the low medical decision point (3.5 mmol/L), repeatability CV% was 1.1% (95% CI: 0.8–1.7%) and intermediate precision CV% was 1.8% (95% CI: 1.4–2.7%), both within the allowable imprecision of 2.5% (desirable biological variation criterion). No Westgard rejection rules were triggered.
