Purpose

The one-sample t-test evaluates whether the mean of a single sample differs from a hypothesized population mean μ0.

When to Use

  • Checking whether a measurement process meets a target value (quality control)
  • Comparing a sample mean to a known benchmark or reference value
  • Testing whether a mean change score differs from zero

Key Idea

Compute the sample mean x̄ and ask: how many standard errors is it away from μ0? If that distance is large relative to chance variation, the test rejects H₀.

Hypotheses

Two-sided: H₀: μ = μ0  |  H₁: μ ≠ μ0

One-sided (greater): H₀: μ ≤ μ0  |  H₁: μ > μ0

One-sided (less): H₀: μ ≥ μ0  |  H₁: μ < μ0

Test Statistic

t = (x̄ − μ0) / SE   where   SE = s / √n

  • x̄ = sample mean
  • s = sample standard deviation
  • n = sample size

Degrees of Freedom

df = n − 1

Confidence Intervals

95% CI of the mean:

x̄ ± tn−1, α/2 × SE   where   SE = s / √n

Answers: "Where does the true population mean likely lie?" Narrows as n increases. This is the interval displayed in the hypothesis visualisation and tested by the one-sample t-test.

95% Reference range (shown for illustration only — not part of the test):

x̄ ± tn−1, α/2 × s

Answers: "Where do 95% of individual data points fall?" Does not narrow with larger n — individual values always scatter around the mean. Shown in the hypothesis visualisation only to contrast with the CI above.

Effect Size

Cohen's d (standardized mean difference):

d = (x̄ − μ0) / s

  • 0.2 = small  |  0.5 = medium  |  0.8 = large

Assumptions

  • Observations are independent
  • Outcome is continuous (or approximately interval-scale)
  • Population distribution is approximately normal (especially important for small n)
  • No extreme outliers that dominate the mean

Normality Check

The Shapiro–Wilk test checks whether the sample plausibly comes from a normal distribution. A Q–Q plot visually compares observed quantiles to theoretical normal quantiles.

With very small n, normality tests have limited power. With large n, they can detect tiny deviations. Combine the test result, the Q–Q plot, and domain knowledge.

If Normality Is Violated

  • Consider the Wilcoxon signed-rank test as a non-parametric alternative
  • Inspect for outliers and data-entry errors
  • Consider transformations (log, square root) if meaningful for your data

Decision Rule

Reject H₀ if p < α.

Clinical Interpretation

A statistically significant result means the sample mean is unlikely under H₀. Statistical significance does not imply clinical relevance — consider the magnitude of difference (effect size) and the confidence interval alongside the p-value.

Reporting Example

A one-sample t-test showed that the sample mean differed significantly from μ₀ = X.X, t(df) = X.XX, p = 0.0X, d = X.XX, 95% CI [L, U].
