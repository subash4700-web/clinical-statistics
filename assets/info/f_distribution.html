F Distribution Explorer

Purpose

The F distribution explorer visualizes the F distribution and allows interactive exploration of critical values, tail probabilities, and variance ratios. It supports comparison of two F distributions with different degrees of freedom.

Key Learning Objectives

  • Understand how the F distribution depends on two degrees of freedom (df₁, df₂)
  • Interpret right-tailed critical regions used in hypothesis testing
  • Connect F statistics to p-values
  • Understand the role of F in ANOVA and variance testing
  • Recognize how increasing degrees of freedom affects distribution shape

What Is the F Distribution?

The F distribution arises from the ratio of two independent chi-square variables divided by their respective degrees of freedom.

If:

F = (χ²₁ / df₁) / (χ²₂ / df₂)

then F follows an F distribution with:

  • df₁ = numerator degrees of freedom
  • df₂ = denominator degrees of freedom

Key Properties

  • F ≥ 0 (never negative)
  • Right-skewed, especially for small df
  • Becomes less skewed as degrees of freedom increase
  • Shape depends on both df₁ and df₂

Connection to Variance Testing

The F statistic is commonly used to compare two sample variances:

F = s₁² / s₂²

Under the null hypothesis of equal variances, this ratio follows an F distribution with:

  • df₁ = n₁ − 1
  • df₂ = n₂ − 1

Connection to ANOVA

In ANOVA, the F statistic compares variability between groups to variability within groups:

F = MSbetween / MSwithin

A large F value suggests that group means are not all equal.

Critical Values and Tail Areas

In most applications, the rejection region lies in the right tail.

Right-tail critical value:

P(F ≥ Fcrit) = α

Left-tail probabilities may be interpreted directly or by taking reciprocals of F values in variance comparisons.

Decision Rule

Reject H₀ if:

F ≥ Fcrit

or equivalently:

p < α

Clinical Relevance

F distributions are central to:

  • ANOVA for comparing multiple clinical groups
  • Testing equality of analytical variances
  • Model evaluation in regression analysis

Understanding how degrees of freedom influence the distribution improves interpretation of laboratory and clinical research results.
