Understanding Categorical Method Comparison

What is categorical method comparison?

When two methods produce qualitative (pos/neg, reactive/non-reactive) results rather than numbers, you cannot use Bland-Altman or regression. Instead, you build a 2×2 contingency table of paired results and use Cohen's Kappa to measure agreement and McNemar's test to check for systematic directional bias.

The 2×2 table

Rows = Method A result. Columns = Method B result.

  • a — both positive (concordant positive)
  • b — only Method A positive (A over-calls, or B misses)
  • c — only Method B positive (B over-calls, or A misses)
  • d — both negative (concordant negative)

a + d are the concordant cells (agreement). b + c are discordant (disagreement) — these drive both Kappa and McNemar.

Cohen's Kappa (κ)

Kappa corrects observed agreement (Po) for the agreement expected by chance alone (Pe):

κ = (Po − Pe) / (1 − Pe)

Landis & Koch scale: <0 poor · 0–0.20 slight · 0.20–0.40 fair · 0.40–0.60 moderate · 0.60–0.80 substantial · 0.80–1.0 almost perfect.

McNemar's test

Tests whether b ≠ c — i.e., whether one method calls significantly more positives than the other. A significant result means there is a systematic directional bias (not just random disagreement). Uses exact binomial when b+c < 25, χ² with continuity correction otherwise.

The Kappa paradox

High observed agreement does not mean high Kappa. When prevalence is very low or very high, chance agreement (Pe) is also high — so Kappa can be surprisingly low even with 95%+ observed agreement. This is the Kappa paradox. Always report both Po and κ.
