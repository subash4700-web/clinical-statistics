Understanding Method Comparison

What is method comparison?

Method comparison evaluates whether two measurement methods produce results that are interchangeable in clinical practice. You run the same patient samples through both methods and examine the pattern of differences — not just whether they correlate, but how much they differ and whether that difference is clinically acceptable.

Why correlation (r) is not enough

Two methods can have r = 0.99 and still produce clinically unacceptable differences. Pearson r measures the strength of the linear relationship, not agreement. If both methods consistently differ by 20% across all concentrations, r is still 1.0 — but patients would be harmed by the difference. Always use agreement-focused methods.

Key methods

  • Bland-Altman plot — plots the difference (Method A − Method B) against the mean of both. Shows the average bias and the 95% limits of agreement (LoA). The LoA define the range within which 95% of differences will fall. Compare LoA against your clinically acceptable difference.
  • Passing-Bablok regression — non-parametric regression that separates constant bias (intercept ≠ 0) from proportional bias (slope ≠ 1). Preferred when both methods have measurement error.
  • Deming regression — like Passing-Bablok but parametric. Requires knowing the ratio of the error variances between the two methods.

What to check

  • Is the mean bias clinically acceptable?
  • Do the limits of agreement fall within your predefined acceptable difference?
  • Is there proportional bias (differences grow with concentration)?
  • Are there outliers suggesting specific sample matrix problems?

Common mistake

Using correlation to accept a new method. r > 0.95 is often quoted as "good agreement" in lab reports — but this is statistically incorrect. Use Bland-Altman and set your acceptable difference criterion before collecting data, not after seeing the results.
