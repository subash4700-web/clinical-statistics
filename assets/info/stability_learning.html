Understanding Stability Studies

What is a stability study?

A stability study (holdbarhedsstudie) evaluates how long a stored sample, reagent, or calibrator maintains acceptable analytical performance. You test the same material at multiple time points and compare results against the fresh baseline (T0). The goal is to establish a functional expiry — the last time point at which performance is still acceptable.

Stability studies are required under CLSI EP25-A and are commonly needed for: patient sample stability for delayed testing, frozen panel storage, kit reagent stability after opening, and transport validation.

Quantitative stability (continuous data)

For measurable analytes, calculate the % change from T0 at each time point. Compare against a predefined acceptance criterion (often derived from biological variation or clinical decision limits). A paired t-test can confirm whether the change is statistically significant, but clinical acceptability (the % change criterion) is the primary decision:

% change = ((Tn − T0) / T0) × 100

Qualitative stability (pos/neg data)

For qualitative assays, compare results at each time point against T0 using a 2×2 contingency table. Calculate Cohen's Kappa (overall agreement corrected for chance) and McNemar's test (directional bias — are positives being lost or gained?). Functional expiry = last time point where κ ≥ acceptance criterion AND McNemar is non-significant.

Common mistakes

Only testing one time point. A single time point tells you whether the material passes or fails at that point — not when it starts to fail. You need multiple time points to establish where the functional expiry actually lies.

Confusing statistical significance with clinical significance. A statistically significant % change (p < 0.05) does not necessarily mean the change is clinically important. Always compare against the predefined acceptance criterion, not just the p-value.
