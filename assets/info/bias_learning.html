Understanding Bias

What is bias?

Bias is systematic error — the consistent tendency of a method to measure higher or lower than the true value. Unlike precision (random scatter), bias shifts every result in the same direction. A biased method is inaccurate even if it is very reproducible.

Two types of bias

  • Constant bias — a fixed offset at all concentrations. Example: every haemoglobin result is 0.5 g/dL too high regardless of concentration. Seen when there is matrix interference.
  • Proportional bias — the error grows with concentration. Example: results are 5% too high across the range. Seen with calibration slope differences.

In practice, bias is rarely purely one type — Passing-Bablok regression in method comparison separates both components simultaneously.

How bias is measured

Compare your method against a reference:

  • Certified Reference Material (CRM) — a sample with a known, traceable true value. Gives the most direct estimate of absolute bias.
  • External Quality Assurance (EQA) — peer group comparison. Tells you how your method compares to others, not necessarily to the true value.
  • Method comparison — paired patient samples on two methods. Estimates the difference between methods, not against truth.

Bias% = ((your result − reference) / reference) × 100

Why it matters

Allowable bias is set by biological variation (EFLM/Ricos). If the allowable bias for Glucose is 2.2% and your method has a bias of 4%, some patients near the diagnostic threshold of 7.0 mmol/L will be misclassified. Bias directly affects diagnostic accuracy.

Common mistake

Trusting EQA peer group comparison as a measure of true bias. If everyone in your peer group uses the same biased method, you will appear to be in excellent agreement — but the whole group may be wrong. CRM-based validation is the only way to establish traceability to the true value.
