Purpose

Fisher's Exact Test evaluates whether two categorical variables are associated in a 2×2 contingency table. It is especially useful when sample sizes are small or expected cell counts are low (< 5), where the chi-square approximation may be unreliable.

When to Use

  • Small sample sizes or rare outcomes
  • Sparse 2×2 tables where any expected cell count < 5
  • Clinical microbiology: positivity rates, resistance vs. exposure, case-control comparisons
  • When exact probabilities are required rather than chi-square approximation

Key Idea

Fisher's test conditions on the fixed row and column totals (marginals) and computes the exact probability of observing a table as extreme as, or more extreme than, the observed one under the null hypothesis of no association. Unlike the chi-square test, it makes no large-sample approximation.

2×2 Table

Exposedaba + b
Unexposedcdc + d
Column totala + cb + dN

The probability of observing this exact table, given fixed marginals, follows a hypergeometric distribution.

Hypotheses

H₀: No association between the two variables (rows and columns are independent).

H₁ (two-sided): There is an association.

H₁ (one-sided): The association is in a specific direction (greater or less).

Two-sided p-values are computed as the sum of probabilities of all tables with probability ≤ the observed table. This is the most common definition used in clinical practice.

Test Statistic

The exact probability of a specific 2×2 table given fixed marginals:

P = (a+b)!(c+d)!(a+c)!(b+d)! / (N! · a! · b! · c! · d!)

The p-value is the sum of probabilities of all tables at least as extreme as the observed.

Effect Size

Odds Ratio (OR):

OR = (a × d) / (b × c)

  • OR = 1: no association
  • OR > 1: exposure associated with increased odds of outcome
  • OR < 1: exposure associated with decreased odds of outcome

Always report the OR and its 95% confidence interval alongside the p-value. For very small counts, confidence intervals may be wide even if p is small.

Assumptions

  • Observations are independent
  • Both variables are binary (2×2 table)
  • Fixed marginal totals (the test conditions on these)

Decision Rule

Reject H₀ if p < α.

Visualisation Notes

The contingency table heatmap shows each cell's count, expected value (under independence), and colour-coded deviation intensity. Orange/red cells indicate the strongest deviations from independence — these are the cells driving the association. Blue cells are close to their expected values under H₀. This makes it easy to identify which exposure–outcome combination is most discrepant.

Clinical Interpretation

A significant result provides evidence of an association between the two variables. Report the odds ratio and confidence interval alongside the p-value. Interpret results in the context of study design, selection bias, and potential confounding. Statistical significance does not imply causation.

Reporting Example

Fisher's exact test showed a significant association between exposure and outcome, p = 0.0X (two-sided). OR = X.XX (95% CI: X.XX–X.XX).

Test Settings

  • Significance level (α): Choose 0.10, 0.05, or 0.01
  • Alternative hypothesis: Two-sided, greater, or less
