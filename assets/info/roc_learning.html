Understanding ROC & Diagnostic Performance

What is a ROC curve?

A Receiver Operating Characteristic (ROC) curve shows how a continuous test's diagnostic performance changes as you move the decision threshold. At each threshold, you get a specific sensitivity (true positive rate) and specificity (true negative rate). The ROC curve plots sensitivity (y-axis) vs (1 − specificity) (x-axis) for all possible thresholds.

Key concepts

  • Sensitivity — proportion of true positives correctly identified. High sensitivity minimises false negatives. Use when missing a disease is dangerous (e.g. screening for sepsis).
  • Specificity — proportion of true negatives correctly identified. High specificity minimises false positives. Use when a false positive causes significant harm (e.g. unnecessary surgery).
  • AUC (Area Under the Curve) — a single number summarising overall discriminatory ability. AUC = 0.5 is no better than chance; AUC = 1.0 is perfect.

AUC interpretation

  • 0.90–1.00 — Excellent
  • 0.80–0.90 — Good
  • 0.70–0.80 — Fair
  • 0.60–0.70 — Poor
  • 0.50–0.60 — Fail (no better than chance)

Choosing the threshold

The optimal threshold depends on the clinical question. The Youden index (sensitivity + specificity − 1) maximises the sum of both. But clinical context often overrides statistics — if missing a disease is dangerous, you accept lower specificity to maximise sensitivity.

Positive and negative predictive value (PPV/NPV)

PPV and NPV depend on disease prevalence in your population, not just sensitivity and specificity. A test with 95% sensitivity has a much lower PPV in a low-prevalence screening population than in a high-prevalence referral population. AUC and the ROC curve are prevalence-independent.

Common mistake

Choosing the threshold after seeing the data. If you test many thresholds and pick the best-performing one retrospectively, you will overfit to your sample. Pre-specify the threshold or validate it in an independent dataset.
