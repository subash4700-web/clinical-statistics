Purpose

Weighted kappa extends Cohen's kappa for ordinal categories, allowing partial credit for near-agreement and penalising larger disagreements more than smaller ones.

When to Use

  • Ordinal rating scales (e.g., mild / moderate / severe)
  • Clinical grading or staging systems
  • When the degree of disagreement matters — a one-step error is less serious than a three-step error
  • Anywhere standard κ would be used but the outcome is ordinal, not purely nominal

Key Idea

Disagreements are penalised according to how far apart the ratings are. Minor disagreements (adjacent categories) receive small penalties; major disagreements (distant categories) receive large penalties. This produces a κw that reflects the severity-weighted reliability of the raters.

Agreement Matrix

Data are arranged in a square ordinal matrix where rows = Rater 1 and columns = Rater 2. Diagonal cells = perfect agreement (weight = 1.0). Off-diagonal cells = disagreement, penalised according to their distance from the diagonal.

Weighting Schemes

Linear weights:

wij = 1 − |i − j| / (k − 1)

Treats all disagreements proportionally to their distance. Appropriate when each step difference is equally important.

Quadratic weights (most common):

wij = 1 − (i − j)² / (k − 1)²

Heavily penalises large disagreements. Equivalent to the intraclass correlation coefficient (ICC) for ordinal scales. Preferred in most clinical contexts.

Choose the weighting scheme that reflects your clinical judgment about the relative severity of different degrees of disagreement.

Formula

κw = 1 − (Weighted observed disagreement / Weighted expected disagreement)

Equivalently:

κw = (Po,w − Pe,w) / (1 − Pe,w)

where Po,w and Pe,w are the weighted observed and expected agreement proportions.

Confidence Interval

The 95% CI for κw is computed using the asymptotic standard error for weighted kappa.

95% CI: κw ± 1.96 × SE(κw)

Confidence intervals are asymptotic approximations — interpret cautiously for small N.

Interpretation Scale

  • κw < 0.20 → Poor agreement
  • 0.21–0.40 → Fair agreement
  • 0.41–0.60 → Moderate agreement
  • 0.61–0.80 → Substantial agreement
  • 0.81–1.00 → Almost perfect agreement

Weighted κ values are generally higher than unweighted κ because near-agreements receive partial credit.

Hypothesis Test

H₀: κw = 0 (weighted agreement is no better than chance).

H₁: κw > 0.

The test statistic is z = κw / SE(κw).

Assumptions

  • Exactly two raters
  • Ordinal outcome — categories have a meaningful order
  • Same ordinal categories used by both raters
  • Independent ratings
  • All subjects rated by both raters

Limitations

  • Result depends on the chosen weighting scheme — report which scheme was used
  • Quadratic weighted κ equals ICC only under specific conditions
  • Like all κ statistics, affected by prevalence and marginal distributions
  • Only applicable to exactly two raters

Decision Rule

Reject H₀ (κw = 0) if p < α. Use the κw value and CI for practical interpretation.

Visualisation Notes

The weighted agreement heatmap shows observed counts with colour-coded weight severity:

  • Diagonal cells (blue, weight = 1.0): perfect agreement — both raters assigned the same category
  • Off-diagonal cells: disagreements — colour reflects both the frequency AND the weight penalty. Cells far from the diagonal (large disagreements) appear darker red regardless of their count, because they contribute most to κw reduction.

The weight penalty for each off-diagonal cell is displayed as text within the cell. This allows you to see not just where disagreements occur, but how severely each type of disagreement penalises the overall agreement score.

Clinical Interpretation

Weighted kappa is particularly important in clinical settings where ordered categories represent severity or staging. A rater who classifies "severe" as "mild" is making a more serious error than one who classifies "severe" as "moderate". Report κw, 95% CI, the weighting scheme used, and the number of subjects.

Reporting Example

Weighted agreement between raters was substantial (κw = 0.81, 95% CI: 0.72–0.90, p < 0.001, quadratic weights).
