Enzyme Activity BETA

What is a Unit?

  • 1 U = 1 µmol substrate converted per minute under defined conditions (temperature, pH, substrate concentration).
  • SI alternative: 1 katal = 1 mol/s. Rarely used in clinical labs. 1 U = 16.67 nkat.

The Assay

  • Spectrophotometric: measure ΔAbs/min = reaction rate.
  • Common chromophores: NADH (340 nm, ε = 6220), pNP (405 nm, ε = 18 300).
  • Continuous assays preferred — you see the rate in real time and can confirm linearity.

The Formula

Activity (U/mL) = (ΔAbs/min × Vtotal) / (ε × l × Vsample)

  • ΔAbs/min = change in absorbance per minute (from the linear portion).
  • Vtotal = total assay volume (mL). Vsample = sample volume added (mL).
  • ε = molar extinction coefficient (L·mol−1·cm−1). l = path length (cm, usually 1).

Specific Activity

  • U/mg protein. Tracks purification progress — should increase with each step.
  • Purification fold = specific activity at step N / specific activity of crude extract.

Km (Michaelis Constant)

  • Substrate concentration at ½ Vmax. Low Km = high affinity.
  • Clinical example: glucokinase (Km ≈ 10 mM, liver glucose sensor) vs hexokinase (Km ≈ 0.1 mM, always active).

Vmax

  • Maximum rate when all enzyme is saturated with substrate. Proportional to [enzyme].
  • Doubling enzyme concentration doubles Vmax but does not change Km.

Lineweaver-Burk Plot

  • 1/V vs 1/[S]. Slope = Km/Vmax. Y-intercept = 1/Vmax. X-intercept = −1/Km.
  • Good for visualizing inhibition patterns. Lines converge on y-axis (competitive), x-axis (non-competitive).
  • Limitation: amplifies errors at low [S]. Prefer non-linear regression for accurate fitting.

Inhibition Types

  • Competitive — inhibitor binds active site. ↑ apparent Km, Vmax unchanged. Overcome by excess substrate.
  • Non-competitive — binds allosteric site. Km unchanged, ↓ Vmax. Cannot be overcome.
  • Uncompetitive — binds ES complex only. Both Km and Vmax decrease.

Clinical Enzymes

  • CK-MB — myocardial infarction (now largely replaced by troponin).
  • ALT / AST — liver damage. ALT more liver-specific; AST also in heart and muscle.
  • Amylase / Lipase — acute pancreatitis. Lipase more specific and stays elevated longer.
  • ALP — bone and liver. Isoenzymes or GGT help distinguish source.
  • LDH — non-specific tissue damage. Isoenzymes (LD1–LD5) for source identification.
