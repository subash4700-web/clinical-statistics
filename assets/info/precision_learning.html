Understanding Precision

What is precision?

Precision describes how reproducible a measurement is — how close repeated measurements of the same sample are to each other. It says nothing about whether the result is correct (that is trueness). Precision is about consistency, not accuracy.

Imagine weighing the same sample five times. If you get 10.1, 10.2, 10.0, 10.1, 10.2 — the method is precise. If you get 9.8, 10.5, 9.3, 10.9, 10.1 — it is imprecise. Neither tells you whether 10.0 is the true weight.

Three levels of precision

  • Repeatability (within-run, Sw) — same sample, same operator, same run. The tightest precision you will ever see.
  • Intermediate precision (between-day, Si) — same sample, different days. Includes day-to-day variation in calibration, reagent lots, operators. This is the most clinically relevant number.
  • Reproducibility — across different laboratories or sites. Used in external quality assurance (EQA) schemes.

How it is expressed

Precision is reported as standard deviation (SD) or coefficient of variation (CV%):

CV% = (SD / mean) × 100

CV% is preferred because it is independent of the concentration level, making it easier to compare across methods and analytes.

Why it matters in the lab

Imprecision adds noise to every patient result. If CV% is 10% and the glucose decision limit is 7.0 mmol/L, a true value of 7.0 could appear anywhere from ~6.3 to ~7.7. This uncertainty directly affects clinical decisions. Allowable imprecision is set by biological variation: the goal is that analytical imprecision does not meaningfully add to the natural variation already seen between patients.

Common mistake

Confusing precision with accuracy. A method can be highly precise (very reproducible) but consistently wrong — all results are tightly grouped around the wrong value. Precision and bias must always be evaluated together.
